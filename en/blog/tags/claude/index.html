Anthropic Legal Turned Contract Review Drudgery Into a Workflow Art With Claude

Dec 9, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Yesterday Anthropic published a new article explaining how their legal team uses Claude / Claude Code to compress work that used to take days into a few hours—while keeping humans in the loop the whole time.

1. Even at a top AI company, legal is still squeezed by grunt work

Section titled “1. Even at a top AI company, legal is still squeezed by grunt work”

Anthropic product counsel Mark described his earlier routine:

  • Replying to compliance documents and customer questionnaires
  • Editing terms of service and privacy policies
  • Reviewing blogs, emails, and marketing assets for the marketing team
  • Redlining contracts and comparing versions line by line
  • Writing a stack of privacy impact assessments (PIA)

These tasks share a few traits:

Repetitive, similar formats, high cost for mistakes, and extremely brain-draining.

He even said many things get pushed to the end of the day because they take so much time yet barely use “the best part of his brain.”

Sound familiar? If you have ever touched legal, compliance, risk, operations, or customer support, you have felt the same pain.

The Anthropic legal team did something very engineer-like:

They held a meeting and wrote on a whiteboard— “What drains the most energy? What repeats the most? What tasks do not deserve this much of our time?”

Once they had answers, they did not rush to “what AI can do.” They focused on a single question:

Which tasks do we never want to do by hand again?

2. How to break down legal workflows: 4 real scenarios

Section titled “2. How to break down legal workflows: 4 real scenarios”

Over a few months, Anthropic’s legal team turned their pain points into repeatable workflows.

1) Self-serve marketing review: AI is the first gate

Section titled “1) Self-serve marketing review: AI is the first gate”

You have surely seen the previous flow:

On the night before launch, marketing would dump landing pages, push copy, and posters on legal: “Please review today; we go live tomorrow.”

Legal had to open the docs -> read end to end -> leave a pile of comments -> go back and forth for multiple rounds. It often dragged on for days.

Anthropic’s rebuild is simple but ruthless:

They built a Self-Review Tool in Slack:

  • Marketing pastes the copy into it

  • Claude calls a dedicated legal Skill file

    • It contains: past review comments, common risk points, and the team’s judgment criteria

  • Claude automatically flags:

    • Exaggerated claims?
    • Is there a source for the data?
    • Any violation of portrait rights or reputation?
    • Any implication of promises that should not be made?

  • Assigns a risk level to each issue: Low / Medium / High

  • Also provides a suggested revision

Only after passing this gate will a ticket be created and enter the legal queue.

StartupHub reported that with this flow, reviews that took days now compress to a few hours, and Slack can auto-generate a summary to help legal see the risk points clearly.

The essence:

Let AI handle the “intake form” and “basic checkup.” The true diagnosis still belongs to the doctor.

2) Side gig / external activity approval: AI asks for details first

Section titled “2) Side gig / external activity approval: AI asks for details first”

Many companies have similar processes:

If an employee wants to moonlight as an adviser, pursue a side business, or serve on a nonprofit board, they must go through “external business activity approval.”

Previously, legal would ask one-on-one about background, conflicts of interest, time commitments, and non-compete risks—going back and forth endlessly.

Here is how Anthropic runs it now:

  • The employee fills out a structured form
  • Claude reads the form
  • If information is missing, it will automatically ask follow-up questions
  • Based on company rules, it provides a recommended decision + risk explanation
  • It then enters the legal queue for an attorney to confirm approval or rejection

Lawyers now mainly focus on two buckets:

  • True edge cases
  • Higher-risk borderline situations

They separated “90% standard flow + 10% extreme judgment.”

3) Privacy impact assessments (PIA): teach AI to write the template

Section titled “3) Privacy impact assessments (PIA): teach AI to write the template”

For privacy projects, every new feature needs a fresh PIA.

The pain:

  • Templates look similar, but you still have to rewrite them each time
  • It must fit the specific scenario without missing required sections

Anthropic’s approach has two steps:

  1. Use MCP (Model Context Protocol) to connect Claude to a Google Drive folder

    • Inside are all historical PIA documents

  2. Equip Claude with a “PIA writing Skill” that spells out:

    • Document structure
    • What to focus on in each section
    • Privacy points the company cares about most

An attorney just says:

“Using the previous cases, draft a PIA for this new feature.”

Claude will:

  • First learn the style of past PIAs
  • Then draft in the same format
  • Transfer historical focus points into the new scenario

The work shifts from “write from scratch” to “edit an 80-point draft.”

4) Legal built a “Morse code lamp,” but the point is that the barrier dropped to human language

Section titled “4) Legal built a “Morse code lamp,” but the point is that the barrier dropped to human language”

There is a fun detail:

Mark used Claude Code to build a “legal desk lamp” that blinks Morse code to send and receive messages—purely a personal hobby project.

But the story highlights something important:

Even a lawyer who cannot code can, by “speaking human language” to AI, build a complete mini app.

For companies, this means:

  • Legal, compliance, and operations—the people who do not code—can build workflows themselves
  • No need to first line up engineering resources or launch a big project
  • As long as they explain the rules clearly, AI can help turn them into tools

It fundamentally changes the belief that “only engineers can build tools.”

3. The building blocks under this legal workflow

Section titled “3. The building blocks under this legal workflow”

Anthropic’s legal system rests on three building blocks:

1) Swap “what tech can do” for “what I refuse to do”

Section titled “1) Swap “what tech can do” for “what I refuse to do””

Mark’s advice is blunt:

Do not start with “what AI can do.” Start with “what work do we most want to escape.”

They followed that approach on a whiteboard:

  • Which documents are repetitive
  • What judgment calls we make every day
  • Which flows can be fully standardized

After confirming, they checked:

  • Can AI do the first pass
  • Reserve human time for disputes, exceptions, and strategic judgment

2) Skills: package team experience into callable assets

Section titled “2) Skills: package team experience into callable assets”

The official blog defines Skills as a document with instructions, scripts, and references that let Claude act like an expert for a task.

For legal, Skills do two things:

  • Keep rules consistent

    • Marketing review Skill: bakes in historical review comments
    • Different domains get different Skills: employment, commercial, privacy, corporate governance…

  • Keep the voice consistent

    • Mark had Claude read 10 of his memos
    • He taught it his structure and tone
    • When Claude drafts memos later, it can “write in his style”

Think of a Skill as:

A senior lawyer’s “plug-in memory card.”

Plug it in when needed; pull it out when you do not.

3) MCP: turn scattered info into a “legal knowledge web”

Section titled “3) MCP: turn scattered info into a “legal knowledge web””

Another key block is MCP (Model Context Protocol).

Anthropic’s legal team connected Claude to:

  • Google Drive: historical contracts, PIAs, templates, policies
  • JIRA: requests and tickets
  • Slack: conversation records
  • Calendar: key meetings and approval checkpoints

This yields two effects:

  • AI stops “hallucinating” because it can check your own materials
  • Each workflow gets just enough context

You can think of it simply:

MCP = connect AI to your “company intranet.”

Skills = teach it “how to use those materials to get work done.”

4. The single most important rule: humans must not be absent

Section titled “4. The single most important rule: humans must not be absent”

Both Anthropic’s blog and the StartupHub article keep repeating: “Legal must stay human-in-the-loop.”

In practice:

  • AI handles:

    • First-pass review and issue spotting
    • Initial risk grading
    • Drafting suggested edits and documents

  • Attorneys handle:

    • Fact-checking and citations
    • Deciding “can the company accept this risk”
    • Final external sign-off

Anthropic is explicit:

AI hallucinates; that is a fact.

So the design is: AI as accelerator, humans as brakes and steering.

For anyone rolling out AI in a company, keep this in mind:

You are not replacing legal with AI. You are using AI so legal stops being a “format fixer and typo hunter.”

5. How teams can “copy the homework”

Section titled “5. How teams can “copy the homework””

After discussing Anthropic, let’s talk about borrowing the idea. Whether you use Claude, ChatGPT, DeepSeek, or your own model, you can follow the same playbook.

Here is how to start:

1) List your team’s “legal hell list”

Section titled “1) List your team’s “legal hell list””

Pick a day and gather legal, compliance, marketing, and product:

  • Write down the tasks you never want to do for the hundredth time
  • Prioritize by “repeatability x risk control”
  • Pick one or two that are ready to pilot now

Typical candidates:

  • First-pass marketing review
  • Standard clause comparison in contracts
  • Data protection and privacy templates
  • Standard questionnaires and compliance Q&A

2) Start with “human + AI dual-layer flow,” then talk automation

Section titled “2) Start with “human + AI dual-layer flow,” then talk automation”

Jumping straight to “end-to-end auto-approval” often dies on risk and trust.

A steadier way:

  • Let AI handle “internal pre-review + draft assistance”

  • Keep the real decision flow unchanged

  • Use one to two months to check:

    • Does it make obvious mistakes?
    • Is speed truly better?
    • Does the team find it more usable?

Once it passes, consider upgrading to:

  • Self-serve tools
  • Workflows wired into internal systems
  • Added templates, Skills, and team norms

3) Feed AI your “rules” and “style” over time

Section titled “3) Feed AI your “rules” and “style” over time”

Follow Anthropic’s two-step method:

  • Rules

    • Compile a “company legal review checklist”
    • For example: banned words, mandatory disclaimers, data points that must be verified
    • Turn it into a long-lived “AI prompt doc” or Skill

  • Style

    • Give AI a few contract reviews or memos your team likes
    • Have it summarize: common structure, phrasing habits, tone preferences
    • Require all future drafts to “follow this style”

Gradually, you will have:

An AI teammate that works the way you do.

4) Do not forget to count the gains

Section titled “4) Do not forget to count the gains”

Is the automation worth it? Anthropic’s “How Anthropic teams use Claude Code” notes that many teams measure efficiency this way, and legal shows especially clear returns.

You can quantify quickly:

  • Average review time per marketing asset
  • Cycle time from “sent to legal” to “legal response” for each contract
  • Hours per week each attorney spends on repetitive documents

Do a before-and-after, and it becomes easy to justify more resources.

6. A quick wrap-up

Section titled “6. A quick wrap-up”

In one line, Anthropic Legal’s practice is:

Use AI as a rules-aware junior assistant to free senior lawyers from grunt work so they can do real judgment.

For anyone who understands the business and a bit of AI, this is a huge opportunity:

  • You can be the person who packages team knowledge into Skills
  • You can build the first “AI + legal workflow”
  • You can turn AI from a “chat toy” into true “process infrastructure”

I am curious:

In your team, what could be handed to AI to handle the groundwork if you follow Anthropic’s approach?

Anthropic Internal AI Development Survey: Frontend Shines, Architecture Is Hardest to Hand Off to AI

Dec 3, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Introduction: Is AI Really Making Engineers More Productive?

Section titled “Introduction: Is AI Really Making Engineers More Productive?”

This year, every developer friend around me keeps asking the same question:

“I use Claude/Copilot every day, but is it really worth it? Are the vendors overselling me?”

Coincidentally, Anthropic turned the real usage data of 132 internal engineers and researchers into a detailed survey and interviews, plus several charts.

It is like an AI vendor giving itself a physical exam. Who is delighted, who is stumbling—everything is written in the numbers.

Let’s look at the report from a working engineer’s perspective and talk about: In software engineering, which tasks have the highest ROI with AI, and which ones should you not rush to offload to a model?

I. What Kind of Internal Survey Is This?

Section titled “I. What Kind of Internal Survey Is This?”

Anthropic asked 132 engineers and researchers a series of very concrete questions:

  • How much of your daily work do you do with Claude?
  • What do you mainly use it for? Debugging, new features, refactoring, tests, documentation, or design and planning?
  • For each task type, does working with Claude make you spend more or less time?
  • Does the volume of output (code, docs, deliverables) go up or down?

They also compared changes over time:

  • A year ago, employees estimated that about 28% of their work used Claude, self-reporting a +20% productivity lift.
  • Now that number has risen to 59% of work done with Claude, with an average self-reported productivity +50%; around 14% of “power users” believe they improved by over 100%.

Another independent study is also striking: using 100,000 real conversations, Anthropic estimated that the median task completion time dropped by roughly 80%.

It sounds dramatic, but engineers know self-reported productivity needs a haircut. The more useful takeaway is: by task type, where are the real gains and where is the fluff?

II. Overall Pattern: Time Saved Is Small, Output Gain Is Big

Section titled “II. Overall Pattern: Time Saved Is Small, Output Gain Is Big”

That figure you saw is one of the core findings:

  • Left: after using Claude, for a given task, time spent goes down, stays the same, or goes up.
  • Right: in the same amount of time, the amount of output goes up by how much.

Looking across all tasks, two patterns stand out:

  1. For most tasks, time is only slightly lower

    • Many points sit around “-1” (a light decrease).
    • It means people are saving a little, not cutting the time in half.

  2. But the output increase is more obvious

    • In the right plot, most points are in the “+1 to +2” range.
    • So even when time only drops a bit, the amount produced jumps a lot.

A hidden detail: In the raw data, a small group actually spends more time on some tasks.

Their interview explanations feel very real:

  • Cleaning up after Claude—fixing the mess it made, redoing debugging and cleanup.
  • Spending time to understand code they did not write themselves.
  • Or because Claude exists, they simply work more thoroughly: more tests, more exploration.

In short:

Time does not vanish; it gets “reinvested” into more work.

III. ROI by Task Type: Frontend Wins, Design and Infra Lose

Section titled “III. ROI by Task Type: Frontend Wins, Design and Infra Lose”

Looking at the distribution and my own experience, the ROI ranking is clear.

1) ROI Ceiling: Frontend, New Features, Debugging, Testing, Documentation

Section titled “1) ROI Ceiling: Frontend, New Features, Debugging, Testing, Documentation”

These tasks share traits:

  • Outcomes are easy to verify

    • Frontend: run it and you immediately see if the UI is right.
    • Debugging: reproduce the bug, run tests—pass or fail.
    • Tests and docs: format and coverage are obvious at a glance.

  • Task boundaries are clear

    • “Make this component responsive.”
    • “Add a set of unit tests for this code.”
    • “Write a README for new teammates.”

In these jobs, the most annoying part for humans is: mechanical, repetitive, boring to write, yet quality is visible instantly.

This matches a keyword Anthropic staff highlighted:

“I’m more willing to hand Claude tasks that are easy to verify.”

IV. Verifiability: The “Watershed” That Decides AI Effectiveness

Section titled “IV. Verifiability: The “Watershed” That Decides AI Effectiveness”

In interviews, Anthropic summarized a pragmatic delegation rule: engineers are more willing to hand Claude tasks that are:

  • Easy to verify
  • Low complexity or clearly bounded
  • Not under extreme quality bars (e.g., one-off debug scripts)
  • Repetitive, dull, but cannot be skipped

This can be abstracted into one word: verifiability.

Think of it this way:

  1. Tasks with tests or demos are great for AI to charge ahead

    • Frontend pages, backend endpoints, data-processing scripts
    • Run them and see whether the result is in the expected range

  2. Tasks relying on “taste” and “judgment” need human oversight

    • System design, UX design, security strategy
    • Often no standard answers, only trade-offs

  3. Local problems vs. global problems

    • Changing one component or writing a doc is a local problem.
    • Planning a cross-team technical roadmap is a global problem.

Claude delivers huge ROI on local, verifiable tasks. On global, fuzzy tasks, it is better for brainstorming, lists, and comparisons, not for directly giving the final plan.

From day one, it has treated AI safety and ethics as a foundational mission, embedding them into the company’s legal structure, technical methodology, and commercial strategy.

Part I: Origins of a Safety‑First Mission

Section titled “Part I: Origins of a Safety‑First Mission”

Born of a split: OpenAI’s philosophical rift

Section titled “Born of a split: OpenAI’s philosophical rift”

Anthropic was founded in 2021 by a group of former OpenAI employees, led by siblings Dario Amodei (former VP of Research at OpenAI) and Daniela Amodei (former VP of Safety and Policy). In late 2020, they left OpenAI with five colleagues due to fundamental disagreements over the company’s direction.

Dario and others were concerned about OpenAI’s 2019 transition from a non‑profit to a “capped‑profit” entity and its deep partnership with Microsoft. They believed this shift moved the mission from “benefiting all of humanity” toward commercialization and profit, relegating AI safety and ethics to a secondary role. As Dario put it, they “could foresee the exponential growth of artificial intelligence,” and AI companies urgently needed “a set of values to constrain these powerful programs.” The founding of Anthropic was a direct counter to what they saw as sacrificing safety for commercial priorities.

Public benefit corporation: writing the mission into the charter

Section titled “Public benefit corporation: writing the mission into the charter”

Anthropic is a Delaware public benefit corporation (PBC). This legal structure requires the company to balance shareholder interests with a public‑benefit purpose. For Anthropic, that purpose is to develop AI systems that are reliable, interpretable, and controllable.

To prevent commercial pressure from eroding its safety mission, Anthropic established the Long‑Term Benefit Trust, which holds a special T‑class of shares and can elect a majority of the board. This governance design aims to legally insulate the safety mission—at least in part—from purely financial interests, addressing the limitations of traditional corporate governance in confronting potential catastrophic risks from AGI.

From the founders’ departures to the company’s structural design, Anthropic’s core narrative is clear: rapid AI progress requires a mission‑driven governance paradigm. Part II: Claude’s Architectural Philosophy

Section titled “Part II: Claude’s Architectural Philosophy”

Anthropic’s uniqueness lies not only in governance but in its technical methodology, centered on directly addressing the alignment problem.

Constitutional AI: encoding values

Section titled “Constitutional AI: encoding values”

Constitutional AI (CAI) aims to train AI to be “helpful, honest, and harmless.” Its core idea is to introduce a human‑written “constitution”—a set of principles that guide the model to self‑critique and revise its outputs during training. Unlike the common approach of deflecting or blurring sensitive topics, CAI enables more direct responses under principled constraints and requires the model to explain refusals when appropriate.

Training occurs in two stages. In supervised fine‑tuning, the model critiques and corrects its own problematic answers using constitutional principles, producing a “less harmful” dataset for further tuning.

Reinforcement learning from AI feedback (RLAIF): an engine for scalable alignment

Section titled “Reinforcement learning from AI feedback (RLAIF): an engine for scalable alignment”

The second stage uses reinforcement learning. Anthropic applies RLAIF: the model generates two candidate answers, then evaluates and selects a preferred one according to a randomly chosen constitutional principle; these judgments train a preference model so the final policy better adheres to the constitution.

Compared with the more common RLHF, Anthropic argues RLAIF is more efficient and scalable (less dependent on human labeling), improves interpretability and transparency by expressing goals in natural language, and somewhat reduces human bias. Critics note the constitution is still human‑written and thus subjective, and that reducing human intervention raises questions about accountability and oversight. The tension between “automation” and “human control” is central to understanding Anthropic’s approach.
