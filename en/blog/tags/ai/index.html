Section titled “Executive Summary”

Competing with n8n, Dify, Coze? Google Opal Generates AI Workflows with One Sentence

Aug 1, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Google strikes again! Opal brings the magical “write one sentence → drag blocks” operation to AI workflows, creating functional mini-apps without spending a dime. Less than a week after launch, it’s already sparking heated discussions that n8n/Zapier might lose sleep over: describe your needs → AI automatically chains together Gemini models, search, and external tools, then gives you a visual flowchart that you can drag and adjust anytime, plus one-click sharing with colleagues.

What Exactly is Opal?

Section titled “What Exactly is Opal?”
  • Google Labs’ brand-new experimental product, featuring a dual mode of “natural language + visualization,” currently 100% free and limited to US account beta testing.
  • Positioned as an AI mini-app builder: automatically arranges input boxes, prompts, Gemini model calls, tool APIs, and other steps into workflow nodes.

You describe: “Turn meeting notes into action items.” Opal instantly generates four nodes: “Upload notes → Gemini summary → Generate todos → Output email,” making the workflow crystal clear.

Five Major Highlights

Section titled “Five Major Highlights”

1. Pure Conversational Workflow Generation

Section titled “1. Pure Conversational Workflow Generation”

Natural language alone can connect multiple models and tools, eliminating the need for code and YAML.

2. Drag-and-Drop Visual Editing

Section titled “2. Drag-and-Drop Visual Editing”

Want to insert new steps or modify prompts? Simply drag blocks or type another instruction.

3. Templates & Remix

Section titled “3. Templates & Remix”

Official Starter Gallery comes built-in, with templates for blog writers, thumbnail generators, data scrapers, and more—ready to grab and modify.

4. One-Click Publish & Share

Section titled “4. One-Click Publish & Share”

After generating a link, colleagues can experience it using their own Google accounts, facilitating collaborative testing.

5. Completely Free + No Deployment Required

Section titled “5. Completely Free + No Deployment Required”

Currently runs on Google’s backend, no need to maintain your own servers, perfect for idea validation and internal team demos.

Use Case Examples

Section titled “Use Case Examples”
ScenarioOne-Sentence DescriptionOpal Auto-Generated Nodes
Content Creation”Write an 800-word SEO-friendly blog post”Keywords→Gemini Writing→SEO Review→Publish
Sales Follow-up”Sync new CRM leads to Slack and draft follow-up emails”Webhook→Gemini→Slack API→Gmail Draft
Product Operations”Analyze user feedback and generate weekly reports”CSV Upload→Gemini Sentiment Analysis→Charts→Google Docs

Average of 10 minutes to get a minimum viable version running.

How is it Different from n8n/Zapier?

Section titled “How is it Different from n8n/Zapier?”
DimensionOpaln8n
Learning CurvePure natural language, designed for non-technical usersRequires understanding node parameters, possibly writing JS
AI ModelsNative Gemini integration, visual promptsVia third-party plugins or custom configuration
PricingCurrently free, cloud-hostedCommunity edition free, commercial use requires self-hosting or paid plans
LimitationsUS Beta only, no custom code support yetHigh flexibility but steep learning curve

Three-Step Getting Started Guide

Section titled “Three-Step Getting Started Guide”
  1. Join Google Labs Waitlist: Log into labs.google.com → Apply for Opal beta access.
  2. Describe: Enter your requirements in the chat box, press Enter.
  3. Refine & Share: Drag and adjust in the visual workflow → Click Share to generate a link.

Potential Pitfalls & Opportunities

Section titled “Potential Pitfalls & Opportunities”
  • Geographic Restrictions: Currently only supports US accounts, domestic users need alternative access methods.
  • Extensibility: Custom functions not yet available, complex business needs may be limited.
  • Opportunities: Workflow template marketplaces, prompt component libraries, team collaboration versions could become the next wave of opportunities.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

Opal downgrades “AI application development” from writing code to writing one sentence + dragging blocks. Any automation idea you can think of can basically be up and running before your lunch break ends. What will you build with it first? Feel free to share in the comments.

How Do Exchange Rates Affect Pricing?

Section titled “How Do Exchange Rates Affect Pricing?”

You can see that price differences are closely tied to exchange rates:

  • Local currency countries: India charges ₹1,999 monthly, equivalent to 165.66 CNY; Brazil’s R$99.90 equals 128.69 CNY.

In other words, when exchange rates change, subscription costs fluctuate accordingly. If you frequently travel or have multiple Apple IDs from different countries, it’s worth comparing prices across regions before deciding where to subscribe.

With So Many Countries, Which One Suits You?

Section titled “With So Many Countries, Which One Suits You?”

Brief analysis of several common regions:

  • Asia: Nigeria and Pakistan offer relatively low prices, while Southeast Asian and Middle Eastern regions mostly use USD pricing with relatively stable costs.
  • Americas: Many Latin American countries also use USD pricing or local currencies pegged to USD, with fairly consistent fees.
  • Europe: Eurozone and UK prices are higher, so bargain hunters should think twice.

If you’re able to register Apple IDs in multiple regions, consider choosing low-price zones for subscription. However, pay attention to the legality of account and payment method issues.

AI Era: Software Engineers' Core Competency is Managing Software Projects

Jul 22, 2025
Vibe Sparking

💥Introduction: When “Tech Gurus” Are No Longer Popular

Section titled “💥Introduction: When “Tech Gurus” Are No Longer Popular”

Remember that era? As long as you could write code, handle system architecture, dominate LeetCode, and shine with technical prowess, you could walk tall. But AI came, and everything changed.

Today, someone who barely codes can piece together an App with ChatGPT; a newcomer can write automation scripts with Claude, even running tests on the side.

At this point, you might wonder: “What value do we engineers actually have?”

Actually, the answer is clear:

In the AI era, truly valuable skills aren’t technical details, but management capabilities.

Not traditional “team leading,” but broad engineering management capabilities— how to define objectives, organize information, decompose tasks, coordinate AI and humans, evaluate outcomes, and adjust timely.

🧠Part One: In the Allocation Economy Era, Everyone is a “Manager”

Section titled “🧠Part One: In the Allocation Economy Era, Everyone is a “Manager””

Dan Shipper proposed a very interesting concept:

We’re transitioning from the “knowledge economy” to the “allocation economy” era.

Previously, competition was about who mastered more skills; Now, it’s about who can better allocate AI, information, and resources.

This isn’t empty talk, but your real daily experience working with AI:

  • How do you clearly explain tasks so AI understands you?
  • How do you break complex requirements into pieces for different tools to handle efficiently?
  • How do you select models, set prompts, and evaluate result quality?
  • When do you let AI run autonomously, and when should you intervene with details?

Sound familiar? Isn’t this exactly a “manager’s” daily work?

Most importantly:

AI has lowered management barriers, allowing everyone to be a project owner.

🛠 Part Two: What Are the Most Valuable Core Skills for the Future?

Section titled “🛠 Part Two: What Are the Most Valuable Core Skills for the Future?”

Based on Dan Shipper’s observations and hands-on practice, the scarcest future skills include:

  • Information organization and problem articulation: Not about knowing more, but expressing problems clearly.
  • Resource scheduling and task decomposition: Assigning tasks to appropriate people/AI and coordinating outputs.
  • Judgment and result evaluation capabilities: Is this work good? Is it trustworthy?
  • Vision setting and goal driving: Telling AI and teams “where we’re going.”

More bluntly:

You need to learn to “run a company” with AI, not “write code” yourself.

🔁 Part Three: Generalist Value Returns, Small Teams Are the Future

Section titled “🔁 Part Three: Generalist Value Returns, Small Teams Are the Future”

Another huge change AI brings is making “generalists” more valuable.

In Dan’s team of 15 people, everyone wears multiple hats:

  • One person can code, design, and handle operations;
  • A project can go from 0 to launch with just 2-3 people;
  • AI becomes “expert patches,” helping generalists quickly reach standards in various fields.

You no longer need a decade-deep specialist for every position. Instead, you need people with big picture thinking + hands-on ability + AI collaboration skills.

Future companies might resemble today’s startup small teams:

Everyone can lead projects, everyone can collaborate with AI for production, everyone has a “managerial mindset”.

🚀Conclusion: AI Isn’t a Silver Bullet, Real Capabilities Are Trump Cards

Section titled “🚀Conclusion: AI Isn’t a Silver Bullet, Real Capabilities Are Trump Cards”

Yes, AI is powerful, but it’s not divine. It can’t set objectives for you or judge truly valuable directions.

Future software engineers won’t just be “code writers” or just architecture-savvy “tech gurus.”

They’ll be:

“Engineering managers” who can orchestrate tools, evaluate outcomes, decompose problems, and drive progress.

So instead of grinding new models and frameworks, consider:

Have you truly mastered the management skills of “allocating AI capabilities”?

Startup AI Deployment Practical Guide: From Training to Impact, Step by Step!

Jul 22, 2025
Vibe Sparking

In recent years, generative artificial intelligence (AI) has sparked a wave of enterprise transformation. Companies across industries have announced “AI-first” strategies, integrating AI into daily operations. As a hedge fund CEO said, not using AI is like refusing to use the internet in 1995 - these tools can instantly make people smarter and more efficient. Many leading companies have already taken action: Companies like Shopify have even made using AI a hard performance requirement, requiring employees to prove AI cannot handle a task before requesting new resources, and prioritizing AI exploration in project prototype phases. Duolingo also announced in a company-wide letter its complete transformation into an “AI-first” company, developing 148 new courses through AI in less than a year, exceeding the total of the past 12 years combined! Clearly, skillful AI use can bring 10x to 100x efficiency improvements. For startups, how to catch this wave and truly implement AI in team work is a key issue related to efficiency and competitiveness. This article combines industry best practices to provide startups with a practical guide for gradually implementing AI training and application deployment.

Step One: Leadership Leads by Example, Creating AI-First Culture

Section titled “Step One: Leadership Leads by Example, Creating AI-First Culture”

To successfully drive AI implementation, executive commitment and participation are crucial. CEOs and founding teams must lead by example, personally experiencing and daily using AI tools like ChatGPT and Claude. If leaders themselves use AI daily and find it “cool,” the entire company often follows quickly. This isn’t exaggeration: observations show that whether CEOs actively use AI is the primary predictor of successful enterprise AI implementation. Companies with CEO-driven initiatives see more employee enthusiasm for trying AI and easier innovation culture formation.

Leadership should clearly communicate “AI-first” strategic commitment to all employees. For example, Shopify CEO’s internal memo stated bluntly: “Instinctively using AI is now a basic expectation for everyone”. This letter wasn’t generic encouragement but elevated AI application to strategic and institutional heights, requiring everyone from CEO to all employees to integrate AI into work without exception. Leaders can follow similar approaches, issuing company-wide letters or declaring AI strategy in all-hands meetings, writing “try AI before asking for human help” into company culture guidelines and performance evaluations. Importantly, leadership must truly invest, deeply understanding AI’s potential and limitations rather than treating it merely as an IT department matter. Only when leadership has proper awareness and leads by example can they set examples for the entire company and create an organizational atmosphere embracing AI.

Step Two: Survey Current State, Identify AI Application Scenarios

Section titled “Step Two: Survey Current State, Identify AI Application Scenarios”

After establishing commitment, the next step is finding the most suitable entry points for introducing AI into team work. Startups can conduct internal surveys or brainstorming sessions to inventory repetitive, tedious, time-consuming tasks across positions and business processes. For example, does customer service frequently answer repetitive questions? Does the marketing team need extensive copywriting? Do developers perform repetitive code reviews? These areas could all benefit from AI efficiency improvements. Many AI consulting advisors entering enterprises interview various teams about workflow processes, identify pain points that AI can optimize, then form briefings with solution approaches for management feedback. When startup teams are small, founders or designated “AI leaders” can personally lead this process, mapping AI applications: listing short-term AI use cases to try and long-term projects to explore.

Based on this, establish an internal knowledge base to accumulate these survey results and potential use cases. For instance, compile collected cases and operation guides into documents, or even build a simple internal chatbot where employees can ask “How do we use AI to accelerate XX tasks?” and get answers. This resembles consultation advisors’ interview material retrieval bots built for clients, helping employees self-explore AI opportunities. Through such knowledge bases, team members encountering problems in daily work can conveniently search for AI solutions, gradually forming “ask AI first” habits.

Step Three: Customized Training, Comprehensively Improve Team AI Skills

Section titled “Step Three: Customized Training, Comprehensively Improve Team AI Skills”

After determining application scenarios, teams need systematic AI skills training. Surveys show nearly half of employees want formal AI training and consider it the best way to improve AI adoption rates. Startups can create targeted training plans based on each position’s needs. AI is general-purpose technology with vastly different applications across functions. Therefore training must align with business: let marketing personnel learn generative AI copywriting, customer service learn conversational model customer responses, product and technical teams learn AI-assisted coding and prototype design.

Practice proves small-step-quick-run training is more effective. A 4-week training sprint can be arranged: using 1 hour weekly for training or workshops over four consecutive weeks covering key skills. Each session focuses on one theme like “AI writing techniques,” “Prompt construction skills,” “Using AI for data analysis,” allowing employees to gradually digest and absorb. Between training intervals, encourage learners to practice in actual work, applying learning to current tasks and sharing feedback at next training. To ensure training unifies knowledge and action, managers should design job-relevant exercises for each employee. For example, prepare simulated customer Q&A prompts for sales personnel, provide AI resume screening demonstrations for HR. Such customized scenario practice helps employees genuinely experience AI’s help with their work, improving learning interest and usage intention.

Worth noting, training should teach not just “how to use” but also help teams understand AI limitations and best practices, managing expectations. Current AI models aren’t omnipotent and still have errors and imperfections. If unrealistic high expectations are placed on AI, disappointment and abandonment may occur when encountering setbacks. Therefore training should introduce AI-suitable task types, common errors and countermeasures, helping employees establish correct mindset: treat AI as a powerful assistant, not a perfect magic wand. When employees master skills while maintaining rational expectations, AI tools can truly integrate long-term into their daily work.

Step Four: Discover Internal AI Pioneers, Drive Experience Sharing

Section titled “Step Four: Discover Internal AI Pioneers, Drive Experience Sharing”

Within teams, there often exists different acceptance levels for new technology - roughly 10% of employees are extremely enthusiastic about trying AI, 10% may resist or oppose, while the remaining 80% mainly observe - if someone guides them to use it in their work, they’re willing to try. Startups should fully leverage the power of the front 10% “AI geek” employees, making them internal AI pioneers and demonstrators.

Specific measures include:

  • Designate AI champions or mentors: Select “AI champions” from employees enthusiastic about exploring AI to serve as mentors in teams, ready to answer colleagues’ questions about AI tools and share usage techniques. This gives pioneers honor and responsibility while providing nearby learning examples for others.

  • Establish sharing mechanisms: Regularly organize AI experience sharing sessions. Weekly online or offline sharing where employees showcase AI-completed work cases, problems encountered, and solution insights. Such “weekly prompt shows” let colleagues from different positions learn from each other and spark new creative ideas.

  • Internal communication channels: Build company internal AI exchange platforms like Slack channels, WeChat groups, encouraging employees to discuss AI discoveries and applications anytime. From companies like Shopify’s experience, providing abundant AI tools and encouraging sharing success and failure cases in internal channels helps create supportive learning atmospheres. When people see colleagues using AI to solve actual problems, they’re more motivated to try themselves.

  • Experience libraries and prompt libraries: Collect and organize employee-shared excellent use cases and prompts into AI experience libraries or prompt handbooks. This way newcomers or temporarily idea-less employees can reference others’ successful cases, lowering exploration barriers. Transform early users’ exploration results into company-wide reusable processes and templates, avoiding detours and achieving quick results.

Through above methods, transform scattered personal exploration into company-wide collective learning. By providing platforms and rewards for AI pioneers, letting them stand out, the entire organization’s learning and innovation will automatically spread. This is one secret to advancing AI implementation: let internal innovation sparks create prairie fire.

Step Five: Establish Incentive Mechanisms, Normalize AI Applications

Section titled “Step Five: Establish Incentive Mechanisms, Normalize AI Applications”

To prevent AI applications from being flash-in-the-pan phenomena, they must be integrated into daily work assessment and incentive mechanisms, forming continuous improvement loops. As experts point out, relying solely on harsh notices to drive change is high-risk unless accompanied by training, tools, and management support, otherwise it easily becomes formalistic. Therefore, companies need institutional safeguards for AI implementation.

Performance evaluation and goal binding: Incorporating AI usage into employee performance indicators is directly effective. For example, examining whether employees used AI tools when completing tasks, usage frequency and incremental value created; teams needing additional staff or budget must prove they’ve fully utilized AI for efficiency improvements. This mechanism sends clear signals: AI application isn’t optional but work’s new normal and basic requirement. Of course, setting indicators should be reasonable, guidance-focused, avoiding excessive pressure causing resentment. The key is making employees realize skillful AI use will positively impact personal development and team performance.

Data tracking and rankings: Following hedge fund Walleye’s approach, establish AI usage dashboards or leaderboards. For example, track each team or individual’s ChatGPT tool usage frequency, even evaluate AI-assisted task completion quantities. Regularly (weekly or monthly) publish this data, recognizing employees with significant progress or good prompt sharing in company-wide emails. This transparency creates “friendly competition” atmosphere on one hand, encouraging everyone to learn AI competitively; on the other hand, it lets management grasp real-time AI implementation across departments, timely discovering obstacles and highlights.

Reward early adopters: Give rewards and recognition to employees actively embracing AI with significant results. For example, material rewards, promotion considerations, or inviting them to share experiences in broader ranges. Walleye even linked AI adoption to rewards, using leaderboards and reward mechanisms to encourage fastest learners, making AI application truly “Mandatory, not optional” internally. Startups can design localized incentives like monthly “AI Innovation Awards,” commending teams with significant cost savings or efficiency improvements.

Integrate results into business objectives: Include AI-related task indicators in OKRs or quarterly goals, like “automate X processes using AI this quarter,” “increase team per-capita customer request processing by Y% (with AI tools).” This drives business leaders to actively consider using AI for business improvements. When setting these goals, consider employee willingness and feedback, ensuring goals balance feasibility and challenge.

Through systems and incentives, transform employee AI use from one-time activities to long-term habits. Only when everyone realizes the company continuously cares about and rewards AI application effectiveness will AI truly integrate into organizational DNA, becoming a natural component of daily work. Once this positive cycle begins, team AI maturity will increasingly improve, with benefits gradually manifesting and amplifying.

Step Six: Deep AI Tool Integration, Optimize Business Processes

Section titled “Step Six: Deep AI Tool Integration, Optimize Business Processes”

After training and culture are established, the key is deeply integrating AI into company daily business processes, achieving real efficiency leaps. McKinsey research shows employees consider seamlessly integrating AI into existing workflows as an important factor promoting daily use (45% of employees hold this view). Startups should examine core processes, exploring how to redesign these processes using AI to achieve higher output with less human input.

Examples include customer service automation, meeting transcription and knowledge management, internal information search, development process integration, and data analysis for decision-making. Through these measures, startups can let existing personnel achieve multiplied effectiveness with AI without significantly increasing headcount. Once AI integrates into main business processes, many previously “impossible tasks” become possible.

Conclusion: Continuous Learning, Embracing the Future

Section titled “Conclusion: Continuous Learning, Embracing the Future”

Successfully implementing AI training and applications in a startup isn’t accomplished overnight but requires continuous learning and iteration. This transformation involves technology, processes, and culture, requiring executive commitment, full participation, and patient refinement. The good news is most successful practices show AI implementation mainly brings efficiency improvements and capability expansion rather than large-scale layoffs. The AI era curtain has just opened. The earlier teams master this “efficiency key,” the more opportunities startups have to stand out in fierce competition. Instead of watching, take action; let AI work for you, do more and better - the future is here, start now!
