Q3 2025 AI Chip Supply Chain Earnings Start Strong: ASML and TSMC Beat — Where Does NVIDIA Go from Here?

Oct 16, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Opening: Tech Earnings Season Kicks Off, Supply‑Chain Confidence Surges

Section titled “Opening: Tech Earnings Season Kicks Off, Supply‑Chain Confidence Surges”

As this earnings season shows, the AI chip supply chain is firing on all cylinders. ASML and TSMC both delivered upside surprises in their Q3 2025 results. More importantly, both turned distinctly more optimistic in their outlooks — a sharp contrast with their cautious tone three months ago.

ASML Q3 Highlights

Section titled “ASML Q3 Highlights”
  • Revenue €7.5B (≈$8.2B), gross margin 51.6%
  • Net income €2.125B, above market expectations
  • Q4 guidance raised sharply to €9.2–9.8B; gross margin 51%–53%
  • The key shift: management explicitly said 2026 revenue will not be below 2025, reversing prior uncertainty

TSMC’s Record Q3

Section titled “TSMC’s Record Q3”
  • Revenue NT$989.92B (≈$32.2B), up 39% y/y
  • Net income NT$452.3B (≈$14.8B), a record high
  • ADR EPS up 39% y/y; dynamic P/E only 33x, PEG < 1
  • Full‑year revenue growth target raised from 30% to 35%
  • Gross margin at a strong 59.5%, underscoring pricing power

The core message from both prints: AI acceleration is itself being accelerated.

Supply‑Chain Triangle: ASML → TSMC → NVIDIA

Section titled “Supply‑Chain Triangle: ASML → TSMC → NVIDIA”

To understand how these three companies interrelate, start with the key nodes in the AI chip value chain:

ASML — The Photolithography Monopolist

Section titled “ASML — The Photolithography Monopolist”
  • World’s only supplier of EUV lithography equipment
  • Each EUV system sells for >$200M; the latest High‑NA EUV is ~$380M per unit
  • All advanced‑node manufacturers (TSMC, Samsung, Intel) depend heavily on ASML

TSMC — The King of Advanced Foundry

Section titled “TSMC — The King of Advanced Foundry”
  • 61% global foundry share; 67% share in advanced nodes (<7nm)
  • Chips from all top AI designers (NVIDIA, Apple, AMD, etc.) are manufactured by TSMC
  • Mass‑production at 3nm; 2nm in progress

NVIDIA — The AI Chip Design Leader

Section titled “NVIDIA — The AI Chip Design Leader”
  • ~$4.38T market cap; data‑center business is ~88% of revenue
  • Relies entirely on TSMC’s advanced nodes to produce H100, H200, and the new Blackwell series
  • Blackwell generated ~$11B in FY25 Q4 revenue alone — the “fastest product ramp” in company history

The transmission goes: ASML’s EUV tools → TSMC’s advanced‑node capacity → NVIDIA’s AI chip supply. A bottleneck at any node constrains the entire chain.

Data: Stock‑Performance and Results Correlations

Section titled “Data: Stock‑Performance and Results Correlations”

Analyzing post‑earnings stock moves for these three from 2022–2025 reveals important patterns:

Finding 1: TSMC’s Link to NVIDIA Is Tighter

Section titled “Finding 1: TSMC’s Link to NVIDIA Is Tighter”

Our statistics show:

  • In the 10 trading days after TSMC earnings, NVIDIA’s return correlation is 0.70 (p=0.004) — statistically significant
  • After ASML earnings, NVIDIA’s 10‑day correlation is 0.49 (p=0.065) — weaker
  • Over 30‑day windows, TSMC↔NVIDIA correlation stays ~0.48; ASML drops to ~0.13

This supports the intuition that the nearer the link in the chain, the stronger the co‑movement. As NVIDIA’s direct supplier, TSMC’s capacity and process progress directly shape how many chips NVIDIA can ship.

Finding 2: Supplier Earnings Tend to Lift NVIDIA

Section titled “Finding 2: Supplier Earnings Tend to Lift NVIDIA”

On average:

  • In the 10 days after ASML reports, NVIDIA rose 3.8%; over 30 days, +14.2%
  • In the 10 days after TSMC reports, NVIDIA rose 2.1%; over 30 days, +15.8%
  • The effect was most pronounced in 2023 (the breakout year for AI): post‑ASML 30‑day average +19.8%; post‑TSMC +26.7%

Finding 3: Correlations Weakened in 2024–2025 but Remain Meaningful

Section titled “Finding 3: Correlations Weakened in 2024–2025 but Remain Meaningful”

As NVIDIA’s market cap surpassed $3T, its stock began to reflect more macro factors:

  • Fed policy expectations
  • U.S. export‑control policy toward China
  • Competition (AMD; hyperscaler in‑house chips)

Even so, supplier earnings remain pivotal indicators for NVIDIA — especially TSMC’s expansion plans and ASML’s EUV shipment cadence, which map to NVIDIA’s potential shipments over the next 12–18 months.

Share‑Price Performance Comparison

Section titled “Share‑Price Performance Comparison”

NVIDIA vs. ASML vs. TSMC since early 2022 (indexed to 100 at Jan 2022)

The chart shows NVIDIA up 497%, TSMC up 137%, and ASML up 27% over the period. The gap reflects differences in value capture:

  • NVIDIA: commands the highest value‑added layer via CUDA software ecosystem and leading chip design
  • TSMC: manufacturing profits are high (≈60% gross margin) but massive capex tempers stock elasticity
  • ASML: EUV monopoly, but the customer base is limited (principally TSMC, Samsung, Intel) and deliveries are long‑cycle, making growth steadier

Three Key Signals from This Earnings Season

Section titled “Three Key Signals from This Earnings Season”

Signal 1: ASML’s 2026 Stance Flips 180 Degrees

Section titled “Signal 1: ASML’s 2026 Stance Flips 180 Degrees”

In July 2024 (Q2), ASML cautioned that “given rising macro and geopolitical uncertainty, we cannot confirm growth in 2026,” triggering a ~16% single‑day drop.

In Q3, management stated clearly that “2026 total revenue will not be below 2025,” stressing enduring AI‑driven demand. Drivers include:

  • Strong EUV orders: €5.4B net bookings in Q3, with €3.6B from EUV
  • High‑NA EUV shipments have begun, at higher price points (~$380M/unit)
  • TSMC, Samsung, Intel are all expanding; photolithography demand remains robust

Signal 2: TSMC Lifts 2025 Full‑Year Growth from 30% to 35%

Section titled “Signal 2: TSMC Lifts 2025 Full‑Year Growth from 30% to 35%”

In Q3, TSMC raised its 2025 USD revenue‑growth outlook from 30% to 35% — the second raise this year. CFO Wendell Huang noted: “We saw strong demand for advanced technologies in Q3, and as we enter Q4, we expect the strength to continue.”

More importantly, TSMC said 3nm and more advanced nodes are fully booked, with customers “competing for capacity,” indicating strong demand from NVIDIA, Apple, and others.

Signal 3: NVIDIA Blackwell Is Supply‑Constrained; Q4 Guide Above Street

Section titled “Signal 3: NVIDIA Blackwell Is Supply‑Constrained; Q4 Guide Above Street”

While NVIDIA has not yet reported Q3 (due Nov 19), Q2 already showed a rapid Blackwell ramp:

  • Q2 Blackwell revenue +17% q/q
  • Q3 guide $54.0B (±2%), above the Street’s $52.7B
  • CEO Jensen Huang: “Blackwell demand is surprising… we have achieved large‑scale mass production of Blackwell AI supercomputers.”

UBS lifted its Blackwell Q4 revenue estimate from $5B to $9B, signaling demand far above prior expectations.

Forecast: NVIDIA’s Q3 and the Road Ahead

Section titled “Forecast: NVIDIA’s Q3 and the Road Ahead”

Q3 (reports Nov 19)

Section titled “Q3 (reports Nov 19)”
  • Revenue: consensus $54.0B; we see a modest beat to $54.5–55.0B
  • Data‑center revenue: $47–48B (≈88–89% mix)
  • Gross margin: 73–74%, a touch above Q2’s 72.7%
  • Q4 guide: $60–65B as Blackwell continues to ramp

Stock outlook

Section titled “Stock outlook”

Near term (1 month post‑print): if results land near expectations, shares could test the $200–210 range (≈11–17% above the current ~$180).

Through 2025 year‑end: Street average target ~$222 (+23%).

Upside case: if Blackwell capacity and demand both exceed expectations, HSBC’s $320 target is conceivable (+78%).

Key risks

Section titled “Key risks”
  • Macro slowdown: weaker global growth could curb enterprise IT spending
  • Rising competition: AMD’s MI300 and hyperscaler in‑house silicon (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium) chip away at share
  • Geopolitics: tighter U.S. export controls on China
  • Valuation pressure: current ~51x P/E sits well above the market average

Investment Takeaways: Positioning for the AI Chip Chain

Section titled “Investment Takeaways: Positioning for the AI Chip Chain”

Core holding: NVIDIA

Section titled “Core holding: NVIDIA”
  • Strengths: clear technology lead and a moat built on CUDA’s software ecosystem
  • Valuation: ~51x P/E not trivial, but ~1.3 PEG (on 35–40% growth) remains reasonable
  • Allocation: core position, ~50–60% of an AI‑theme sleeve

Quality anchor: TSMC

Section titled “Quality anchor: TSMC”
  • Strengths: leading advanced‑node manufacturing; diversified blue‑chip customers (Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, etc.); hard to replace
  • Valuation: ~33.6x P/E, below NVIDIA; PEG < 1 — attractive
  • Allocation: primary holding for balanced profiles, ~30–40%

Defensive ballast: ASML

Section titled “Defensive ballast: ASML”
  • Strengths: absolute EUV monopoly; high long‑term visibility
  • Weaknesses: slower growth; more sensitive to macro cycles; sold off in 2024 on China concerns
  • Allocation: defensive weight, ~10–20%

Note: Mind the transmission lags along the chain

Section titled “Note: Mind the transmission lags along the chain”

Our work suggests ASML/TSMC earnings impact NVIDIA with lag:

  • Short term (≤10 days): higher correlations, smaller absolute moves
  • Medium term (~30 days): weaker correlation, larger cumulative moves
  • Longer term (quarterly): all three converge toward fundamentals

Hence we do not recommend short‑term trading purely on supplier reports; focus on multi‑quarter trends.

Conclusion: The Acceleration of AI Acceleration Is Just Beginning

Section titled “Conclusion: The Acceleration of AI Acceleration Is Just Beginning”

ASML and TSMC’s Q3 beats are not only their own victories; they’re also barometers of supply‑chain health. As TSMC’s CFO put it, “We continue to see strong AI‑related demand, and we expect the trend to extend into 2026.”

Historically, NVIDIA has risen an average 15.8% in the 30 days post‑TSMC earnings, and 14.2% after ASML. With both suppliers beating this season, NVIDIA enters its Q3 report with a constructive setup.

For investors, the AI chip chain presents a rare case of full‑chain resonance: upstream ASML orderbooks are full; midstream TSMC is running flat out; downstream NVIDIA demand remains robust. In this backdrop, the acceleration of AI acceleration may be only getting started.

How ChatGPT Helps Fix Bluetooth Device UI Chaos

Oct 16, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Scenario recap: You built a mobile app that scans nearby BLE devices. Your office/ward/desk is full of the same model, all with the same name. The scan results keep “jumping” as new devices arrive; just as users tap one, it gets pushed away — pure frustration for a PM.

This article doesn’t go into protocol internals. Instead, it shows how to use ChatGPT as your AI interaction partner: turn complex technical constraints into a UI your team can build, users can understand, and QA can validate.

1) Align on technical boundaries first, then pick the UI

Section titled “1) Align on technical boundaries first, then pick the UI”

For BLE scanning, a few facts directly shape your design:

  • Advertising payload: Useful info includes local name, service UUIDs, manufacturer data, Tx Power, etc. These can help you distinguish devices and filter. They’re read from the advertisement data structures.
  • iOS does not expose MAC addresses (privacy). Apps receive a CBPeripheral.identifier-style UUID, and it is not guaranteed to be permanently stable. Do not bake “display/remember MAC address” into any cross‑platform plan. Prefer short codes/manufacturer data from the advertisement as the differentiator.
  • Android can pre-filter and batch callbacks via ScanFilter and ScanSettings (e.g., filter by service UUID/manufacturer data and use setReportDelay for batched dispatch). This dramatically reduces UI jitter.

With these boundaries clear, ChatGPT can propose cross‑platform interaction patterns that are actionable, not just theory.

2) Break “I can’t tap the right one” into three solvable UX problems

Section titled “2) Break “I can’t tap the right one” into three solvable UX problems”

A. Recognition: Too many identical names — which one do I pick?

Section titled “A. Recognition: Too many identical names — which one do I pick?”

Solution: Generate a human‑recognizable short label per device.

  • Ask hardware to add a short code in the advertised name or Manufacturer Data (e.g., last 4 digits of S/N or batch + checksum). Surface it as a subtitle: Device Name · Short Code 3K7F. This is a common use of BLE advertisement.
  • iOS cannot show MAC: use the short code and/or service signature from the advertisement as the distinguishing factor. Don’t promise MAC in product specs.

B. Jitter: List jumps as it refreshes; finger misses the target

Section titled “B. Jitter: List jumps as it refreshes; finger misses the target”

Solution: Decouple “data changes” from “interface changes”.

  • Batched callbacks: On Android, enable ScanSettings.Builder#setReportDelay(xxx) so N updates arrive as a batch. Diff‑update the list once to reduce item shifts.
  • Freeze interactive zones: When the user long‑presses or enters multi‑select, freeze sorting and positions. Only update light fields inside items (e.g., RSSI value). Merge the full refresh after the action. This follows the principle “don’t let refreshes interrupt the user’s task”.

C. Decision load: Too noisy — help me narrow scope first

Section titled “C. Decision load: Too noisy — help me narrow scope first”

Solution: Put filter controls where the hand is.

  • Use Filter/Assist Chips above the list for one‑tap toggles like “Recently used only”, “Strong signal only”, “Connectable only”, “Target service only”. Chips are made for quick filtering.
  • Make filtering/sorting intent‑aligned — avoid large jumps that break focus. NN/g specifically recommends minimizing “view jumping” when applying filters.

3) A ready‑to‑ship UI package for PMs (via ChatGPT)

Section titled “3) A ready‑to‑ship UI package for PMs (via ChatGPT)”

You can paste the following to ChatGPT to polish copy, generate a prototype (describe it), or output component and acceptance lists.

(1) List item anatomy

Section titled “(1) List item anatomy”
  • Title: Device name (append batch/model if names collide)
  • Subtitle: Short code · Service overview (e.g., 3K7F · Battery, UpdateSvc)
  • Right‑side info: RSSI (supports the “tap the one you’re close to” mental model; if showing distance, prefer coarse buckets strong/medium/weak to avoid misleading precision)

Engineer Growth Principles in the AI Era

Sep 16, 2025
Vibe Sparking

This article distills four often-overlooked yet crucial growth principles: argue less about who’s right and more for win–wins, ship first then optimize, don’t fear overlap—insist on differentiation, and stop wasting time proving yourself.

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

As AI advances at breakneck speed, engineers face not only a technical upgrade but also a test of worldview and workplace mindset. Have you ever alienated a colleague by clinging to “being right”? Missed a window chasing perfection? These are common, and exactly what this piece explores. After years of trial and error, I’ve refined four practical growth principles that continue to work in the real world. I hope they resonate.

1. Incentive Thinking: Don’t Argue Right vs. Wrong—Argue for Win–Win

Section titled “1. Incentive Thinking: Don’t Argue Right vs. Wrong—Argue for Win–Win”

Early in my career, I was eager to prove I was “right.” Over time I realized: in organizations, “right” is rarely the most important thing—“what benefits the project/team” is. Obsessing over abstract “elegance” and “pure logic” while ignoring delivery, risk, resources, and ROI balance easily gets labeled as nitpicking.

Key mindset shifts:

  • From “I must win” to “Does the team get better overall?”
  • From “theoretical perfection” to “maximizing this iteration’s value”
  • From “debate positions” to “collaborative game → win–win options”

A practical trick: before speaking or rebutting, silently count to 5 and ask yourself:

  1. Will this statement move the decision or outcome forward?
  2. Is it fair and beneficial to both the other party and the team?
  3. Is there a way to express this with lower conflict cost?

Argue less about right/wrong, aim for win–wins, and your reputation as a collaborator—along with your influence and trust—will rise naturally.

2. No Premature Optimization: Ship First, Optimize After Validation

Section titled “2. No Premature Optimization: Ship First, Optimize After Validation”

Perfectionism often delays the first version. When users haven’t validated and value isn’t closed-loop, pouring effort into performance, abstraction, and grand architecture is classic “premature optimization.”

The first version (V0 / MVP) has only two missions:

  • Validate there are real users who want it.
  • Validate the path is worth doubling down on.

Practice tips:

  • Set your “Minimal Success Level” (MSL) for this round.
  • Cap initial delivery investment (person-days / complexity / dependencies).
  • Leave room for refactoring instead of hard-locking the architecture up-front.
  • Track hypothesis → ship → feedback → iteration cadence (accelerate loops, don’t just pile features).

When you see stable core usage + 100+ real, recurring users + paying signals or strong retention, that’s when systematic refactors/perf work actually pay off.

The mantra isn’t “settle for less,” it’s “validate fast + optimize with purpose.”

3. Don’t Be Intimidated by Logic: It’s Fine If Others Did It—Build Your Difference

Section titled “3. Don’t Be Intimidated by Logic: It’s Fine If Others Did It—Build Your Difference”

“Someone’s done it” isn’t a veto; it’s market proof and a learning sample. Differentiation isn’t only technical—often it comes from:

  • Perspective: a new framing, entry point, or audience focus for the same problem
  • Experience: compressed flows, friction removal, instant feedback
  • Value exchange: open/closed source, subscription/one-off, tool + community hybrids
  • Narrative and persona: trust and cultural tone as invisible moats

Suggested execution steps:

  1. List a pros/cons matrix of existing solutions.
  2. Highlight under-served user scenarios and emotional pain points.
  3. Define your “Differentiated Core” via subtraction: the smallest set that makes you distinct.
  4. Build a demo with perceivable differences first; collect real feedback, then expand.

Don’t overestimate the risk of “repetition” and underestimate the compounding of “consistency + differentiation + focus.”

4. Stop Proving Yourself: Spend Energy on Building and Joy

Section titled “4. Stop Proving Yourself: Spend Energy on Building and Joy”

For technologists, the scarcest resource isn’t tools—it’s sustained, deep attention. Instead of bleeding energy in pointless debates and emotional tug-of-war, convert your will to win into building power.

Common self-draining traps:

  • “I must make them admit I know more.”
  • “Their lack of recognition = I’m not good.”
  • “I must clap back at every jab.”

Replacements:

  • Let outcomes/code/metrics/demos speak.
  • Identify “non-productive debates”: not falsifiable + no decision rights + emotion-led.
  • Maintain a personal “attention blacklist”: don’t trigger, don’t chase, don’t loop.
  • Build positive loops: output (articles/open source/prototypes) → feedback → improvement.

Let your work establish your identity and reputation—not momentary keyboard wins.

Final Thoughts

Section titled “Final Thoughts”

To recap the four principles:

  1. Argue less about right/wrong; aim for win–wins.
  2. Validate value first; then refine and optimize.
  3. Don’t fear overlap; insist on differentiation.
  4. Don’t waste energy proving; commit to sustained creation.

In the AI era, tool dividends are accelerating “capability depreciation.” Knowing a stack is no longer a lasting moat. What compounds sustainably is:

  • Mindset flexibility
  • Strategic execution
  • Differentiated expression
  • Focus and continuous retrospection

The path of growth is inevitably winding. If your direction is right, the distance won’t matter. May we embrace the technological tide while consolidating inner judgment and creativity.

What Is Vibe Coding?

Section titled “What Is Vibe Coding?”

Why Is This the Biggest Perk for 35+ Architects?

Section titled “Why Is This the Biggest Perk for 35+ Architects?”

Because you hold three rare assets:

  • System‑level abstraction: you know what to do and what not to do. AI needs guardrails.
  • Engineering cadence: you’ve lived through versioning, dependencies, environments, and regressions. AI fears “no constraints.”

Your moat is exactly this: channel AI’s “speed” into a predictable engineering process. That’s where Vibe Coding sits.

The Truth: “Geniuses Don’t Need It; Ordinary Folks Can Trip Over It”

Section titled “The Truth: “Geniuses Don’t Need It; Ordinary Folks Can Trip Over It””

The core issue isn’t “can a model write a few lines of code,” but can you run requirements → design → implementation → verification → deployment → observability as a repeatable loop. The data already signals it: in 2025, developer sentiment toward AI has dropped while distrust has risen — treating AI outputs as “answers,” instead of “artifacts to be verified,” sends you off course quickly.

Typical failure patterns (common among outsiders / low‑experience programmers):

  1. Requirements drift: no clear acceptance criteria → prompts keep getting longer → results drift farther.
  2. Structural entropy: no governance for directories, module boundaries, env vars, or dependency versions → unmaintainable in weeks.
  3. Blind bug‑fixing: no test baseline → manual clicking → fix one, break three.
  4. Broken data loop: no events/metrics captured → no activation, retention, or value evidence.

  7. Only “prompt tuning,” never “interface + constraint design”: agent artifacts lack consistency → reuse fails.

  • CS fundamentals (compilers, OS, database normal forms, algorithms and data structures)
  • Engineering practice (version control, dependency governance, performance and incident triage, CI/CD)

Why AI Product Development Differs from Traditional Software: Continuous Calibration and Continuous Development — Uncertainty and the ‘Agency vs. Control’ Challenge

Aug 21, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

The current wave of generative AI has pushed companies to ship AI‑powered features for fear of missing the moment. ByteDance launched the conversational app “Doubao,” and Tencent rolled out its in‑house chat assistant “Yuanbao.” Many product teams, under executive pressure, rapidly integrated AI: the idea looked promising, the demo dazzled, and early user feedback seemed positive. Yet once the product goes live, reality bites. The system surfaces unexpected errors; users bring a wild variety of requests; AI responses are inconsistent. Teams dig in and find the issues are intertwined and hard to attribute to a single fix, shaking confidence in the entire product approach. Worse, these hidden faults quietly erode user trust.

Why the yawning gap between a slick demo and a fragile production reality? At the root, AI products break several assumptions that traditional software depends on. If you build AI products the traditional way, you’ll hit trap after trap. Boiled down, there are two critical differences to take seriously:

  • AI products are inherently uncertain — both user inputs and model outputs are unpredictable.
  • AI products must balance agency and control — every time you grant the system more autonomous “agency,” you cede some human control. Ignore this and push autonomy too far, and you invite runaway decisions and hard‑to‑trace errors.

Many teams miss these two differences, leading to cascading problems during scale‑up and optimization: bizarre failures, bad decisions, even trust crises. To address these challenges, experienced practitioners have proposed a new product development frame that helps teams build stable, trustworthy AI features with intent. Below we unpack the two differences and introduce the Continuous Calibration / Continuous Development (CC/CD) loop that tackles them, followed by a concrete example showing how to ship AI features step by step in daily work.

The Uncertainty of AI Products

Section titled “The Uncertainty of AI Products”

Traditional software tends to be deterministic. User interactions are limited and explicit: clicking buttons, filling forms, calling fixed APIs. Developers hard‑code logic to map inputs to outputs. When something breaks, it’s usually a code bug that’s relatively easy to locate and fix.

AI products are born uncertain. They introduce a double layer of unpredictability:

  1. Input uncertainty

    • Inputs are no longer constrained to predefined operations; they may be open‑ended natural language, voice commands, images, and more.
    • Expressions are flexible, hard to exhaustively validate, and prone to ambiguity.
    • In support bots, the same question can be phrased in hundreds of ways; in content generation, prompt nuances produce drastically different results; in code assistants, vague requirements can yield entirely different implementations.

  2. Output uncertainty

    • Traditional software produces the same output for the same input; AI models generate probabilistic answers — essentially, “best guesses.”
    • The same request can vary across contexts, times, or model versions.
    • Output uncertainty makes testing harder — classic unit tests don’t fit; you need to assess quality by probabilities and trends.

Therefore, AI product development must accept: model behavior is not absolutely controllable; you must continuously observe and calibrate deviations from intent.

Balancing Agency and Control

Section titled “Balancing Agency and Control”

Another defining difference is the introduction of “agency” — the system’s ability to act and decide on the user’s behalf.

  • High‑agency examples

    • A support bot replies directly to users
    • A writing assistant auto‑publishes articles
    • A code assistant commits changes to a repo

  • Risks and issues

    • More autonomy → weaker control
    • When wrong, consequences exceed typical bugs
    • Harder to trace root causes; debugging and fixes get tougher

So autonomy must be “earned” gradually:

  • Start with assistive suggestions (low agency, high control) and human review
  • Increase permissions after proven reliability
  • Keep human takeover and rollback paths

This stepwise approach avoids large losses from AI mistakes and steadily builds user trust.

The CC/CD Framework

Section titled “The CC/CD Framework”

The CC/CD framework borrows from CI/CD but targets AI’s uncertainty and staged empowerment. It alternates Continuous Development (CD) with Continuous Calibration (CC).

Continuous Development (CD)

Section titled “Continuous Development (CD)”
  • Define capability scope; plan staged version boundaries
  • Build an app prototype; add logging and human‑fallbacks
  • Design evaluation metrics (Evals) to set baselines for calibration
  • Roll out to a small cohort and start gathering real feedback

Continuous Calibration (CC)

Section titled “Continuous Calibration (CC)”
  • Run evaluations to verify real‑world performance
  • Analyze logs to locate error patterns
  • Targeted improvements to prompts, retrieval, models, or data
  • Iterate until behavior stabilizes and becomes trustworthy

As loops progress, capabilities rise and agency expands — while safety and trust stay intact.

Case Study: Building a Support Bot in Stages

Section titled “Case Study: Building a Support Bot in Stages”

Version 1: AI‑assisted ticket routing (low agency, high control)

Section titled “Version 1: AI‑assisted ticket routing (low agency, high control)”
  • Role: classify only → route to the right team
  • Metric: routing accuracy
  • Low risk; humans can correct anytime
  • After calibration, accuracy can exceed 95%

Version 2: AI‑suggested replies (medium agency, medium control)

Section titled “Version 2: AI‑suggested replies (medium agency, medium control)”
  • Role: draft responses → humans review and send
  • Metrics: adoption rate and consistency
  • Improve retrieval to boost specificity and expertise
  • Draft adoption rises from 50% to 80%

Version 3: AI resolves issues autonomously (high agency, low control)

Section titled “Version 3: AI resolves issues autonomously (high agency, low control)”
  • Role: direct replies → restricted to high‑frequency, low‑risk scenarios
  • Metrics: auto‑resolution rate and CSAT
  • Start narrow, then expand coverage gradually
  • Keep human takeover and post‑hoc sampling in place

Through these three stages, the bot evolves from “assistive” to “self‑service,” lifting efficiency while preserving reliability.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

Building AI products is like welcoming a brilliant but new teammate:

  • Don’t grant full autonomy on day one — let them learn the business step by step
  • Give room while keeping oversight to ensure correct direction
  • Continuous calibration with gradual empowerment is the only stable path

The core of CC/CD:

  • Facing uncertainty → observe and calibrate continuously
  • Facing autonomy → grant agency stepwise, evolve as needed

This way, AI becomes a reliable “team member,” upgrading experience and efficiency while protecting trust.

By providing platforms and rewards for AI pioneers, letting them stand out, the entire organization’s learning and innovation will automatically spread. This is one secret to advancing AI implementation: let internal innovation sparks create prairie fire.

Step Five: Establish Incentive Mechanisms, Normalize AI Applications

Section titled “Step Five: Establish Incentive Mechanisms, Normalize AI Applications”

To prevent AI applications from being flash-in-the-pan phenomena, they must be integrated into daily work assessment and incentive mechanisms, forming continuous improvement loops. As experts point out, relying solely on harsh notices to drive change is high-risk unless accompanied by training, tools, and management support, otherwise it easily becomes formalistic. Therefore, companies need institutional safeguards for AI implementation.

Performance evaluation and goal binding: Incorporating AI usage into employee performance indicators is directly effective. For example, examining whether employees used AI tools when completing tasks, usage frequency and incremental value created; teams needing additional staff or budget must prove they’ve fully utilized AI for efficiency improvements. This mechanism sends clear signals: AI application isn’t optional but work’s new normal and basic requirement. Of course, setting indicators should be reasonable, guidance-focused, avoiding excessive pressure causing resentment. The key is making employees realize skillful AI use will positively impact personal development and team performance.

Data tracking and rankings: Following hedge fund Walleye’s approach, establish AI usage dashboards or leaderboards. For example, track each team or individual’s ChatGPT tool usage frequency, even evaluate AI-assisted task completion quantities. Regularly (weekly or monthly) publish this data, recognizing employees with significant progress or good prompt sharing in company-wide emails. This transparency creates “friendly competition” atmosphere on one hand, encouraging everyone to learn AI competitively; on the other hand, it lets management grasp real-time AI implementation across departments, timely discovering obstacles and highlights.

Reward early adopters: Give rewards and recognition to employees actively embracing AI with significant results. For example, material rewards, promotion considerations, or inviting them to share experiences in broader ranges. Walleye even linked AI adoption to rewards, using leaderboards and reward mechanisms to encourage fastest learners, making AI application truly “Mandatory, not optional” internally. Startups can design localized incentives like monthly “AI Innovation Awards,” commending teams with significant cost savings or efficiency improvements.

Integrate results into business objectives: Include AI-related task indicators in OKRs or quarterly goals, like “automate X processes using AI this quarter,” “increase team per-capita customer request processing by Y% (with AI tools).” This drives business leaders to actively consider using AI for business improvements. When setting these goals, consider employee willingness and feedback, ensuring goals balance feasibility and challenge.

Through systems and incentives, transform employee AI use from one-time activities to long-term habits. Only when everyone realizes the company continuously cares about and rewards AI application effectiveness will AI truly integrate into organizational DNA, becoming a natural component of daily work. Once this positive cycle begins, team AI maturity will increasingly improve, with benefits gradually manifesting and amplifying.

Step Six: Deep AI Tool Integration, Optimize Business Processes

Section titled “Step Six: Deep AI Tool Integration, Optimize Business Processes”

After training and culture are established, the key is deeply integrating AI into company daily business processes, achieving real efficiency leaps. McKinsey research shows employees consider seamlessly integrating AI into existing workflows as an important factor promoting daily use (45% of employees hold this view). Startups should examine core processes, exploring how to redesign these processes using AI to achieve higher output with less human input.

Examples include customer service automation, meeting transcription and knowledge management, internal information search, development process integration, and data analysis for decision-making. Through these measures, startups can let existing personnel achieve multiplied effectiveness with AI without significantly increasing headcount. Once AI integrates into main business processes, many previously “impossible tasks” become possible.

Conclusion: Continuous Learning, Embracing the Future

Section titled “Conclusion: Continuous Learning, Embracing the Future”

Successfully implementing AI training and applications in a startup isn’t accomplished overnight but requires continuous learning and iteration. This transformation involves technology, processes, and culture, requiring executive commitment, full participation, and patient refinement. The good news is most successful practices show AI implementation mainly brings efficiency improvements and capability expansion rather than large-scale layoffs. The AI era curtain has just opened. The earlier teams master this “efficiency key,” the more opportunities startups have to stand out in fierce competition. Instead of watching, take action; let AI work for you, do more and better - the future is here, start now!
