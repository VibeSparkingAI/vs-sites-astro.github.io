From Frontend to Monitoring: A Practical Engineering Map for Agentic AI

Dec 2, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Introduction:

Section titled “Introduction:”

In the past two years, terms like “Agentic AI” and “AI Agent” have been overused to the point of cliché.

When many people hear “Agent,” they immediately think:

“I can build one too; just use a large model + Prompt + LangChain and that’s it.”

But when it comes to actual projects, they discover: A production-ready Agentic AI is far more than just a simple llm(prompt) call.

The “iceberg diagram” you see today lays out a practical Agentic AI technology stack for everyone to see clearly: Visible above the water are the chat interfaces and Web Apps you see; Submerged below is a comprehensive system for document processing, vector databases, retrieval, monitoring, and deployment.

In this article, I want to take you through this “Agentic AI Iceberg” from top to bottom, using plain language as much as possible.

I. Above the Water: The “Shell” You See

Section titled “I. Above the Water: The “Shell” You See”

1. Frontend: You Think It’s an “Interface,” It’s Actually an “Experience Engine”

Section titled “1. Frontend: You Think It’s an “Interface,” It’s Actually an “Experience Engine””

The diagram lists frontend frameworks like React, Next.js, and Streamlit, paired with hosting environments like Azure App Service.

Many AI projects start by just wrapping a dialog box around a model, which looks usable; But when it comes time for internal colleagues or clients to use it, problems arise:

  • Supporting multi-turn conversation history
  • Handling file uploads, result exports, and visualization
  • Implementing permission controls and organizational statistics

These all fall on the frontend + simple backend.

In short: The frontend isn’t just “writing some UI by the way”; it is the “console” for your entire Agent experience. If you don’t think this through, users will just think:

“Isn’t this just ChatGPT with a skin?”

II. The Middle of the Iceberg: The Knowledge Pipeline That Lets the Agent “Read the World”

Section titled “II. The Middle of the Iceberg: The Knowledge Pipeline That Lets the Agent “Read the World””

The real power of Agentic AI lies in “understanding your own data”. This corresponds to these layers in the diagram: Document Ingestion → Chunking/Preprocessing → Vector Representation → Vector Database → Retrieval Layer.

1. Document Ingestion: Feeding the Agent Clean “Raw Materials”

Section titled “1. Document Ingestion: Feeding the Agent Clean “Raw Materials””

Components like Microsoft Fabric, Azure AI OCR, Apache Tika, and LangChain do very plain work:

  • Uniformly extracting content from PDFs, Word docs, web pages, and screenshots
  • Removing noise, ads, and messy formatting
  • Structuring data, such as tables of contents, headings, paragraphs, and tables

Think of it this way:

To feed a bodybuilder, you must first ensure “real meat and vegetables,” not junk food.

If you just throw a bunch of raw PDFs at the model and expect it to be your “enterprise search/knowledge assistant,” you are basically playing with fire.

2. Chunking & Preprocessing: Cutting Knowledge into Bite-Sized Pieces for the Model

Section titled “2. Chunking & Preprocessing: Cutting Knowledge into Bite-Sized Pieces for the Model”

Tools like spaCy, Hugging Face, LangChain, and some capabilities of Azure AI Search in the diagram are there to help you do this:

  • Intelligent chunking by paragraph or logical structure
  • Identifying entities and topics, doing some lightweight NLP preprocessing
  • Some also add metadata tags (e.g., source system, time, business line)

What happens if this step isn’t done well?

  • Either the chunk is too large: super long context, expensive and inaccurate
  • Or the chunk is too fragmented: the model sees a pile of “debris” each time, answering irrelevantly

A common engineering approach is to create a dynamic chunking strategy based on document structure + word count + semantics, paired with overlapping windows to ensure “no key information is missed in a query”.

3. Embeddings: Compressing Enterprise Knowledge into “Vector Fingerprints”

Section titled “3. Embeddings: Compressing Enterprise Knowledge into “Vector Fingerprints””

This is where Embedding models like Azure OpenAI, OpenAI, and Cohere come into play.

Simply put, they help you convert:

Text → High-dimensional Vector

The distance between vectors represents “semantic similarity.”

Why is this step critical?

Because questions in an enterprise basically look like this:

  • “Help me check the reasons for the abnormal return rate of a certain product in 2023?”
  • “List the differences in contract terms between these versions?”

No one searches by exact original text; everyone asks “randomly in natural language,” and the Agent has to rely on semantic retrieval to quickly find the truly relevant content in your knowledge base.

4. Vector Database: A “Gym-Specific Hard Drive” for the Agent

Section titled “4. Vector Database: A “Gym-Specific Hard Drive” for the Agent”

The diagram lists Azure AI Search, Cosmos DB, PostgreSQL + vector plugins, as well as Milvus, FAISS, etc.

In the real world, you basically have to consider a few things:

  • When the data volume is large, can retrieval still maintain millisecond-level response?
  • Does it support hybrid search (vector + keyword + filter conditions)?
  • How are security and isolation handled, such as sharding by tenant, department, or project?

Here you are actually choosing your “Agent Memory Hub”. Choose wrong, and all your subsequent “RAG + Agent” capabilities will be stuck on performance and cost.

5. Retrieval Layer: Let the Agent “Look Up Before Thinking”

Section titled “5. Retrieval Layer: Let the Agent “Look Up Before Thinking””

This layer is what everyone calls RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation):

  • LangChain, LlamaIndex, and Haystack do retrieval orchestration
  • Azure AI Search provides built-in retrieval + ranking capabilities

You can design a fixed routine for the Agent:

User asks → Retrieve N pieces of related knowledge → Let the large model answer based on “evidence” → Return citations to the user when necessary

If this step is done well, the Agent’s “hallucination rate” and “nonsense” will drop by an order of magnitude.

III. Prompt Engineering Layer: From “Writing Prompts” to “Designing Strategies”

Section titled “III. Prompt Engineering Layer: From “Writing Prompts” to “Designing Strategies””

The diagram includes Prompty, LangChain, and DSPy.

Many people’s understanding of Prompts is stuck at “thinking of a few good English descriptions.”

But Prompts in Agentic AI are closer to a type of:

“Decision Strategy + Workflow Orchestration Language”

They can be roughly divided into categories:

  • System Prompts: Define role, tone, boundaries
  • Tool Invocation Strategy: When to check the knowledge base, when to call an API
  • Multi-Agent Collaboration: How the planning, execution, and review roles talk to each other

Tools like DSPy even try to “automatically optimize Prompts”, somewhat like treating Prompts as “parameters,” tuning them through datasets and feedback signals.

Only when you start managing Prompts as “experimental, iterative product configurations” rather than just scribbling them down, will the ceiling of large model applications be opened.

IV. LLM Layer: Not “Choosing a Model,” But “Choosing a Brain Combo”

Section titled “IV. LLM Layer: Not “Choosing a Model,” But “Choosing a Brain Combo””

The diagram mentions Azure OpenAI, plus models like LLaMA and Mistral.

In real projects, using a single model for everything is no longer very realistic; more often, it’s about making a “model platter”:

  • Cheap models run most chitchat and simple tasks
  • Specialized models run vertical tasks like code, law, medicine
  • Enterprises will also mix open-source models + commercial models to balance privacy and cost

You can understand this layer as:

The Agent’s “Multi-Core Brain Pool”

It has to be responsible for those “Prompt strategies” and “retrieval results” above, making decisions that are as reliable and consistent with business logic as possible.

V. Monitoring & Evaluation: If an Agent Isn’t Monitored, It Slowly Becomes a “Time Bomb”

Section titled “V. Monitoring & Evaluation: If an Agent Isn’t Monitored, It Slowly Becomes a “Time Bomb””

The diagram shows Azure Foundry, OpenTelemetry, and Grafana.

A pitfall many teams fall into is:

“The app is online, people can use it, so it’s a success.”

A few weeks later you’ll find:

  • Users often complain “the answer this time is different from the last time”
  • After a model upgrade, results in some scenarios get worse, but no one notices
  • Certain Prompts are called tens of thousands of times a day, costs blowing straight through the budget

At this point, Observability and Evaluation become critical:

  • Record the input, output, tools used, latency, and cost of every call
  • Establish an automatic evaluation set for key scenarios, running comparisons as soon as a new version comes
  • Hang these metrics on a dashboard like Grafana for operations and tech to monitor together

In short:

An Agent without monitoring is like a car without rearview mirrors. The faster you drive, the more dangerous it is.

VI. Infrastructure & Deployment: Turning the Agent from a “Lab Toy” into a “Productivity Tool”

Section titled “VI. Infrastructure & Deployment: Turning the Agent from a “Lab Toy” into a “Productivity Tool””

The bottom layer contains things like Azure Container Apps, AKS, Docker, and Kubernetes.

The problem they solve is really just two words: Stability.

  • Withstanding high concurrency
  • Meeting high availability requirements (can’t keep crashing)
  • Supporting multiple environments (dev, test, staging, prod)

Many people think everything is fine when doing a Demo: “It runs pretty fast locally.”

But in a production environment, you have to consider:

  • How to do horizontal scaling and auto-scaling?
  • How to split model services and business services?
  • How to isolate and audit sensitive data?

These things are the engineering details at the very bottom of the iceberg. The sooner you realize their existence, the less rework you’ll have later.

VII. A Landing Route Suggestion for You Who Want to Build Agentic AI

Section titled “VII. A Landing Route Suggestion for You Who Want to Build Agentic AI”

If you are currently in a company and planning to build an “Intelligent Customer Service,” “Internal Knowledge Assistant,” or “AI Operations Assistant,” you can think about the route like this:

  1. Start with a clear scenario For example, “allow operations to instantly query any historical campaign data,” rather than “make an Agent that can chat about anything.”

  2. Run a MVP with the simplest frontend + RAG pipeline Don’t use complex multi-Agent scheduling at the start. Being able to do: “Ask business question → Check knowledge base → Provide cited answer” is already very powerful.

  3. Leave hooks for monitoring and evaluation from the very beginning Even if you only do simple logging + a few typical question sets at first, it will save a lot of time for tuning later.

  4. Treat this iceberg diagram as a “Technical Debt List” Think clearly:

    • Which layers must be done now
    • Which layers can be added gradually later
    • Which layers can use cloud vendor/open-source solutions to “borrow for a while”

Wait until you make the first version of the Agent indispensable to the team’s daily routine, then slowly dig down into this iceberg, and you will find:

The truly hard part is not “knowing how to use large models,” but “being able to embed large models into business, running stably, for a long time, and affordably.”

Conclusion: Agentic AI Is Not a New Term, But a Whole New Engineering Paradigm

Section titled “Conclusion: Agentic AI Is Not a New Term, But a Whole New Engineering Paradigm”

Back to that diagram.

Above the water are the “cool AI applications” users see; Below the water is a comprehensive engineering system from data and models to monitoring and deployment.

If you are currently a developer, product manager, or are considering using AI to improve business efficiency, I highly recommend saving this “Agentic AI Iceberg Diagram” as a long-term reference engineering map.

Next time you are urged to make “an AI Agent Demo,” you can throw this diagram at them and say with a smile:

“We can first make the part visible above the water, but to run in production, the whole iceberg below will have to be filled in sooner or later.”

What do you think, in your current work/project, which layer of this iceberg is the hardest? Welcome to chat in the comments section about the most “outrageous” AI project requirements you have encountered.

AI’s Golden Tipping Point: 10% Penetration and Industry Reshaping

Sep 12, 2025
Vibe Sparking

1. Key Takeaways at a Glance

Section titled “1. Key Takeaways at a Glance”
  • 9.7% enterprise AI adoption ≈ about to cross the 10% threshold → moving from the “trial phase” to the “scale-up phase”
  • Technology diffusion follows an S‑curve: the 10%→50% band is often the “golden period” for investment and value realization
  • AI will diffuse faster than past mainstream waves (cloud / smartphones / SaaS), driven by reusable foundation models, API ecosystems, compute-as-a-service, and agentic development
  • Leading industries share: high data density + frequent decisions + large automation surface
  • AI is shifting from an “efficiency tool” to a “strategic nerve center” and “growth lever”
  • Challenges concentrate around: algorithmic bias, data compliance, moat reconstruction, talent and skills redefinition

2. Why the Inflection Now?

Section titled “2. Why the Inflection Now?”

Goldman Sachs reports that by August 2025, US enterprise AI adoption reached 9.7% (approaching the 10% threshold). Historically, this marks the moment when the “early majority” starts to enter at scale:

  1. Follower risk perception declines (validated cases + falling costs)
  2. Budgets shift from “pilots” to “integration / process re‑engineering”
  3. Org structures and governance evolve (AI PM / AI Ops / data risk & controls)

Rule of thumb: as penetration advances from ~10% to 50%, compound growth is steepest and both valuation elasticity and industry dividends expand in tandem.

Section titled “Lower barriers: the learning curve is reshaped”

    • Generative models are pattern matchers, not causal reasoners.
    • Weak on causal logic and cross‑domain insight.
    • On complex reasoning and novel exploration, they often struggle.

Shortcut thinking creates plateaus. Many people ask AI for answers instead of thinking. Short‑term, progress looks fast; soon they hit a ceiling and get surpassed by those who kept deliberate practice.

Red: shortcut‑seekers plateau early; black: independent learners build lasting momentum.

Bottom line: mastery is still hard. AI lowers “entry difficulty,” but not “summit difficulty.” Real understanding, innovation, and experience still require human effort.

The Hidden Ticket Behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini: Why Big Models Work

Aug 20, 2025
Vibe Sparking

From small to massive: an old “law” breaks

Section titled “From small to massive: an old “law” breaks”

Five years ago, proposing to train a trillion‑parameter network sounded insane.

Conventional wisdom said: small models underfit; large models memorize and fail to generalize — the classic bias–variance trade‑off. In short, simple models miss patterns; overly complex ones memorize noise and bomb on new data.

Think of teaching a child addition. Two strategies: learn the carry rules, or memorize every problem and answer.

The latter yields perfect homework scores but collapses on new formats — like overfitting: stellar train accuracy, poor test performance.

So for years we obsessed over regularization and parameter parsimony. Big models were dismissed as overfit by default.

Reality surprised us. The very large models once deemed impossible now shine — from conversational AI to protein prediction — riding the wave of scale.

Double descent: a turning point

Section titled “Double descent: a turning point”

In 2019, researchers defied the warning labels and kept scaling models even after they reached 100% train accuracy.

The shock: test error went down again.

Train error fell, rose (overfit), then test error dropped sharply once more as parameters grew — a phenomenon dubbed “double descent.”

With repeated evidence across CNNs, ResNets, Transformers, “bigger is better” gained ground. Yet one question lingered:

If bigger should overfit more, why do giant models get smarter?

The secret weapon: the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis

Section titled “The secret weapon: the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis”

“Large networks contain many randomly initialized subnetworks. During training, the best ‘winning tickets’ get amplified while others fade.”

In 2018, MIT researchers found that inside a large neural net, there exist sparse subnetworks that can match the full model’s performance even after pruning ~96% of parameters.

The twist: they only work that well if you keep the original random initialization. Re‑initialize them and the magic disappears.

Hence the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis:

Large models succeed not because they learn a baroque solution, but because with enough parameters, they contain many candidate subnetworks — some happen to be highly aligned with the task. Training “draws tickets,” reinforcing the winners while suppressing the rest.

This reconciles double descent with classical theory:

Big models aren’t just memorizing; they discover simpler explanations in a vast parameter space. Occam still holds — via a subtler mechanism.

Brains and LLMs: convergent patterns

Section titled “Brains and LLMs: convergent patterns”

Human brains have ~86B neurons and quadrillions of synapses — absurdly over‑parameterized on paper — yet we generalize.

Viewed through the lottery‑ticket lens, redundancy offers a huge pool of candidates from which effective, simple patterns can be selected. Intelligence is less rote memory, more “finding structure among many possibilities.”

This also clarifies why LLMs thrive.

Buy more tickets, increase your odds. Giant language models contain countless randomly initialized subnetworks; training elevates those “born good at language.” As scale grows, more winning tickets emerge, enabling in‑context learning, reasoning, and other capabilities.

The lesson: dare to go past “common sense”

Section titled “The lesson: dare to go past “common sense””

AI researchers accidentally scratched a jackpot that shifted the field.

The bias–variance rule wasn’t overturned; it operates differently in huge parameter spaces. Large networks aren’t cheating — they follow the rules at a higher level.

Practice, then theory. Pushing beyond the comfort zone of theory yielded milestone progress. In a math‑heavy field, some breakthroughs come from embracing uncertainty.

Closing

Section titled “Closing”

There’s no magic behind ChatGPT’s success.

Give models a big enough stage and they shed redundancy, converging on elegant solutions.

It’s a simple idea that reshapes how we think about learning.

In life, too, stepping past the safe line can reveal the next elegant surprise.

Hedge AI Risk: Why Internet Workers Should Hold Nvidia Instead of Worrying About Pay Cuts and Layoffs

Jul 28, 2025
Vibe Sparking

The internet era had near-zero marginal costs for “information flow”; the AI era has persistently high marginal costs for “information generation” that scale linearly with usage. Resources shift from “people” to “computing power + electricity,” as companies accelerate from light-asset tertiary industries toward “secondary industrialization.” As internet workers, we should upgrade our knowledge and skills with one hand, while using asset allocation (like Nvidia) to hedge the risk of dilution in the value chain with the other.

TL;DR

Section titled “TL;DR”
  1. Fundamental Change: PC/Mobile Internet = low marginal cost “information distribution”; AI = high marginal cost “information generation.”
  2. Company Profile: OPEX→CAPEX, talent→GPU, electricity, data centers, internet companies becoming “industrialized.”
  3. Personal Strategy: Career shifts from “writing features” to “managing engineering and computing economics”; Assets use “AI upstream leaders + diversified ETFs” to hedge personal cash flow risk (not investment advice).

Recent Headlines

On July 2, 2025, according to WIRED magazine, Mark Zuckerberg offered top research talent compensation packages worth up to $300 million (approximately 21.8 billion RMB) over four years for his newly established Meta Super Intelligence Lab, with first-year total compensation exceeding $100 million (approximately 727 million RMB).

1. Three Fundamental Differences: PC/Mobile Internet vs. AI Era

Section titled “1. Three Fundamental Differences: PC/Mobile Internet vs. AI Era”

① Information Method: From “Information Flow” to “Information Generation”

Section titled “① Information Method: From “Information Flow” to “Information Generation””
  • Past: The internet delivered existing content faster, broader, cheaper to users (search, social, video).
  • Present: AI regenerates text, images, code, speech, etc. for each user request, consuming computing power and electricity with every generation.

② Marginal Cost: From Near Zero to Persistently High

Section titled “② Marginal Cost: From Near Zero to Persistently High”
  • Internet: Once network and CDN infrastructure is built, distribution marginal cost approaches zero.
  • AI: Training isn’t “one-time,” requiring annual iterations + continuously increasing investment; inference is pay-per-call, higher usage means higher costs. Even if hardware iterations bring “better performance at same price,” stronger models will consume the saved costs (trading experience for cost).

③ Usage Scale: From “Population/Device Ceiling” to “Unlimited Automation”

Section titled “③ Usage Scale: From “Population/Device Ceiling” to “Unlimited Automation””
  • Internet: Active users constrained by device and population ceilings.
  • AI: Agents/automation/autonomy remove “human usage” limits — machine-to-machine calls can amplify 7×24×N times, with demand side having exponential growth potential.

2. New Profile of Internet Companies in the AI Era

Section titled “2. New Profile of Internet Companies in the AI Era”

① Rising Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) with “Annual Renewals”

Section titled “① Rising Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) with “Annual Renewals””
  • Training: Large model infrastructure isn’t one-time, hardware depreciation cycles are short, replacing every 2-3 years.
  • Inference: Each call generates costs, revenue grows with usage, computing bills grow synchronously.
  • Financial Side: Shift from emphasizing OPEX (R&D salaries) to emphasizing CAPEX (GPUs, data centers, electricity).

② Resource Scarcity Reversal: From “Engineer Time” to “GPU + Electricity + Data Centers”

Section titled “② Resource Scarcity Reversal: From “Engineer Time” to “GPU + Electricity + Data Centers””
  • Internet era moats were network effects and near-zero distribution costs;
  • Current moats increasingly come from computing scale, power capacity, site selection and cooling, cluster scheduling and operational experience.

③ Industry Attribute Switch: Tertiary Industry → Secondary Industrialization

Section titled “③ Industry Attribute Switch: Tertiary Industry → Secondary Industrialization”
  • Internet companies increasingly resemble traditional industrial enterprises: heavy assets, long cycles, strong maintenance, engineering-focused.
  • Talent Structure evolution: From large-scale SDE software development engineers → Pre-training / Foundation Model and other “computing management” roles.

3. Crisis and Opportunity in the AI Era

Section titled “3. Crisis and Opportunity in the AI Era”

① Physical Constraints: GPUs and Electricity

Section titled “① Physical Constraints: GPUs and Electricity”
  • Power capacity and grid access are hard constraints; data center differences in location, electricity prices, cooling determine unit computing costs.
  • Result: Computing expansion isn’t purely “throwing money,” but a systematic capability competition of “engineering + supply chain + electricity.”

② Generation Excess: Strong Supply Side, Relatively Lagging Demand Side

Section titled “② Generation Excess: Strong Supply Side, Relatively Lagging Demand Side”
  • AI capacity increases exponentially, but human attention and purchasing power don’t match, easily creating periodic “bubbles.”
  • Solution: Let AI become “consumers” themselves — automated agents complete retrieval, ordering, coordination processes, connecting supply to real business loops.

③ Worker Reality: High-Benefit Era Contraction

Section titled “③ Worker Reality: High-Benefit Era Contraction”
  • Resources shift from “people” to “computing power,” SDEs’ marginal bargaining power gets diluted.
  • Philosophy: Collaborate with AI in a friendly, polite, efficient manner, upgrade to “engineering managers” (managing requirements, quality, data, costs, risks).
