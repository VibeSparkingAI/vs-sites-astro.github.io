MIT Research Report: Why Do 95% of AI Projects Fail? How Does Publicly Listed Company Palantir Succeed Against the Odds?

Aug 24, 2025
Vibe Sparking

A recent research report from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) reveals that 95% of generative artificial intelligence projects piloted within enterprises fail to produce substantial business value. This shocking failure rate has sparked concerns about an “AI bubble.” On the surface, companies are rushing to implement the most advanced AI models, yet most projects remain stuck in proof-of-concept stages, contributing virtually nothing to the bottom line. The root cause isn’t that AI models lack power, but rather that there exists a “learning gap” between tools and organizations—AI systems cannot integrate into enterprise workflows or access and learn from companies’ proprietary data and knowledge. In other words, failures stem from inadequate integration and application methods, not model quality.

Consider a simple example: if a retail company wants to use generative AI to decide “which products to increase in inventory,” a general large language model might offer suggestions based on public industry data. However, without access to the company’s internal, real-time, comprehensive sales and customer data, these recommendations would likely be unreliable. When data isn’t relevant and doesn’t connect to actual business operations, AI’s intelligence has nowhere to shine. Therefore, what truly determines AI project success is whether AI is applied correctly and whether enterprise data is properly utilized.

This is why a new generation of companies dubbed “enterprise AI operating systems” are playing crucial roles, bridging enterprises and AI. Among the leaders—Palantir Technologies—is renowned for successfully deploying AI projects in military, government, and large enterprise settings. Its practices demonstrate that AI can create real value once trust and engineering implementation challenges are resolved. Let’s examine how Palantir addresses the AI failure factors revealed in MIT’s report through “trust” and “engineering capabilities”.

Trust: From Culture to Ground-Level Presence, Winning Core Customer Recognition

Section titled “Trust: From Culture to Ground-Level Presence, Winning Core Customer Recognition”

Mission-Driven Culture Gains Recognition: Since its founding in 2003, Palantir has carried an extraordinary sense of mission. The company emerged from post-9/11 counter-terrorism needs, with co-founders Peter Thiel and Alex Karp driven not merely by profit, but by the conviction to “use data to safeguard national security.” This corporate culture emphasizing social responsibility and values helped Palantir gain trust endorsements from government and military sectors early on. For instance, the CIA’s venture capital arm In-Q-Tel invested in Palantir in 2004, helping refine products for intelligence analysis. Palantir’s commitment to data privacy and security is deeply embedded in the company’s DNA—co-founder Karp, with his legal and philosophical background, emphasized from the beginning the importance of incorporating protections for civil liberties and privacy into software. This steadfast commitment has gradually built Palantir’s “reliable and secure” brand image, earning recognition from highly sensitive customers like government and military agencies.

On-Site Collaboration Builds Trust: Palantir understands that large traditional institutions often have complex IT architectures and rigid processes, creating significant resistance to new technology adoption. To address this, Palantir pioneered the “Forward Deployed Engineer (FDE)” model, directly stationing excellent software engineers at customer frontlines. These engineers work side-by-side with customer teams, deeply understanding business pain points and providing hands-on assistance in implementing Palantir software. Practice has proven that this embedded collaboration significantly accelerates AI system integration with customers’ existing systems. As one analysis noted: “By deploying engineers with autonomous decision-making authority on-site and focusing on customer value during integration, Palantir enables traditional enterprises to deploy software much faster than when they attempt it themselves.” Through this shoulder-to-shoulder collaboration, customer doubts and issues can be identified and resolved in real-time, while Palantir earns customer trust through concrete actions. Reports indicate that Palantir has even sent engineers with laptops to Afghanistan’s frontlines to provide on-site data analysis support for U.S. military units. This approach of sparing no cost for personalized service makes customers genuinely feel Palantir’s commitment to project success.

Rigorous Security Safeguards Trust: For customers like military and government agencies that highly prioritize data confidentiality, data security and compliance auditing are primary considerations when selecting AI solutions. Palantir stands as an industry benchmark in this regard. Its platform incorporates “privacy by design” principles from inception, providing a comprehensive suite of fine-grained security controls including granular access control (ensuring users only see necessary data), operational audit logs (recording all data access and operations for traceability), data minimization (using only necessary data for analysis), and anonymization/pseudonymization processing. Palantir also pioneered “Purpose-Based Access Control (PBAC)” mechanisms that can audit “who accessed what data under what circumstances and why” at any time. This rigorous security framework enables even the most data-demanding institutions like the U.S. Department of Defense, intelligence agencies, and the UK’s National Health Service (NHS) to confidently entrust critical data to Palantir’s platform. By solving the difficult balance of “security and AI,” Palantir has gained trust from many traditional customers who were previously skeptical of commercial software. In a recent interview, a U.S. State Department official praised Palantir as an “outstanding American company,” expressing pride in collaborating with them to “modernize diplomatic work.”

Real Case Studies Build Reputation: Trust ultimately comes from actual track records. Palantir has proven its reliability and capability through challenging projects—this is key to winning customer hearts. For example, during the War on Terror, Palantir’s analytical platform helped U.S. forces identify and destroy hidden improvised explosive device (IED) networks: it correlated vast amounts of phone records, suspicious location data, vehicle movements, and past attack data, revealing enemy operational patterns to soldiers. A frontline commander sincerely praised it as “the only software that actually works on the battlefield”. Palantir’s software reportedly also assisted in planning the 2011 operation to eliminate Osama bin Laden and was used to coordinate complex logistics during the 2021 U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan. These successful applications in real scenarios greatly enhanced customer trust in Palantir—after all, systems tested by fire are more reassuring. Additionally, in the UK, Palantir’s data integration platform for the NHS became a cornerstone in fighting COVID-19. The Foundry platform consolidated nationwide pandemic data, medical supplies, and vaccination information, for the first time allowing frontline hospitals, NHS headquarters, and government decision-makers to share the same “single source of truth”, ensuring timely delivery of ventilators and protective equipment while supporting the UK’s most efficient vaccination program. Each outstanding performance in critical missions accumulated “trustworthy” reputational assets for Palantir, forming the foundation for its continued expansion in military, government, and enterprise markets.

Engineering Capabilities: From On-Site Development to Productized Delivery, Solving Real Problems

Section titled “Engineering Capabilities: From On-Site Development to Productized Delivery, Solving Real Problems”

Gaining trust is just the first step; to make AI truly effective requires solid engineering implementation capabilities. Palantir excels in this area as well, with unique methods and strengths.

Immediate Customization Solves Problems: Traditional enterprise software projects often face “last mile” challenges—systems are purchased but not used effectively. However, through the aforementioned FDE model, Palantir stations excellent engineers long-term at customer sites, enabling real-time software adjustments and custom feature development based on actual needs. These engineers are both technically proficient and business-savvy; when they discover that certain Palantir platform functions don’t align with customer processes, they can immediately write code for fixes or configure new data models. For example, forward deployed engineers have worked beside production personnel at Airbus aircraft assembly lines, optimizing Foundry platform analytical models; many Palantir engineers even operate permanently in “air-gapped” environments without internet connections, supporting highly classified data systems. This agile, embedded engineering service resolves issues on-site without lengthy feedback iteration cycles. To enterprises working with Palantir, it’s as if they’ve gained an on-call super IT team. No wonder analysis points out that compared to enterprises fumbling through development themselves, working with professional teams like Palantir accelerates software integration much faster.

Connecting Core Data Pathways: For AI to be intelligent, “provisions”—data—must come first. Palantir’s strength lies precisely in helping large institutions integrate and utilize scattered core business data, providing high-quality fuel for AI models. Its flagship platform Foundry can automatically extract data from numerous isolated business systems, clean and transform it into unified analytical models. Previously, internal enterprise projects often took months to organize data from different sources, but with Palantir reportedly this can begin analysis in just days. “Reducing 3 months of data preparation work to 3 days”—this represents the dramatic efficiency gains from Palantir’s engineering capabilities. For example, in manufacturing, Airbus leveraged Palantir Foundry for deep analysis and optimization of production process data, successfully significantly reducing A350 aircraft final assembly time. In finance, JPMorgan Chase used Palantir’s platform early on to strengthen anti-fraud risk controls, integrating transaction and customer data scattered across departments to help meet stricter compliance requirements post-financial crisis. These cases demonstrate Palantir’s exceptional ability to connect “data silos”, linking enterprise critical system data pathways, thereby enabling AI applications built on solid, rich internal data foundations. MIT’s report indicates that most AI projects stagnate because generic tools cannot adapt to enterprises’ unique processes and data. Palantir precisely fills this gap through powerful data integration, deeply embedding AI into enterprise operational fabric.

Productization Empowers the Whole: Notably, Palantir is not a consulting company but a product company. It hasn’t fallen into the trap of “customizing completely different systems for each customer,” but rather achieves solution standardization through technology abstraction and reuse. Palantir’s internal R&D teams extract common functionalities developed in each frontline project and incorporate them into their universal platform products. Early on, Palantir focused on government intelligence, creating the Gotham platform; later they distilled data integration capabilities accumulated in military and government projects into the enterprise platform Foundry, enabling large enterprises to use Palantir-level data operating systems out-of-the-box. This strategy essentially transforms individual custom projects into repeatedly deliverable modules, maintaining adaptability to complex business problems while improving efficiency when delivering to new customers. According to industry analysis, Palantir precisely “rolls” technologies with commonalities into its globally unified platform, gradually transitioning from “engineer crowd tactics” to “product-as-a-service” models. This way, new customers require fewer “forward deployed engineers” and less time for deployment (though initial guidance is still needed), while Palantir’s platform capabilities continuously enhance universality. This productization mindset enables Palantir to serve more customer needs while ensuring high-standard quality control. Simply put, Palantir not only knows how to fight one battle, but also summarizes tactics into strategic handbooks, winning subsequent battles faster.

By simultaneously focusing on both “trust” and “engineering capabilities,” Palantir’s AI project success rate far exceeds industry averages, becoming one of the few positive cases breaking the “95% failure curse.” MIT’s report corroborates this: surveys show that rather than going it alone, companies collaborating with professional AI vendors like Palantir have success rates about three times higher. Those companies skilled at leveraging external AI platforms with deep customization and implementation are often among the few success stories achieving AI value realization.

Conclusion: From Failure Lessons to Future Paradigms

Section titled “Conclusion: From Failure Lessons to Future Paradigms”

When we look back at those 95% failed AI projects, it’s clear that Palantir’s principles directly address those lessons: use appropriate scenarios (focus on pain points rather than blindly chasing trends), data is king (integrate internal data rather than relying only on general models), human-machine collaboration (involve frontline personnel rather than parachuting in black-box tools), and long-term evolution (accumulate common products rather than one-time assemblies). Palantir has proven through real combat that for AI to create value in enterprises, technical strength is certainly important, but more critical is earning trust and deep integration. As CEO Karp puts it: “AI alone can’t give you answers; the key lies in human-AI collaboration.”

For those worried about the AI bubble, Palantir’s successful experience provides a shot in the arm: truly valuable AI implementation isn’t a pipe dream, provided we face those “invisible foundational tasks”—building trust, integrating data, transforming processes, and refining products. Only by addressing these shortcomings can AI’s magic be unleashed and transformed into tangible business results and social value. In the future, as more enterprises adopt such paradigms, we have reason to expect that today’s 95% failure rate will eventually be reversed, and AI will no longer be a mirage but become a solid engine driving transformation across all industries.

AI Raises the Floor, Not the Ceiling

Aug 20, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Introduction: Raising the floor vs. lifting the ceiling

Section titled “Introduction: Raising the floor vs. lifting the ceiling”

In the AI era we often hear: AI is great at “raising the floor,” but not so good at “lifting the ceiling.” In plain terms, AI dramatically lowers entry barriers across fields, helping more people get started or complete baseline work faster. But breaking human ceilings — reaching the peak of creativity and expertise — still depends mostly on us.

This floor‑up, ceiling‑unchanged pattern shows up differently across domains. Below, we examine how AI lowers learning and work barriers, why pushing the frontier remains hard, and compare effects across coding, writing, creative arts, and design.

Lower barriers: the learning curve is reshaped

Section titled “Lower barriers: the learning curve is reshaped”

The “resource fit” problem is fading. Before AI, learners struggled to find material that matched their specific background and level. For example:

  • You’re new to a field but have adjacent experience — few tutorials bridge the two.
  • You don’t know the prerequisites, so you grind inefficiently.
  • After reaching intermediate, you hit a plateau and can’t find targeted resources.

With AI, this improves dramatically. AI adapts to you and customizes content on demand: no matter how basic or niche your question, AI can respond at your level and even handle repetitive practice. The early learning slope flattens — the “first mile” gets much easier.

The green curve shows progress with AI: beginners cross the baseline faster than the black curve (without AI). As mastery deepens, the curves converge — AI helps less near the frontier.

Typical AI benefits:

  • Always‑on tutor for questions;
  • Example code and cases that blast through the newbie wall;
  • Offloads repetitive work so you can focus on understanding.

Developers feel this acutely: instead of trawling docs for a new framework, you ask AI and get explanations with code. As some joke, “AI knows every rookie mistake — like Stack Overflow on steroids.”

Studies also show novice programmers complete tasks ~55% faster with tools like GitHub Copilot — and the less experience you have, the bigger the boost.

It’s not just learning — work barriers drop too. Tasks like coding, copywriting, and basic modeling now produce “good‑enough” outputs from newcomers using AI tools. More people can join knowledge work. This is “raising the floor”: lifting the default starting level.

Why the ceiling is hard to lift: cognition and data limits

Section titled “Why the ceiling is hard to lift: cognition and data limits”

Experts are more sober about AI. The deeper you dig, the more you find its limits.

Two main reasons:

  1. Training‑data constraints

    • Common topics → reliable answers.
    • Frontier/deep topics → sparse data, higher error and vagueness.
    • Contested topics → conflicting data, shallow synthesis.
    • Net effect: AI tends toward averaged answers, not breakthrough insight.

  2. Cognition and reasoning bottlenecks

    • Generative models are pattern matchers, not causal reasoners.
    • Weak on causal logic and cross‑domain insight.
    • On complex reasoning and novel exploration, they often struggle.

Shortcut thinking creates plateaus. Many people ask AI for answers instead of thinking. Short‑term, progress looks fast; soon they hit a ceiling and get surpassed by those who kept deliberate practice.

Red: shortcut‑seekers plateau early; black: independent learners build lasting momentum.

Bottom line: mastery is still hard. AI lowers “entry difficulty,” but not “summit difficulty.” Real understanding, innovation, and experience still require human effort.

Domain snapshots: uneven impact

Section titled “Domain snapshots: uneven impact”

Coding: novices fly, complex systems still need veterans

Section titled “Coding: novices fly, complex systems still need veterans”
  • Upside: Faster ramp on languages/frameworks; productivity up.
  • Limits: Lacks system‑level context on large projects; weaker solution quality.
  • Risk: Skipping fundamentals weakens independent problem‑solving.

Writing: first drafts are easy; great work still takes work

Section titled “Writing: first drafts are easy; great work still takes work”
  • Upside: Blazing fast on copy, summaries, newsy content.
  • Limits: Style converges; originality is scarce.
  • Take: AI is a second‑draft assistant; distinct voice is human.

Creative arts: mimicry for all; novelty is rare

Section titled “Creative arts: mimicry for all; novelty is rare”
  • Upside: Anyone can generate decent illustrations or music.
  • Limits: True novelty remains scarce.
  • Pattern: Trendy styles spike, but don’t replace enduring originals.

Design: smarter tools; taste still rules

Section titled “Design: smarter tools; taste still rules”
  • Upside: AI features in Photoshop/Figma cut ops overhead.
  • Limits: Brand, UX, and tradeoffs are judgment calls.
  • Trend: Designers shift toward “AI curator” and “creative director.”

Closing: opportunity and challenge, person‑dependent

Section titled “Closing: opportunity and challenge, person‑dependent”

AI raises the floor — it brings more people onto the stage of knowledge and creativity. But lifting the ceiling is still on us — AI offers a higher starting point, not a higher endpoint.

  • Learners: AI accelerates, but effort compounds.
  • Creators: AI assists, but originality is scarce.
  • Industries: AI lowers gates, but the peak remains rare.

In a world where everyone has AI, the real gap is wisdom in how we use it.

The Hidden Ticket Behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini: Why Big Models Work

Aug 20, 2025
Vibe Sparking

From small to massive: an old “law” breaks

Section titled “From small to massive: an old “law” breaks”

Five years ago, proposing to train a trillion‑parameter network sounded insane.

Conventional wisdom said: small models underfit; large models memorize and fail to generalize — the classic bias–variance trade‑off. In short, simple models miss patterns; overly complex ones memorize noise and bomb on new data.

Think of teaching a child addition. Two strategies: learn the carry rules, or memorize every problem and answer.

The latter yields perfect homework scores but collapses on new formats — like overfitting: stellar train accuracy, poor test performance.

So for years we obsessed over regularization and parameter parsimony. Big models were dismissed as overfit by default.

Reality surprised us. The very large models once deemed impossible now shine — from conversational AI to protein prediction — riding the wave of scale.

Double descent: a turning point

Section titled “Double descent: a turning point”

In 2019, researchers defied the warning labels and kept scaling models even after they reached 100% train accuracy.

The shock: test error went down again.

Train error fell, rose (overfit), then test error dropped sharply once more as parameters grew — a phenomenon dubbed “double descent.”

With repeated evidence across CNNs, ResNets, Transformers, “bigger is better” gained ground. Yet one question lingered:

If bigger should overfit more, why do giant models get smarter?

The secret weapon: the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis

Section titled “The secret weapon: the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis”

“Large networks contain many randomly initialized subnetworks. During training, the best ‘winning tickets’ get amplified while others fade.”

In 2018, MIT researchers found that inside a large neural net, there exist sparse subnetworks that can match the full model’s performance even after pruning ~96% of parameters.

The twist: they only work that well if you keep the original random initialization. Re‑initialize them and the magic disappears.

Hence the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis:

Large models succeed not because they learn a baroque solution, but because with enough parameters, they contain many candidate subnetworks — some happen to be highly aligned with the task. Training “draws tickets,” reinforcing the winners while suppressing the rest.

This reconciles double descent with classical theory:

Big models aren’t just memorizing; they discover simpler explanations in a vast parameter space. Occam still holds — via a subtler mechanism.

Brains and LLMs: convergent patterns

Section titled “Brains and LLMs: convergent patterns”

Human brains have ~86B neurons and quadrillions of synapses — absurdly over‑parameterized on paper — yet we generalize.

Viewed through the lottery‑ticket lens, redundancy offers a huge pool of candidates from which effective, simple patterns can be selected. Intelligence is less rote memory, more “finding structure among many possibilities.”

This also clarifies why LLMs thrive.

Buy more tickets, increase your odds. Giant language models contain countless randomly initialized subnetworks; training elevates those “born good at language.” As scale grows, more winning tickets emerge, enabling in‑context learning, reasoning, and other capabilities.

The lesson: dare to go past “common sense”

Section titled “The lesson: dare to go past “common sense””

AI researchers accidentally scratched a jackpot that shifted the field.

The bias–variance rule wasn’t overturned; it operates differently in huge parameter spaces. Large networks aren’t cheating — they follow the rules at a higher level.

Practice, then theory. Pushing beyond the comfort zone of theory yielded milestone progress. In a math‑heavy field, some breakthroughs come from embracing uncertainty.

Closing

Section titled “Closing”

There’s no magic behind ChatGPT’s success.

Give models a big enough stage and they shed redundancy, converging on elegant solutions.

It’s a simple idea that reshapes how we think about learning.

In life, too, stepping past the safe line can reveal the next elegant surprise.

The 10× Designer Playbook in the AI Era: Curate, Write Clearly, Ship Fast, Tell Stories

Aug 19, 2025
Vibe Sparking

For anyone building with AI: upgrade from “drawing screens” to “designing systems.” From “I can produce” to “I can judge.” Trade single‑point skills for a compound skillset.

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

Recently, Lovable designer Felix Haas published the “New Designer Playbook”: a way to reach 10× output in the AI wave. The core is not “how many models you can use,” but how you judge, express, and turn ideas into shippable products.

This handbook blends Felix’s public notes with Lovable’s official practices, adapted into a growth path any product role can execute.

Speed‑read (for the busy)

Section titled “Speed‑read (for the busy)”
  • Curate > Create: AI can generate 100 options; your value is which to pick, why, and how to polish.
  • Words are leverage: clear goals, constraints, and acceptance criteria set the ceiling of AI output.
  • Systems > Screens: design journeys, data flows, permissions, and state machines together.
  • Ship to learn: small steps, ship fast, validate while going.
  • Know a bit of tech: APIs, DBs, auth, hosting — at least stitch a runnable product.
  • Work with AI: not competing, but using AI to amplify taste and judgment.
  • Tell stories: tie problem, human value, and evidence; stickier products (see method below).

1. Curatorial sense: from “can make” to “can choose”

Section titled “1. Curatorial sense: from “can make” to “can choose””

Image/text/UI generation is easy; judgment is hard. Build your own “taste rules + anti‑patterns”:

  • Create a pattern library: components, whitespace, hierarchy, motion you admire.
  • Create an anti‑pattern library: bad palettes, noisy info, anti‑human flows.
  • Use the three questions in review: Is it clearer? More credible? More actionable? Curatorial design helps you consistently choose right among AI’s abundance.

2. Writing: make AI truly understand you

Section titled “2. Writing: make AI truly understand you”

Clarity ships faster. Recast prompts into Goal – Constraints – Process – Acceptance:

  • Goal: for whom, to complete what, faster/with less friction.
  • Constraints: brand tone, palette, component library, compatibility, budget.
  • Process: user flow first, then information architecture, then screens.
  • Acceptance: success metrics, A/B KPIs, usability tasks. Lovable’s “Prompting Handbook” formalizes such structured prompts for stable output.

3. Systems thinking: design “runnable products,” not pages

Section titled “3. Systems thinking: design “runnable products,” not pages”

Plan journeys, states, permissions, data flow, errors & recovery in one pass; screens are the expression layer.

  • Start with an event flow: trigger → validate → write/rollback → notify.
  • Define a state machine: draft, pending, failed, success, archived.
  • Unify design tokens and voice so teams don’t drift. Lovable’s “Best Practices / From idea to app” breaks “concept → runnable” into repeatable moves.

4. Speed: learning via shipping

Section titled “4. Speed: learning via shipping”

Delivery speed = learning speed.

  • Slice work into 24‑hour shippable increments: solve one narrow problem.
  • Use fake data + real flows first; wire in the real backend when metrics justify.
  • Weekly retro + hygiene: delete dead weight, codify what worked. In PM practice, Lovable stresses: AI lowers implementation barriers — strategy and validation become the core.

5. Tech fluency: don’t be an engineer, but do assemble

Section titled “5. Tech fluency: don’t be an engineer, but do assemble”

For non‑devs, master this minimum viable tech list:

  • APIs: read Swagger, call REST/GraphQL.
  • Databases: schema, PK/FK, basic indexes.
  • Auth & access: login, roles, rate limits.
  • Hosting: deploy, logs, rollback. Lovable’s guides take you step‑by‑step from first command to full‑stack runnable.

6. Storytelling: make the product more human

Section titled “6. Storytelling: make the product more human”

Frame it as a three‑act story:

  • Character: who is struggling?
  • Conflict: why is the current way painful?
  • Transformation: with your product, what becomes lighter? Close with evidence: metrics, quotes, comparative demos. That “human flavor” turns features into a brand. (Felix frequently stresses “co‑creating with AI to amplify humanity and taste.”)

7. Work with AI: become the “chief designer”

Section titled “7. Work with AI: become the “chief designer””
  • Use AI for rough work: options generation, style fillers, usability sweeps.
  • Keep judgment: you own direction, constraints, acceptance.
  • Teach AI you: encode your style and taboos into templates and system prompts. As Felix puts it: “The best don’t compete with AI; they work alongside it.”

7‑day practice plan (copyable)

Section titled “7‑day practice plan (copyable)”

Day 1: write a Goal–Constraints–Acceptance prompt template and try your go‑to models. Day 2: build 20 good/bad examples with one‑line rationales. Day 3: upgrade a page into a system diagram (journey/state/permission/data). Day 4: ship a runnable slice on Lovable. Record perf/usability. Day 5: write a 300‑word three‑act product story with two evidence points. Day 6: align API contracts with engineering; add error codes and rollback plans. Day 7: ship + retro: delete one ineffective element; codify one rule that worked.

Tooling (official resources)

Section titled “Tooling (official resources)”
  • Lovable platform & docs: from Figma import to full‑stack shipping.
  • Best Practices / Tips & Tricks: avoid common pitfalls and build team norms.
  • Prompting Handbook: structured prompting, automation, and debugging.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

AI lets anyone “produce,” but judgment, expression, and making it real are scarce. With curation, writing, systems, speed, technical basics, and storytelling, you’re no longer “someone who draws,” but someone who, with AI, lands value.

① Information Method: From “Information Flow” to “Information Generation”

Section titled “① Information Method: From “Information Flow” to “Information Generation””
  • Past: The internet delivered existing content faster, broader, cheaper to users (search, social, video).

② Marginal Cost: From Near Zero to Persistently High

Section titled “② Marginal Cost: From Near Zero to Persistently High”
  • Internet: Once network and CDN infrastructure is built, distribution marginal cost approaches zero.
  • AI: Training isn’t “one-time,” requiring annual iterations + continuously increasing investment; inference is pay-per-call, higher usage means higher costs. Even if hardware iterations bring “better performance at same price,” stronger models will consume the saved costs (trading experience for cost).

③ Usage Scale: From “Population/Device Ceiling” to “Unlimited Automation”

Section titled “③ Usage Scale: From “Population/Device Ceiling” to “Unlimited Automation””
  • Internet: Active users constrained by device and population ceilings.
  • AI: Agents/automation/autonomy remove “human usage” limits — machine-to-machine calls can amplify 7×24×N times, with demand side having exponential growth potential.

Section titled “① Rising Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) with “Annual Renewals””
  • Training: Large model infrastructure isn’t one-time, hardware depreciation cycles are short, replacing every 2-3 years.
  • Inference: Each call generates costs, revenue grows with usage, computing bills grow synchronously.
  • Financial Side: Shift from emphasizing OPEX (R&D salaries) to emphasizing CAPEX (GPUs, data centers, electricity).

② Resource Scarcity Reversal: From “Engineer Time” to “GPU + Electricity + Data Centers”

Section titled “② Resource Scarcity Reversal: From “Engineer Time” to “GPU + Electricity + Data Centers””
  • Internet era moats were network effects and near-zero distribution costs;
  • Current moats increasingly come from computing scale, power capacity, site selection and cooling, cluster scheduling and operational experience.

Section titled “③ Industry Attribute Switch: Tertiary Industry → Secondary Industrialization”
  • Internet companies increasingly resemble traditional industrial enterprises: heavy assets, long cycles, strong maintenance, engineering-focused.
