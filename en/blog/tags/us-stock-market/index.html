Decoding the AI Chip Wars: Why Google TPU Won't Kill NVIDIA

Dec 7, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Decoding the Real AI Chip War

Section titled “Decoding the Real AI Chip War”

Lately the market’s favorite storyline goes like this:

“Google TPU is rising, NVIDIA is about to be taken out; AWS has Trainium, Microsoft has Maia, in-house ASICs will push GPUs off the throne.”

But if you look at Morgan Stanley internet analyst Brian plus semiconductor analyst Joe Moore’s kind of “inside view,” and cross-check the public data, a picture closer to the industry’s real logic emerges:

This isn’t about “who replaces whom”—it’s “if it can compute, we want it.” In the short run it’s compute scarcity; in the long run profits concentrate with the few who control standards and cost.

I’ll frame this article around four questions:

  1. Have we really entered an “infinite compute era”?
  2. Whose normal is multi-architecture coexistence—the giants, or the whole industry?
  3. Google has TPU, so why can’t it ditch NVIDIA?
  4. How should regular investors choose in this “everyone is building chips” melee?

Along the way I’ll weave in:

  • Fact checks on each point (at least 3 professional sources cross-verified),
  • Logic breakdowns,
  • And some perspective on “who is selling what story” from the standpoint of incentives and human nature.

I. Compute Flood: “Never Enough” or “Shortage for Now”?

Section titled “I. Compute Flood: “Never Enough” or “Shortage for Now”?”

1. How much of a “flood”? Start with some hard numbers

Section titled “1. How much of a “flood”? Start with some hard numbers”

(1) Data center CapEx is exploding

  • Dell’Oro reports: in 2024 Q2, global data center capital expenditure rose 46% YoY, driven mainly by deployments of AI accelerator servers (GPU / TPU / in-house accelerator cards).
  • Same firm later tallied: full-year 2024 global data center CapEx jumped to $455 billion, up 51%, with 2025 still expected to post double-digit growth, powered chiefly by AI accelerator servers.

These numbers are basically saying:

“Everyone is spending on data centers at a historically rare speed—and it’s specifically for AI.”

(2) On the GPU side: NVIDIA as an “income black hole”

  • NVIDIA’s latest earnings: FY2025 Q4 (through 2025-01-26) revenue of $39.3 billion for the quarter, +78% YoY, mainly from data center.

  • Industry estimates: in FY2025 Q3, NVIDIA’s data center segment alone topped $50 billion in a single quarter, with gross margin near 75%.

  • For the “data center GPU” market by itself, research firms estimate: about $10.5 billion in 2025, with potential to expand to $77 billion by 2035, >22% CAGR.

(3) Blackwell’s real state: not “no one wants it” but “nowhere near enough supply”

NVIDIA management repeatedly stressed across late 2024 and 2025 earnings calls:

  • Hopper and Blackwell platforms are both supply-constrained;
  • Blackwell demand is expected to “exceed supply” for multiple consecutive quarters in FY2026, with much of next year’s capacity already pre-booked.

Most research reports and media agree:

High-end AI GPUs (H100/H200/Blackwell) are in “line up and rationing” mode.

So your original line:

“The game isn’t about chip architectures, it’s about whether you can get cards”

is consistent with public data in the near-term 1–3 year window.

2. But “compute is forever scarce” is a feeling, not a law of physics

Section titled “2. But “compute is forever scarce” is a feeling, not a law of physics”

That nuance matters for investors.

(1) Physical and infrastructure constraints

  • AI data centers aren’t just about buying cards; you also need:

    • Power: large AI facilities routinely draw hundreds of megawatts;
    • Copper and materials: analyses show AI data centers use 27–33 tons of copper per MW. At current project plans, existing and in-progress copper mines would cover only about 70% of demand by 2035, with a potential 300,000-ton shortfall as soon as 2025.

These constraints are a reminder:

“Infinite compute” is a demand-side wish—the real world applies brakes via the grid, materials, and permits.

(2) Cash flow and shareholder constraints

  • Cloud providers now spend tens to hundreds of billions of dollars annually on AI infrastructure,
  • But shareholders ask: “How fast do these GPUs/TPUs pay back? Are AI products earning enough?”

If over the next two to three years “big-model monetization” lags market expectations, CapEx growth will inevitably fall from 50%+—just like any prior infrastructure cycle (4G, cloud, fiber).

Bottom line on this point:

  • Facts:

    • Right now we truly are in a phase of compute scarcity + acute high-end GPU shortage;

  • Opinion:

    • “Compute is forever insufficient” is an emotion amplified;

  • Investment meaning:

    • Short term you can justify valuations with “card-hoarding logic”;
    • Medium to long term you must watch when power / materials / ROI start cutting growth—the three blades of the scissors.

II. “Whoever has compute, we use”: Multi-architecture Is a Survival Move, Not a Pretty Choice

Section titled “II. “Whoever has compute, we use”: Multi-architecture Is a Survival Move, Not a Pretty Choice”

Your second takeaway was:

“Right now I’ll use any compute I can get. NVIDIA, AMD, in-house ASICs, TPU, even the next cheap chip—I’ll buy.”

Let’s see what parts of that line up with reality and which are privileges of the very top tier.

1. The facts: top players truly are running “multi-architecture”

Section titled “1. The facts: top players truly are running “multi-architecture””

(1) The hyperscaler structure

Multiple research shops (Kearney, MLQ.ai, etc.) in 2025 reports note:

  • The data center AI accelerator market is still dominated by NVIDIA,
  • But Google TPU, AWS Trainium, Microsoft Maia and other in-house chips are expected to reach 15–20% internal deployment share in the late 2020s (mostly their own workloads).

In other words:

  • For Google/AWS/Microsoft and peers:

    “GPU + in-house ASIC mix” is already written into the roadmap as reality.

(2) How specific vendors do it

  • Google Cloud: AI Hypercomputer

    • On one side it’s rolling out the new Trillium TPU (6th gen),
    • On the other it explicitly says in the official blog it will include NVIDIA Blackwell platforms (B200 / GB200) as part of Hypercomputer, letting customers pick between TPUs and NVIDIA GPUs.

  • AWS: Trainium + NVIDIA GPU sold side by side

    • At re:Invent 2024/2025, AWS launched Trainium2/3/4 in-house AI chips and UltraServer, pitched as “cheaper training + inference compute”;

    • Meanwhile, its compute-track tech blog stresses:

      “Our accelerated compute lineup includes both GPU instances and AWS AI chip instances; customers choose based on cost / performance / compatibility.”

1. Executive Summary

Section titled “1. Executive Summary”

With notable gains in both nominal and real wages, the probability of a Bank of Japan (BOJ) rate hike as early as October is rising. Beyond its domestic significance for Japan’s cycle, this shift can catalyze a reversal in yen-funded carry trades, with meaningful spillovers to global markets.

Section titled “2. Core Drivers and Logic of Japan’s Policy Normalization”

2.1 The wage–inflation virtuous cycle is forming

Section titled “2.1 The wage–inflation virtuous cycle is forming”
  • Past inflation was largely imported (energy shocks, weak JPY), leaving real wages negative for an extended period.
  • In 2024 “Shunto”, nominal wage growth reached ~5.1%, the highest since 1991. Inflation dynamics are shifting from exogenous to endogenous (labor shortages, structural transition).
  • A self-reinforcing wage–price cycle gives the BOJ cover to exit ultra-easy policy.

  • In March 2024, the BOJ ended negative rates and YCC, delivering the first hike in 17 years (-0.1% → 0–0.1%).
  • Given growth and external uncertainty, the BOJ then proceeded cautiously, holding rates steady for a period.
  • As the Fed potentially pivots dovish, BOJ tightening would narrow the U.S.–Japan rate differential — a key trigger for global capital reallocation.

Section titled “3. Reversal of Yen Carry Trades: The Transmission Mechanism”

3.1 Mechanics and scale

Section titled “3.1 Mechanics and scale”

Yen carry trade: borrow low-yielding JPY, convert to higher-yielding currencies, and deploy into overseas risk assets. This trade has been large and long-lived.

Broad scale is roughly $9.3T, including:

  • Japanese residents’ foreign securities investment: ~$5.4T
  • Non-residents borrowing JPY to buy Japanese equities: ~$2.1T
  • Non-residents borrowing JPY to buy overseas securities: ~$1.8T

When the U.S.–Japan rate spread narrows due to BOJ hikes, carry attractiveness falls and losses can trigger unwind: sell overseas assets (e.g., U.S. stocks and Treasuries) and buy back JPY. Even small proportional flows may meaningfully impact prices. If U.S. dollar weakness coincides with BOJ hikes, the speed and scale of the unwind can be amplified.

Section titled “4.1 U.S. Treasuries: weaker demand and upward yield pressure”

Transmission channels:

  • Direct: Higher Japanese rates lift the appeal of JGBs and domestic assets, reducing overseas allocation (including USTs).
  • Indirect: Stronger JPY and appreciation expectations raise FX hedging costs, further discouraging USD asset holdings.

Combined with expanding U.S. fiscal supply (rising debt and deficits), a weaker demand base — with Japan among the largest foreign holders — points to structural upward pressure on U.S. long-end yields. Recent JGB volatility has already transmitted to higher EUR and USD long-end yields, highlighting cross-market contagion.
