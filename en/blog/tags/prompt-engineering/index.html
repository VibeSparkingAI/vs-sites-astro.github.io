Conquering Complexity with Add & Divide: The 80/20 Truth of Generative AI

Aug 17, 2025
Vibe Sparking

The big victory of generative AI boils down to two small things: addition and division. Add one more time-honored rule of the game: 80/20.

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

We often mystify AI. Crack open a mainstream generative model and its most common moves are downright plain: weighted sums (addition), normalization (division), and residual addition (addition). Attention is a weighted sum; softmax and normalization use division; layer normalization divides by the standard deviation; residual networks add inputs directly to outputs. These “brick-laying” operations somehow stack into today’s miracles.

Why does such simplicity work? The answer points to two ideas: low-dimensional manifolds + 80/20. The world looks high-dimensional and chaotic, yet the patterns that actually occur are squeezed onto a few “main roads.” If you catch that 20% of main roads, you can explain 80% of phenomena.

I. The “Add/Divide” Aesthetics of Models

Section titled “I. The “Add/Divide” Aesthetics of Models”

Attention = weighted sum + normalization In Transformers, the attention output is a weighted sum over V; the weights come from softmax(QK^T / √d_k), which hides a division (scaling). It’s “who resembles me → how much weight → add it up.”

Layer normalization = subtract mean + divide by std LayerNorm computes mean and variance, then divides by the standard deviation to stabilize feature distributions and ease training.

Residual connections = direct addition ResNet’s “shortcut” adds the input as-is to the output, preserving information and smoothing gradients so very deep networks are trainable.

Diffusion models = a loop of adding noise / removing noise During training we gradually add Gaussian noise; during generation we denoise in reverse. At heart it’s linear combinations and normalization again.

Naively simple? Yes. But when data lies along a low-dimensional main road, these linear/near-linear “add–subtract–multiply–divide” moves are enough to sketch that road.

II. Why the World Respects “Simple Methods”: Low-D Manifolds + 80/20

Section titled “II. Why the World Respects “Simple Methods”: Low-D Manifolds + 80/20”

The manifold hypothesis says that although natural data lives in high-dimensional space, effective samples concentrate on a low-dimensional manifold. Many studies find that learned representations in deep nets have intrinsic dimensionality far below the surface dimensionality—the model is compressing the world.

Enter 80/20: When the critical structure occupies “few dimensions,” grabbing the vital 20% of features can reproduce 80% of the phenomena. This isn’t mysticism; power-law/Pareto patterns are frequent guests in nature and human systems.

There’s also an industry consensus: neural network scaling laws show that as you scale model, data, and compute, the loss falls along a power law. Simple architectures + large-scale “add–subtract–multiply–divide” can steadily eat into more complex patterns.

We can visualize this with a metaphor: imagine standing on a vast prairie (a high-dimensional space). In theory you can walk in any direction, but in practice most people travel along a few clear trails. Footsteps aren’t random; together they stamp out a handful of paths favored by nature. Those trails are the “manifolds of natural preference.” Weather changes, protein folding, and image generation are similar—although the possibility space is enormous, real situations compress into low-dimensional regions.

III. A Hard-Core Case: How AlphaFold “Harvests the Manifold”

Section titled “III. A Hard-Core Case: How AlphaFold “Harvests the Manifold””

The theoretical conformation space of proteins is absurdly large—on the order of 10^300—so brute force is impossible. Yet nature folds proteins in milliseconds, implying the true folding path is compressed onto a low-dimensional main road. AlphaFold learned this road from vast sequence–structure pairs, then locates the most likely structure directly in that “road space.”

The upgraded AlphaFold 3 even folds in a diffusion architecture, using a differentiable “add noise/denoise” pipeline to jointly predict complexes, ions, and small molecules—again, a simple operations assembly line serving low-dimensional structure.

Some liken it to “inferring a few plastic leaves and mirrors from a kaleidoscope pattern.” In plain terms: compress the complex surface into a small set of stable structures.

IV. Operationalizing: Use 80/20 to Thin and Deepen Your Generative Workflow

Section titled “IV. Operationalizing: Use 80/20 to Thin and Deepen Your Generative Workflow”

1) Data side: prioritize “main road” samples

  • Review 30 days of real tasks and tally the top 20% scenarios/styles by frequency; create high-quality samples and templates for them first.
  • Build your eval set with Pareto coverage: max out the core 80% first, then expand to the long tail. (A Pareto chart makes this vivid.)

2) Prompting side: make “add/divide” concrete

  • Addition: provide explicit weighted factors (goal, audience, style, length, banned terms). Pair each factor with examples—this feeds “weights” to attention.
  • Division: norms and constraints are “division”—length caps, structure templates, term glossaries, scoring rubrics all scale/normalize the model’s output.
  • Pattern: a handful of high-quality few-shot examples > a pile of fuzzy cases (they act as anchors to the “main road”).

3) Retrieval/RAG: put your corpus on a diet

  • Only ingest the top 20% highest-weight documents (authoritative, freshest, widest coverage).
  • Keep each retrieval small and sharp with top-k, and force cite-and-link positions to reduce “off-road” wandering.

4) Evaluation and launch: two stages

  • Phase A: hammer on reliability across the 80% high-frequency main roads (auto-scoring + light human review).
  • Phase B: tackle the long tail by iterating in order of impact × frequency.

V. Don’t Forget the Boundary Conditions

Section titled “V. Don’t Forget the Boundary Conditions”

Simple is strong, not omnipotent.

  • Domain shift/long tail: models learn “this main road.” Out-of-distribution (OOD) they stumble; you need new data/constraints to pave a new road. Proteins are similar—certain classes or disordered regions remain tough.
  • Power laws aren’t laws of nature: 80/20 is a common empirical pattern; don’t apply it mechanically.
  • “Information-first” ontology is debated: treating physics as information-first is tempting, but credible counter-arguments exist. Don’t canonize it.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

If you see generative AI as a crew doing addition/division, guided by a low-dimensional road map, many thorny problems suddenly get simpler. Compress first, then orchestrate; first the 20%, then the 80%. That’s today’s blunt method—and the steadiest kind of cleverness.

What do you think? In your projects, which 20% of scenarios most deserve to be “thickened”? Share in the comments.

Why this series is worth your time

Section titled “Why this series is worth your time”

  1. It teaches “how to write,” not just concepts. The official Prompt Engineering Overview clarifies which problems to solve with prompting vs. model choice/architecture, and it provides directly reusable structures.

  2. Learn‑by‑doing interactive lessons. Anthropic’s interactive course splits techniques into short, focused chapters: clarity, role setup, separating data from instructions, output formats, reasoning steps, example‑driven prompting, and more — each with quick exercises.

The five core moves

Section titled “The five core moves”

  1. Be as clear as onboarding a new teammate. Spell out goal, output format, audience, and evaluation criteria. Use bullet points — don’t rely on inference.

  2. Make roles concrete. “You are a B2B PM writing a weekly update to your VP. ≤200 words. End with 3 actions.” The more specific, the steadier the results.

  3. Separate instructions from data. Fence off “requirements” and “materials” with delimiters. Claude misreads less when inputs are cleanly separated.

  4. Provide good examples. One to three high‑quality examples instantly align structure and style — more effective than abstract requirements.

  5. Leave room for thinking. Ask for step‑by‑step reasoning / think‑then‑answer / justification. Complex tasks become noticeably more accurate.

AI Prompt Writing Technique: Two-Stepping Method - Generate First, Then Optimize

Aug 15, 2025
Vibe Sparking

First, let AI complete the task normally; then in the same prompt, have it immediately summarize, polish, refactor, or self-check the just-produced content. One prompt, double value.

Two-Stepping has been continuously promoted by creator Rob Lennon in public posts and courses, adopted by many content and product teams. Its core is the sequential thinking of “generate first, optimize later.”

Why Does the “Two-Step Method” Work So Well?

Section titled “Why Does the “Two-Step Method” Work So Well?”

1) Reduces cognitive load. Cramming too many requirements at once causes the model to get distracted, pulled in different directions, and conflicted. Breaking it into “do the work first, then review” is more stable. This approach aligns with academia’s self-feedback → rewrite approach: first produce a draft, then have the model provide feedback and revise accordingly, showing significant improvements across multiple tasks (research reports show improvement ranges of 5%–40%).

2) Lets the model “see itself” and then optimize. After the model writes out its thoughts, it has a “criticizable” object; reviewing based on the finished product often yields higher quality. Similar two-stage processes commonly seen in research: first produce then answer/review, also proven to be more stable.

3) Solves “output + processing” in one prompt. Stringing “write → revise → compress → style conversion” into the same prompt reduces round trips and maintains context consistency. Rob Lennon’s Two-Stepping concept and course practice also emphasize breaking complex tasks into executable sequential steps.

Applicable Scenarios (The More “Processing-Heavy,” the Better)

Section titled “Applicable Scenarios (The More “Processing-Heavy,” the Better)”
  • Content Creation: Long article → 50-word lead / 3 key points / multi-platform adaptation.
  • Product & Documentation: PRD → risk checklist / review questions / acceptance criteria.
  • Programming Collaboration: Generate function → unit tests + edge cases + self-check report.
  • Data Analysis: Full analysis → conclusion table / decision recommendations / retrospective points.
  • Marketing Growth: Ad script → objection handling version / A/B copy / platform adaptation.

The underlying logic aligns with “Self-Refine”: the generate—feedback—regenerate loop, proven to steadily improve result quality.

Ready-to-Use: 4 “Two-Step Method” Super Templates (Copy & Paste)

Section titled “Ready-to-Use: 4 “Two-Step Method” Super Templates (Copy & Paste)”

Universal Version (Works for Any Task)

First, complete the task according to the following requirements: – Goal: {your desired result} – Audience: {who will see it} – Constraints: {length/style/forbidden elements} – Output format: {structure/fields} Then, based on the content you just output, do secondary processing: – Extract 3–5 key points + 1 golden sentence – Point out 3 areas for improvement and provide the complete improved version – Provide a “shorter, more conversational” version (≤ {word count})

Content Creation Version (Long Article → Title Matrix + Social Media Cards)

First write an article about {topic}, including: introduction/3–5 sections/conclusion/call to action. Then output secondary results:

  1. 10 convertible titles (with emotional words/numbers/promises),
  2. 50-word lead + 3 key points,
  3. Generate 2 social media posts for {platform}.

Programming Version (Function → Unit Tests + Self-Check Report)

First implement {function/module}, requirements: readability, exception handling, complete comments. Then: – Generate high-coverage unit tests (list edge cases and expected results) – Provide complexity assessment and 3 refactoring suggestions, with improved code – Output a “self-check report” (defects, risks, improvement points)

Analysis & Decision Version (Insights → Decision-Ready)

First complete analysis of {data/solution/competitors}, output structured conclusions. Then provide: – Decision overview (TL;DR 100 words) – 3 recommendations for leadership with implementation checklist (owner, milestones, metrics) – Possible objections and counterargument points

Tip: Treat the “then” part as “editor/QA role description” – whatever role you’re playing, the model will improve toward that standard. This approach aligns with “two-stage prompting/self-feedback iteration” research conclusions.

Real-World Examples (Close to Your Daily Work)

Section titled “Real-World Examples (Close to Your Daily Work)”

1) WeChat Article Production Pipeline

  • First write 1200–1500 word main text (with subheadings, bold text).
  • Then produce 50-word lead, 3 key points, ending CTA, 10 title alternatives, one-sentence social media post.
  • Result: One prompt, complete draft + publishing materials.

2) Frontend Feature Implementation

  • First write a date picker component.
  • Then auto-generate unit tests, list edge cases (timezone/leap year), with complexity and refactoring suggestions.
  • Result: Code + tests + review feedback all-in-one.

3) PRD to Task Breakdown

  • First output complete PRD.
  • Then produce deliverables list, milestones, risks & responses, acceptance criteria.
  • Result: PRD becomes plan, team can start immediately.

Precautions (Pitfall Checklist)

Section titled “Precautions (Pitfall Checklist)”
  • Prevent “sandbagging”: Sometimes the model deliberately writes the first version poorly to “significantly improve” in step two. Key point: Set hard metrics (length, format, constraints) in step one, and require “both versions must be directly usable”.
  • Important tasks suggest true two-step: Split into two messages for more stability, especially for compliance, contracts, risk control scenarios; this aligns with multi-round iteration experience in self-refine research.
  • Role switching: Clearly state in step two “you are editor/reviewer/QA” and provide standards.
  • Control length & format: Provide word limits/table fields to avoid going off-topic.
  • Observability: Require “point out what changed” for easy review and comparison.
  • Don’t rely entirely on it: Content involving timeliness/facts still needs your external verification.

Advanced: Turn “Two-Step Method” into Team “Buttons”

Section titled “Advanced: Turn “Two-Step Method” into Team “Buttons””
  • Make into Snippets/Slash commands: /two-step-content, /two-step-code, /two-step-prd.
  • Preset styles & restrictions: Add “Disallowed/Allowed” blocks in prompts for consistent stable output.
  • Bind with review SOP: Make “step two” always output key points + improvements + actionable checklist.

Summary

Section titled “Summary”

The essence of Two-Stepping is packaging “doing the work” and “doing the review” into delivery. It balances speed and quality, suitable for content, product, development, analysis, and all “produce first, process later” workflows. One prompt, double harvest.

How to Effectively Tell AI What Not to Do: Positive Constraints and Structured Output for Prompts

Aug 14, 2025
Vibe Sparking

This article turns “don’t write ××” into “will reliably write √√.” I’ll first distill your six ideas into executable prompt patterns, then blend in frontline team best practices and research-backed evidence: why negative instructions (“don’t …”) often fail, and how to use hierarchical instructions and structured output to pull models back on track. Authoritative sources are cited conceptually so you can extend and reuse.

Introduction | Why does “the more you forbid, the more it appears” keep happening?

Section titled “Introduction | Why does “the more you forbid, the more it appears” keep happening?”

Many people begin with: “Don’t fabricate, don’t sound like AI, don’t write fluff.” And then what happens? The model proceeds to fabricate, gets long-winded, and gives you a “warm, upbeat” tone on top. It’s not being rebellious; rather, models are intrinsically less sensitive to negation and more likely to skip the “not” in complex language. Multiple studies and practical guidance point to the same conclusion: using positive phrasing instead of negative prohibitions yields more stable results.

Main Text

Section titled “Main Text”

1) Turn “don’t” into “do”: replace negative bans with positive constraints

Section titled “1) Turn “don’t” into “do”: replace negative bans with positive constraints”
  • Replace “don’t write ungrammatical sentences” with: “Self-check grammar and cohesion sentence by sentence and revise to fluency; when uncertain, write ‘I’m not sure’ and pause output.”
  • Replace “don’t sound like AI” with: “Use colloquial language and short sentences; keep paragraphs within four lines; provide two everyday examples before concluding.” Why it works: Research shows models have systematic weaknesses in handling negation; positive instructions reduce ambiguity and off-topic drift.

Bonus: Allowing the model to admit uncertainty significantly reduces hallucination. Hard-code this line into your prompt for a low-cost, high-return gain.

2) Set the rules before writing: put prohibitions up front and repeat them

Section titled “2) Set the rules before writing: put prohibitions up front and repeat them”
  • Structural order: [System/High-Level Instructions] → [Prohibitions/Positive Requirements] → [Task & Input] → [Output Format].
  • Repetition matters: Front-load once, remind again mid-task, and add a pre-output checklist. Enterprise-grade guides suggest you should “double down” on critical instructions to keep model attention steady in long contexts.

3) Use hierarchical instructions as a safety net: whose words are “harder” law?

Section titled “3) Use hierarchical instructions as a safety net: whose words are “harder” law?”

When instructions conflict, higher-level directives win. Place high-priority content such as style, prohibitions, compliance in the system/high-level slot, priority > task description > external materials. OpenAI’s instruction hierarchy research and practice reinforce this precedence rule. If you write the hierarchy clearly, the model is less likely to be “led astray.”

Practical crib sheet:

  • System layer: role, values, prohibitions (e.g., no privacy leaks / no fabrication)
  • User layer: this task’s goals, audience profile, scenario constraints
  • Third-party content: references, source links (readable, not to be copied) This way, precedence is clear when conflicts arise, keeping the model steady.

4) Lock down the form with structured output to reduce drift

Section titled “4) Lock down the form with structured output to reduce drift”

Instead of saying “don’t write fluff,” specify the output blueprint:

  • Ask the model to output via JSON Schema or a table, with bounded fields and lengths. Official docs from OpenAI and Google support structured output, converting “style slogans” into verifiable fields.

Example fields: tone (tone description), max_lines_per_paragraph, examples (must include concrete examples), etc. Upside: programmatic validation makes “out-of-bounds” easy to spot.

5) Provide high-quality examples so the model can “ape the cat”

Section titled “5) Provide high-quality examples so the model can “ape the cat””

Rather than “don’t be a laundry list,” give one or two positive/negative examples:

  • Positive example: short sentences, two brief stories, action-oriented takeaway.
  • Negative example: slogan-stacking, abstract nouns, no supporting data. Anthropic and OpenAI both stress that explicit exemplars materially improve hit rate.

6) Split prohibitions into two steps: produce first, then self-check

Section titled “6) Split prohibitions into two steps: produce first, then self-check”

Your fourth idea is spot on: split one-shot completion into two passes. Make it operational:

  1. Draft: generate according to the structured format.
  2. Self-check (performed by the model): compare against the prohibition checklist and style checklist; wherever unmet—fix only that part and leave the rest unchanged. This is a lightweight form of self-critique, aligned with Constitutional AI (CAI): use explicit principles and iterative self-audits to reduce boundary violations.

7) One more thing: can bans actually “ban”? Define a “style source” first

Section titled “7) One more thing: can bans actually “ban”? Define a “style source” first”

Many attempts to “ban AI-ness” fail because there’s no actionable style definition. Solution:

  • Specify audience, scenario, tone, sentence length, paragraph length;
  • Provide two sample passages you approve of as the “style source”;
  • Require citation-and-proofing (e.g., “one example every 300 words / data must have a source”). These practices are repeatedly validated in official best-practice guides.

8) The anti-hallucination trifecta (say goodbye to “don’t fabricate”)

Section titled “8) The anti-hallucination trifecta (say goodbye to “don’t fabricate”)”
  • Allow “I don’t know.”
  • Require citations and verification (extract source text first, then summarize; if nothing is found, withdraw).
  • Separate facts and opinions into columns in the output. Anthropic’s anti-hallucination guidance includes copy-ready phrasing—fold it into your long-term templates.

Copy-Ready Template | Operationalizing the six methods into an “engineering prompt”

Section titled “Copy-Ready Template | Operationalizing the six methods into an “engineering prompt””

Use cases: WeChat articles / project proposals / marketing copy Traits: high-priority bans, positive constraints, structured output, two-stage self-check

This template converts prohibitions into positive, testable, correctable constraints with a structured blueprint.

Mini case | Making “ban AI-ness” operational for content production

Section titled “Mini case | Making “ban AI-ness” operational for content production”

What not to do:

Don’t use an AI tone. Don’t write fluff. Don’t fabricate.

Engineering rewrite:

  • Style: colloquial + short sentences; ≤4 lines per paragraph; one concrete example per ~300 words.
  • Structured: { sections[].example, sections[].max_lines = 4 }
  • Anti-hallucination: allow “uncertain”; if no source found → write “none” in citations.
  • Two stages: draft → self-check (does each section include ≥1 example? are sources provided?). This aligns with official guidance on structured output and hallucination reduction, and works reliably in practice.

Conclusion | Turn “don’t” into habit

Section titled “Conclusion | Turn “don’t” into habit”

Rather than shouting “don’t,” turn “don’t” into clear, executable “do’s”: Pre-rules + positive phrasing + structured output + repeated reminders + two-stage self-check. With instruction hierarchy and permission to not know, most “the more you forbid, the more it appears” traps get paved over.

Action checklist:

  • Refine your “do-not”s into two positive rules;
  • Put them at the very top, and repeat once mid-way and once pre-output;
  • Close with JSON/table;
  • Produce first, then self-check, fixing only noncompliant parts. After that, hand the final draft to your human partner for a second pass.
