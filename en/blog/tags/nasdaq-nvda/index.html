NVIDIA’s P/E Paradox: A Deep Valuation Analysis Behind a Multi‑Trillion Run

Section titled “NVIDIA’s P/E Paradox: A Deep Valuation Analysis Behind a Multi‑Trillion Run”

At the peak of the global tech investment wave, NVIDIA stands out as the brightest star. Yet despite a market cap that has crossed $4T, its recent price action has puzzled many investors. The company delivered another quarter above Wall Street expectations, but the stock fell after hours and even flirted with a technical bear market. This “beat but drop” paradox raises a central question: is this an overreaction driven by short‑term sentiment, or the first crack in a grand narrative of overvaluation?

This article uses the price‑to‑earnings ratio (P/E) as the core lens to examine NVIDIA’s value. We look at the latest financials, trace valuation shifts over time, analyze the engines that power long‑term growth and the risks that could slow it, and outline a forward view for the next decade—offering a clear, grounded perspective for everyday investors.

Why did the stock fall after an earnings beat?

Section titled “Why did the stock fall after an earnings beat?”

On the surface, NVIDIA’s latest report was “picture‑perfect.” In FY26 Q2, total revenue reached $46.7B, up 56% year over year. GAAP net income rose 59% to $26.4B. The board also approved up to $60B in share repurchases.

So why the sell‑off? The core reason is the “expectations trap.” For a company whose share price nearly tripled over the past year, adding roughly $2T in market value, the market has become conditioned to miracle‑level growth. When expectations are super‑charged, even a small shortfall versus the most optimistic whispers can trigger disappointment.

In particular, investors fixated on the Data Center segment. Although it contributed 88% of total revenue ($41.1B) and grew strongly, it modestly missed the loftiest estimates, which some interpreted as a sign that near‑term growth may be bumping up against a ceiling. And while 56% y/y growth is extraordinary for almost any business, it was also NVIDIA’s slowest growth rate in nine quarters since the AI upcycle began.

When a company’s bar moves from “great” to “mythic,” even strong prints can disappoint. Short‑term price swings here reflect the market’s sensitivity to NVIDIA’s forward growth trajectory.

P/E as the measuring stick: history, reality, and the “overvaluation” debate

Section titled “P/E as the measuring stick: history, reality, and the “overvaluation” debate”

P/E is the simplest yardstick for what investors are willing to pay today for one dollar of a company’s earnings. High‑growth companies command higher P/Es, reflecting expectations for sustained, superior earnings expansion.

NVIDIA’s P/E evolution broadly falls into three phases:

  • Phase 1 (Pre‑AI boom): Before the generative AI surge, NVIDIA traded around 20–60x P/E, supported by strong gaming and cryptocurrency cycles.

  • Phase 2 (AI mania begins): With the rise of large language models and the “picks‑and‑shovels” narrative, NVIDIA’s P/E spiked to an all‑time high—peaking around 139x in April 2023, with some estimates running even higher—capturing the market’s euphoric sentiment.

  • Phase 3 (Back to rationality): Despite the stock’s continued rise thereafter, the P/E multiple steadily compressed—reflecting earnings growth catching up with price. The market shifted from purely “story‑driven” to increasingly “earnings‑driven.”

This transition matters. When growth is both fast and visible, multiples don’t have to be extreme to support strong returns; earnings do more of the heavy lifting as the business scales. NVIDIA’s P/E compression—alongside rising profitability—suggests a maturing narrative: from hype premium to fundamentals premium.

Section titled “What supports a premium multiple—and what could challenge it”

  • Full‑stack moat: From CUDA to networking, systems, and software, NVIDIA’s integrated stack creates high switching costs and developer lock‑in.

  • Geopolitics: Export controls and supply chain restrictions may cap upside in specific regions or product lines.
  • Competition: AMD (MI series), custom silicon from hyperscalers, and potential vertical integration can pressure share and pricing power.
  • Supply‑demand balance: If AI infrastructure spend normalizes faster than expected, revenue growth and gross margins could decelerate.
  • Valuation risk: If the market concludes that hyper‑growth is not sustainable, multiples can revert quickly.

Long‑term view through the P/E lens

Section titled “Long‑term view through the P/E lens”

Consensus expects NVIDIA’s P/E to continue normalizing as earnings compound: roughly the mid‑40s in FY26, low‑30s in FY27, and mid‑20s by FY28, assuming high double‑digit to strong‑double‑digit earnings growth.

This shift suggests a transition from a company priced mainly on narrative to one anchored in durable profitability—a “tech blue chip.” Investors should expect fewer sudden multiple expansions and more return driven by steady earnings growth.

Ten‑year scenarios

Section titled “Ten‑year scenarios”

— Bull case: A new industrial wave of AI. If NVIDIA defends its CUDA moat, executes the Blackwell roadmap, and AI infrastructure spending becomes a multi‑trillion market, P/E can sustain in the 30–40x range while earnings growth drives continued share appreciation—more “Microsoft‑like.”

— Base case: A powerful but cyclical super‑cycle. Geopolitics and competition pressure margins and share, slowing growth and compressing P/E toward 20–30x, with more volatility and cyclicality.

— Bear case: Bubble deflates. AI ROI disappoints, geopolitical headwinds worsen, and China exposure erodes. Earnings underwhelm, P/E contracts sharply, and the stock retraces materially.

For a high‑valuation, high‑volatility leader like NVIDIA, investing is ultimately a judgment on long‑term fundamentals—not short‑term price moves. Keep an eye on:

  • Gross margin: Can GM hold ~70%+, signaling pricing power and platform moat?
  • Geopolitics: Track U.S.–China policy developments and revenue exposure.
