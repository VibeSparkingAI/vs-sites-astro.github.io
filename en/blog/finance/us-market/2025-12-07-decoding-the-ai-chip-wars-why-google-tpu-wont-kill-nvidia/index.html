Decoding the AI Chip Wars: Why Google TPU Won't Kill NVIDIA

Dec 7, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Decoding the Real AI Chip War

Section titled “Decoding the Real AI Chip War”

Lately the market’s favorite storyline goes like this:

“Google TPU is rising, NVIDIA is about to be taken out; AWS has Trainium, Microsoft has Maia, in-house ASICs will push GPUs off the throne.”

But if you look at Morgan Stanley internet analyst Brian plus semiconductor analyst Joe Moore’s kind of “inside view,” and cross-check the public data, a picture closer to the industry’s real logic emerges:

This isn’t about “who replaces whom”—it’s “if it can compute, we want it.” In the short run it’s compute scarcity; in the long run profits concentrate with the few who control standards and cost.

I’ll frame this article around four questions:

  1. Have we really entered an “infinite compute era”?
  2. Whose normal is multi-architecture coexistence—the giants, or the whole industry?
  3. Google has TPU, so why can’t it ditch NVIDIA?
  4. How should regular investors choose in this “everyone is building chips” melee?

Along the way I’ll weave in:

  • Fact checks on each point (at least 3 professional sources cross-verified),
  • Logic breakdowns,
  • And some perspective on “who is selling what story” from the standpoint of incentives and human nature.

I. Compute Flood: “Never Enough” or “Shortage for Now”?

Section titled “I. Compute Flood: “Never Enough” or “Shortage for Now”?”

1. How much of a “flood”? Start with some hard numbers

Section titled “1. How much of a “flood”? Start with some hard numbers”

(1) Data center CapEx is exploding

  • Dell’Oro reports: in 2024 Q2, global data center capital expenditure rose 46% YoY, driven mainly by deployments of AI accelerator servers (GPU / TPU / in-house accelerator cards).
  • Same firm later tallied: full-year 2024 global data center CapEx jumped to $455 billion, up 51%, with 2025 still expected to post double-digit growth, powered chiefly by AI accelerator servers.

These numbers are basically saying:

“Everyone is spending on data centers at a historically rare speed—and it’s specifically for AI.”

(2) On the GPU side: NVIDIA as an “income black hole”

  • NVIDIA’s latest earnings: FY2025 Q4 (through 2025-01-26) revenue of $39.3 billion for the quarter, +78% YoY, mainly from data center.

  • Industry estimates: in FY2025 Q3, NVIDIA’s data center segment alone topped $50 billion in a single quarter, with gross margin near 75%.

  • For the “data center GPU” market by itself, research firms estimate: about $10.5 billion in 2025, with potential to expand to $77 billion by 2035, >22% CAGR.

(3) Blackwell’s real state: not “no one wants it” but “nowhere near enough supply”

NVIDIA management repeatedly stressed across late 2024 and 2025 earnings calls:

  • Hopper and Blackwell platforms are both supply-constrained;
  • Blackwell demand is expected to “exceed supply” for multiple consecutive quarters in FY2026, with much of next year’s capacity already pre-booked.

Most research reports and media agree:

High-end AI GPUs (H100/H200/Blackwell) are in “line up and rationing” mode.

So your original line:

“The game isn’t about chip architectures, it’s about whether you can get cards”

is consistent with public data in the near-term 1–3 year window.

2. But “compute is forever scarce” is a feeling, not a law of physics

Section titled “2. But “compute is forever scarce” is a feeling, not a law of physics”

That nuance matters for investors.

(1) Physical and infrastructure constraints

  • AI data centers aren’t just about buying cards; you also need:

    • Power: large AI facilities routinely draw hundreds of megawatts;
    • Copper and materials: analyses show AI data centers use 27–33 tons of copper per MW. At current project plans, existing and in-progress copper mines would cover only about 70% of demand by 2035, with a potential 300,000-ton shortfall as soon as 2025.

These constraints are a reminder:

“Infinite compute” is a demand-side wish—the real world applies brakes via the grid, materials, and permits.

(2) Cash flow and shareholder constraints

  • Cloud providers now spend tens to hundreds of billions of dollars annually on AI infrastructure,
  • But shareholders ask: “How fast do these GPUs/TPUs pay back? Are AI products earning enough?”

If over the next two to three years “big-model monetization” lags market expectations, CapEx growth will inevitably fall from 50%+—just like any prior infrastructure cycle (4G, cloud, fiber).

Bottom line on this point:

  • Facts:

    • Right now we truly are in a phase of compute scarcity + acute high-end GPU shortage;

  • Opinion:

    • “Compute is forever insufficient” is an emotion amplified;

  • Investment meaning:

    • Short term you can justify valuations with “card-hoarding logic”;
    • Medium to long term you must watch when power / materials / ROI start cutting growth—the three blades of the scissors.

II. “Whoever has compute, we use”: Multi-architecture Is a Survival Move, Not a Pretty Choice

Section titled “II. “Whoever has compute, we use”: Multi-architecture Is a Survival Move, Not a Pretty Choice”

Your second takeaway was:

“Right now I’ll use any compute I can get. NVIDIA, AMD, in-house ASICs, TPU, even the next cheap chip—I’ll buy.”

Let’s see what parts of that line up with reality and which are privileges of the very top tier.

1. The facts: top players truly are running “multi-architecture”

Section titled “1. The facts: top players truly are running “multi-architecture””

(1) The hyperscaler structure

Multiple research shops (Kearney, MLQ.ai, etc.) in 2025 reports note:

  • The data center AI accelerator market is still dominated by NVIDIA,
  • But Google TPU, AWS Trainium, Microsoft Maia and other in-house chips are expected to reach 15–20% internal deployment share in the late 2020s (mostly their own workloads).

In other words:

  • For Google/AWS/Microsoft and peers:

    “GPU + in-house ASIC mix” is already written into the roadmap as reality.

(2) How specific vendors do it

  • Google Cloud: AI Hypercomputer

    • On one side it’s rolling out the new Trillium TPU (6th gen),
    • On the other it explicitly says in the official blog it will include NVIDIA Blackwell platforms (B200 / GB200) as part of Hypercomputer, letting customers pick between TPUs and NVIDIA GPUs.

  • AWS: Trainium + NVIDIA GPU sold side by side

    • At re:Invent 2024/2025, AWS launched Trainium2/3/4 in-house AI chips and UltraServer, pitched as “cheaper training + inference compute”;

    • Meanwhile, its compute-track tech blog stresses:

      “Our accelerated compute lineup includes both GPU instances and AWS AI chip instances; customers choose based on cost / performance / compatibility.”

  • Microsoft: Maia for inside, NVIDIA for customers

    • Microsoft introduced Maia 100/200 AI accelerators, clearly for internal mega-model workloads like its own and OpenAI’s;
    • At the same time Azure keeps deploying and touting the latest NVIDIA GPUs (including GB300 NVL72 clusters), with joint blogs on “extracting every drop of value from every GPU on Azure.”

All this shows:

In the very top circle, “multi-architecture coexistence” is already fact. They literally practice your line: “whoever can deliver compute, we use.”

2. But this isn’t the industry’s default—it’s a giant’s privilege

Section titled “2. But this isn’t the industry’s default—it’s a giant’s privilege”

The logical trap is:

Seeing frontier model shops run 4–5 architectures and assuming every AI company can do that.

Reality is closer to:

  • Only cloud giants / top model companies can afford to:

    • Maintain multiple hardware stacks (GPU / TPU / Trainium / Maia / other ASICs);
    • Spend engineering to port models across backends (CUDA, ROCm, XLA, in-house SDKs);
    • Build unified scheduling / compilation / MLOps layers to mask heterogeneity.

  • Most AI startups and enterprise customers behave more like:

    “As long as I can rent enough NVIDIA GPUs, I won’t deliberately tinker with other architectures.”

Ecosystem proof points:

  • CUDA still enjoys much broader framework, toolchain, tutorial, and open-source support than any other stack;
  • When third-party training/inference platforms do “multi-backend support,” they still default to NVIDIA as first priority.

So:

  • Your line “multi-architecture became the norm” is

    • True for Google/AWS/Microsoft/OpenAI/Anthropic;
    • For typical AI companies, it’s more “NVIDIA first, others as backups.”

3. Incentives: who is gaming whom behind multi-architecture?

Section titled “3. Incentives: who is gaming whom behind multi-architecture?”

Pull this back from “technical pluralism” to “incentive reality”:

  • For big model firms / cloud providers, the real motives are three:

    1. Survival: during compute shortages, “whoever has cards is your lifesaver”;
    2. Price pressure: use TPU / Trainium / Maia to lean on NVIDIA in negotiations;
    3. TCO: shift some inference loads to cheaper, more efficient ASICs to lower long-run costs.

  • For NVIDIA, the prime goal is:

    “Make CUDA + platform ecosystem the default industry standard, so even if others ship cheaper chips, migration costs stay high.”

Conclusion:

Multi-architecture isn’t “the beauty of technical diversity”— it’s a supply-chain, cost, and bargaining-power game.

III. Google Has TPU—Why Still Buy NVIDIA?

Section titled “III. Google Has TPU—Why Still Buy NVIDIA?”

Your third point hits the mark:

“Google has TPU yet keeps buying NVIDIA GPUs—TPU supplements, not replaces.”

That’s squarely in line with public info.

1. What Google itself says

Section titled “1. What Google itself says”

  • Google Cloud’s blogs and docs state plainly:

    • AI Hypercomputer includes in-house Trillium TPU,
    • And NVIDIA H200 / Blackwell (B200, GB200 NVL72) GPU instances,
    • Customers can choose different accelerator mixes as needed.

  • Many Google customer stories mention using both TPU and NVIDIA GPU:

    • Some training jobs run on TPU,
    • But latency-sensitive or CUDA-native workloads stay on GPU.

2. Why not “TPU only”?

Section titled “2. Why not “TPU only”?”

(1) Business mix differences

  • Core internal businesses (search, ads, YouTube recommendations, GWS, etc.):

    • Can be forced onto TPU on Google’s own timeline—this is “internally controlled + long-term ROI.”;

  • For the external cloud business:

    • Customers have mountains of CUDA-based code, models, and tooling;
    • If Google said “TPU only, no GPU option,” it would shove customers to AWS/Azure.

So for Google, GPU and TPU split is:

  • TPU: mass internal production + some external, focused on lowering its own long-run compute costs;
  • NVIDIA GPU: ecosystem default + customer demand, essential for keeping cloud clients.

(2) Path dependence and migration costs

Even if TPU is cheaper or more efficient for certain tasks, for most developers already “trained by CUDA”:

“Rewrite tons of code + rebuild the entire toolchain” costs more than “pay a bit of NVIDIA tax.”

3. In the bigger picture: what in-house ASICs really do

Section titled “3. In the bigger picture: what in-house ASICs really do”

Across Google TPU, AWS Trainium, Microsoft Maia public statements, a more grounded conclusion is:

  • In-house ASICs are tools for giants to “lower long-run compute costs + secure supply chain”;

  • They’re not meant to “sell so much on the open market that NVIDIA dies”;

  • They’re leverage to say at the bargaining table:

    “I’m not forced to use your cards; I can make my own—keep prices reasonable.”

So your line—“TPU is a complement, not a replacement”— holds perfectly at the industry-structure level.

IV. “Infinite Compute, Everyone Wins”?—The Retail Investor Trap

Section titled “IV. “Infinite Compute, Everyone Wins”?—The Retail Investor Trap”

Your vivid wrap-up was:

“All architectures and vendors get pulled into the same black hole—racing for time, cards, iteration speed. GPU, TPU, ASIC aren’t substituting but expanding together; the winners are anyone who can supply compute.”

The direction is right:

  • Right now “anyone who can deliver high-performance compute has business”;

But for investors, the key question is:

Business ≠ high profit, and certainly not that everyone makes big money.

1. Profit pools are highly concentrated—not “rain for all”

Section titled “1. Profit pools are highly concentrated—not “rain for all””

From current data:

  • NVIDIA:

    • Single-quarter data center revenue ~$50 billion, gross margin about 75%—classic “money-printing”.

  • Other vendors (including some GPU/ASIC makers):

    • Revenue ranges from hundreds of millions to low billions;
    • Gross margins, pricing power, and ecosystem stickiness nowhere near NVIDIA.

Consensus view:

The AI accelerator profit pool is highly concentrated in a handful of leaders; Many others have revenue and a story, but not necessarily fat margins.

For retail investors, the “everyone wins” narrative is dangerous—it nudges you to buy a basket of fringe players, while the real money may accrue only to those with standards + ecosystem + cost curve control.

2. In-house ASIC as a long-term threat—who feels it first?

Section titled “2. In-house ASIC as a long-term threat—who feels it first?”

In-house chips don’t “instantly slay NVIDIA”; they gradually reshape who captures how much profit.

  • For NVIDIA:

    • Short term: still the prime beneficiary; all clouds are still frantically buying its cards;
    • Medium/long term: once TPU/Trainium/Maia reach 15–25% internal share at each cloud, they’ll start chipping away at NVIDIA’s ultimate pricing power (the dreaded “GPU tax”).

  • For second-tier / smaller chip vendors:

    • Squeezed from above by in-house ASIC price pressure, from below by NVIDIA’s ecosystem moat;

    • They sit in the most painful spot—

      “A bit less performance, far weaker ecosystem, and not cheap enough to be irresistible,”

    • Many could be the first out in the elimination round.

So:

The bullets from in-house ASICs may not hit NVIDIA first; they may land on the tier lacking ecosystem, scale, and cost advantage.

3. Three blades against the “infinite compute” story: power, capital, regulation

Section titled “3. Three blades against the “infinite compute” story: power, capital, regulation”

One more time: compute cannot truly expand without limit:

  1. Power and materials:

    • AI data centers compete with society-wide energy infrastructure for power, transformers, copper, and cooling;
    • Long-cycle copper supply has been flagged to tighten further around 2030.

  2. Return on capital:

    • If AI app monetization lags CapEx spending,
    • Shareholders will force clouds to slow the “spend curve,” directly braking upstream chipmakers.

  3. Policy and regulation:

    • Energy use, carbon, data center siting, AI risk rules—once scaled, these become hard constraints;
    • They’re in the brewing stage now; once enacted, they immediately affect compute expansion pace.

V. How Can Individual Investors Use This?

Section titled “V. How Can Individual Investors Use This?”

To compress the above into a usable investment framework, I’d give readers a few rules:

1. Don’t get dragged by “who replaces whom” plots

Section titled “1. Don’t get dragged by “who replaces whom” plots”

  • Near term:

    • NVIDIA remains the AI compute center;
    • TPU / Trainium / Maia mostly serve giant internal needs.

  • Long term:

    • Watch not “are there new players,” but “who controls the standard (software ecosystem) and cost curve.”

2. Slice the industry into three layers—not one big stew

Section titled “2. Slice the industry into three layers—not one big stew”

Roughly split into three:

  1. Compute chip/module providers

    • NVIDIA, AMD, in-house ASICs (TPU / Trainium / Maia), some new-arch players;

    • Those likely to earn thick profits long run are the few with:

      • Ecosystem (CUDA/platform/middleware),
      • Scale (volume + supply-chain control),
      • Cost advantage (process + packaging + efficiency).

  2. Compute infrastructure providers

    • Clouds (AWS / Azure / GCP, etc.), data center operators, power and cooling solution vendors;
    • They feast on CapEx demand while bearing “payback pressure” and policy risk.

  3. Compute “upstream applications”

    • Big model firms / AI SaaS / vertical AI apps;
    • They determine whether these GPUs/TPUs turn into sustained, meaningful cash flow.

3. Flag two common pitfalls for readers

Section titled “3. Flag two common pitfalls for readers”

  1. “AI concept stocks rain on all” trap

    • Seeing everyone build chips/AI and assuming all stocks get equal upside;
    • In reality profits concentrate—bad picks hurt more than missing the sector.

  2. “Forever shortage” emotional trap

    • When everyone shouts “cards are never enough, compute is forever scarce,” ask:

      “In which year do supply/power/regulation flip from ‘shortage story’ to ‘price pressure, expansion slowdown’ reality?”

VI. One-Line Summary of the Real Industry Logic

Section titled “VI. One-Line Summary of the Real Industry Logic”

Compressing everything into one line for readers:

This isn’t a “TPU vs. NVIDIA vs. Trainium” winner-take-all; it’s a systemic war over who can control high-performance compute + software ecosystems + cost curves for the long haul.

In the short run, anyone who can reliably ship top-tier compute can gorge on the flood of demand; over the long run, profits concentrate with the very few who have ecosystem, scale, and cost advantages.

For regular investors, instead of staring at “who replaces whom” soundbites, calmly watch three things:

  1. Whose product is the industry’s “default option”? (standards & ecosystem)
  2. Who lowers cost per unit of compute fastest? (cost curve)
  3. Who can survive the post-CapEx-peak “rational return” phase? (business model & cash flow)
