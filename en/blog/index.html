Jan 15, 2026
Vibe Sparking

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

BMAD (Business Method Agent Development) represents a paradigm shift in how we approach software development and project management. It’s not just another AI assistant—it’s a comprehensive multi-agent system with 26 specialized agents, 68 registered workflows, 4 core tasks, and 655 files working in harmony.

This article takes you deep into BMAD’s architecture, revealing how its modular design enables specialized expertise across business/IT projects, game development, creative innovation, and system building.

Thinking Tools vs Making Tools

Section titled “Thinking Tools vs Making Tools”

One major shift expected in 2026 is the nature of tools themselves. All current knowledge work tools focus on execution:

  • IDEs for creating code
  • Figma for creating design
  • Spreadsheets for creating models

When it comes to tools for exploration—tools that help us think—we don’t really have any modern products outside of LLMs themselves emerging as thinking partners.

The Fundamental Problem Shift

Section titled “The Fundamental Problem Shift”

As coding agents work with increasing accuracy and longer time horizons, the hard problem moves from:

“How do I build it?” → “What do I build?”

You can imagine a near-future PM who sets broad goals for their AI and wakes up every morning to review 2-3 features the model dreamt up, executed on, and A/B tested overnight. However, current models still struggle with deciding what to build next—the ideas are bland, derivative, and lack the spark seen in truly innovative product thinking.

The Rise of Exploration-First Tools

Section titled “The Rise of Exploration-First Tools”

The spiritual successors of coding, design, and productivity tools will focus on exploration vs execution:

  • Coding tools are already leading—Cursor is furthest along
  • Antigravity is interesting as “agent-first” (exploration first) in product design

Software Eats All “Service” Functions

Section titled “Software Eats All “Service” Functions”

  • Power functions (engineering/product/performance marketing): closer to software
  • Service functions (legal/finance/HR): further from software, more human-capital levered

Two Implications of Coding Agents

Section titled “Two Implications of Coding Agents”

1. Every team should be software-first All teams (marketing, legal, procurement, finance) must reach for a software toolbox before traditional process/human systems. Options include:

  • Domain-specific products (like Harvey for legal)
  • “Bare metal” coding agents (Codex, Claude Code)

Every team should be a software team.

2. Enterprises can be dramatically more ambitious An enterprise producing software can be dramatically more ambitious in what they produce. The entire ideation/prioritization pipeline must reboot to accommodate this. Every feature that can be built will be built—most enterprises aren’t ready for this reality.

“The culture change problem will be as hard as the organizational change problem.”

  • Orchestration of cutting-edge models
  • Domain-specific UI
  • Extensive feature surface (now very cheap to build)

This is the natural implication of “Narrow Startups”—extraordinary specialization is now possible.

Why Apps Won’t Be Subsumed by Models

Section titled “Why Apps Won’t Be Subsumed by Models”

  • Complex commitments (e.g., Google’s commitments to regulators)
  • Hard prioritization problems (OpenAI simultaneously competing as consumer, enterprise, model, and hardware company)

Context Graphs: What the Ontology Debate Gets Wrong

Jan 6, 2026
Vibe Sparking

Context Graphs: What the Ontology Debate Gets Wrong

Section titled “Context Graphs: What the Ontology Debate Gets Wrong”

Source: @KirkMarple | Published: January 6, 2026

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

The context graph conversation has exploded over the past two weeks. Foundation Capital’s “Context Graphs: AI’s Trillion-Dollar Opportunity” kicked it off. Animesh Koratana’s thread on coordinate systems deepened the technical framing. Daniel Davis published “The Context Graph Manifesto” connecting it to the semantic web’s heritage.

The conversation is valuable. The questions being asked—how do we give agents organizational memory? how do we capture decision traces? how do we build systems that learn from how work actually happens?—are the right questions.

But Kirk Marple, who has been building context infrastructure for over three years, argues there’s a false dichotomy emerging that misses the point entirely.

The False Dichotomy

Section titled “The False Dichotomy”

The current conversation has crystallized around a binary choice: prescribed ontologies vs learned ontologies.

On one side: Palantir. The argument goes that Palantir built a $400B+ company on prescribed ontologies—define the schema upfront, map messy enterprise data into it, deploy forward engineers to customize it for each customer. It works, but it’s expensive, slow, and doesn’t scale.

On the other side: The future. Learned ontologies. Structure that emerges from how work actually happens rather than how someone designed it to happen. Agent trajectories as training data. Decision traces that compile into organizational world models. The ontology isn’t declared—it’s discovered.

As Jaya Gupta recently wrote:

“The next $50B company will be built on learned ontologies. Structure that emerges from how work actually happens, not how you designed it to happen… Memory assumes you know what to store and how to retrieve it. But the most valuable context is structure you didn’t know existed until agents discovered it through use.”

Here’s the problem: This framing ignores twenty years of ontology work that already exists.

The Third Option: Adopt, Then Learn

Section titled “The Third Option: Adopt, Then Learn”

What’s missing from the prescribed vs learned framing is a third option that’s been hiding in plain sight:

Adopt what exists. Extend where needed. Focus learning on what’s genuinely novel.

The conversation proceeds as if ontology is either an expensive custom artifact (Palantir) or an emergent property of sufficient agent runs (the learned vision). But there’s twenty years of production ontology work that neither camp is acknowledging.

The Ontologies That Already Exist

Section titled “The Ontologies That Already Exist”

Before we talk about learning ontology, it’s worth acknowledging how much of this work already exists in production systems.

Schema.org

Section titled “Schema.org”

Schema.org is a collaborative vocabulary for structured data on the web, founded in 2011 by Google, Microsoft, Yahoo, and Yandex. It defines canonical types for the entities that matter:

  • Person
  • Organization
  • Place
  • Event
  • Product
  • CreativeWork
  • And hundreds more

This isn’t theoretical. It’s in production across billions of web pages. When you see rich snippets in Google search results—event dates, product prices, recipe ratings—that’s Schema.org markup being parsed and displayed.

The types are well-defined. A Person has a name, a jobTitle, a worksFor relationship to an Organization. An Organization has a name, a location, employees. An Event has a startDate, endDate, location, organizer.

This isn’t a custom ontology someone has to build. It exists. It’s maintained. It’s designed for exactly the kind of entity modeling that context graphs require.

WAND and Microsoft Common Data Model

Section titled “WAND and Microsoft Common Data Model”

Here’s something surprising: Microsoft’s Common Data Model—the schema that powers Dynamics 365, Power Platform, and much of Microsoft’s enterprise stack—was licensed from WAND.

WAND has been building enterprise taxonomies and ontologies for decades. Their work predates the current AI wave by years. When Microsoft needed a canonical data model for enterprise entities—Account, Contact, Lead, Opportunity, Case, Product, Campaign—they didn’t build it from scratch. They licensed it from people who had already done the work.

CDM defines the entities that enterprise software operates on. Not in theory—in production, across thousands of organizations running Microsoft’s business applications.

Industry-Specific Standards

Section titled “Industry-Specific Standards”

Beyond Schema.org and CDM, there are industry-specific ontologies refined over years of real-world use:

  • Healthcare: FHIR for clinical data exchange, SNOMED for medical terminology
  • Finance: FIBO for financial instruments and business entities
  • Manufacturing, logistics, energy: Each has domain-specific standards

These aren’t academic exercises. They’re production infrastructure. FHIR, for instance, defines how to represent patients, conditions, medications, observations, and clinical workflows.

What Palantir Actually Does

Section titled “What Palantir Actually Does”

Daniel Davis made an important observation:

“I don’t know why everyone thinks Palantir has ontology tech. They do not. They take months, if not years, building it with ‘forward deployed engineers’. This is not the way to achieve context graphs at scale.”

This is worth unpacking. Palantir’s expense isn’t because “prescribed ontologies are hard.” It’s because they build custom for every deployment. They’re not leveraging existing standards—they’re creating bespoke models for each customer’s specific data landscape.

That’s a business model choice, not an inherent property of prescribed ontologies. You can adopt Schema.org types, extend with CDM patterns, and add domain-specific entities without spending years on custom modeling.

The Palantir approach is expensive because it’s bespoke consulting dressed up as platform. The prescribed ontology itself isn’t the bottleneck. The bottleneck is refusing to leverage what already exists.

Where Learned Ontologies Actually Help

Section titled “Where Learned Ontologies Actually Help”

The learned ontology vision conflates two very different problems:

  1. Entity modeling: What types of things exist and how do they relate? (Solved—or at least solvable with existing standards)
  2. Organizational intelligence: How does this specific organization actually work? What patterns govern decisions? (Genuinely novel, genuinely requires learning)

The entities themselves—Person, Organization, Account, Contact, Event—don’t need to be learned. We know what these are. The types are stable. The core relationships are well-understood.

Where inference genuinely helps is discovering which custom fields actually matter, which relationships get used, which objects are central vs peripheral to how this specific team works. That’s organizational intelligence on top of a foundation—not the foundation itself.

Specifically, the organizational-specific layer:

  • Which contacts at an account actually matter for this deal?
  • What’s the real decision-making process (vs. the org chart)?
  • Which exceptions get approved and which don’t?
  • What precedents actually govern how decisions are made?

This is tacit knowledge. It’s not in any ontology because it’s specific to how this organization operates. It lives in people’s heads, in Slack threads, in the pattern of which escalations get resolved how.

This is where learning from trajectories makes sense. Not “discover that Account is an entity” but “discover that Sarah Chen is the actual decision-maker even though the CRM says it’s her boss.”

The Bootstrap Problem

Section titled “The Bootstrap Problem”

Here’s where the learned ontology vision runs into practical trouble: you can’t learn from agent trajectories until agents are running effectively. But agents can’t run effectively without foundational context.

“You can’t wait for thousands of agentic RAG runs to ‘discover’ that Sarah Chen is a person who works at Acme Corp. You need to know that before agents start reasoning. Otherwise every agent trajectory is fighting the identity resolution problem anew—and you’re paying for that confusion in tokens, latency, and errors.”

The node2vec analogy actually illustrates this. Node2vec learns embeddings from walk patterns over existing edges. It doesn’t discover nodes and edges from scratch. The graph must exist first.

Agentic workflows operate the same way. The agent harness orchestrates tool calls over a pre-built map. The map is operational context: resolved entities, established relationships, temporal state. Build the map first, then agents walk it effectively.

So how do you bootstrap? The learned ontology vision doesn’t have a clean answer. “Run agents and let structure emerge” assumes you have something to run agents over.

The practical answer: adopt existing ontologies as the foundation, then let learning refine and extend.

What’s Actually Unsolved

Section titled “What’s Actually Unsolved”

If entity modeling is largely solved by existing standards, what’s actually novel? What’s the hard problem that context graphs need to address?

It’s not the nouns. It’s the temporal and decision layer.

Temporal Validity

Section titled “Temporal Validity”

Most systems can tell you what’s true now. Almost none can tell you what was true at a specific point in the past, or how truth evolved over time.

“Paula works at Microsoft” is a fact. But it’s not a complete fact. When did she start? Does she still work there? What was true in 2022?

This is what Animesh Koratana called the “event clock”—the infrastructure for tracking not just current state, but how state changes over time. Facts with validAt and invalidAt timestamps. The ability to query “What did we believe about this account when we made that decision?”

Existing ontologies don’t solve this. Schema.org defines Person and Organization, but it doesn’t have native primitives for “this relationship was true from date X to date Y.”

Decision Traces

Section titled “Decision Traces”

Foundation Capital’s original piece got this right: the reasoning that connects data to action has never been captured as data.

When a renewal agent proposes a 20% discount despite a 10% policy cap, it pulls context from multiple systems. Finance approves. The CRM records “20% discount.” Everything that made that decision legible—the inputs, the policy evaluation, the exception route, the approval chain—disappears.

Capturing decision traces isn’t an ontology problem in the traditional sense. It’s an instrumentation problem. You need to be in the execution path where decisions are committed, capturing not just what happened but why it was allowed to happen.

Fact Resolution

Section titled “Fact Resolution”

When you extract facts from content, you get assertions—not necessarily truth. Content can be wrong, outdated, or contradictory.

“The customer is renewing” might appear in a March email. “The customer churned” might appear in a September Slack thread. Both are facts extracted from content. But they can’t both be currently true.

Fact resolution—determining what’s canonical, what’s superseded, what’s corroborated—is genuinely hard. It requires judgment about temporal validity, source authority, and how later information updates earlier assertions.

Why This Matters for Builders

Section titled “Why This Matters for Builders”

The “learned ontology” framing suggests you should focus on trajectory capture, embedding learning, emergent structure. Build the infrastructure for agents to discover ontology through use.

Kirk Marple suggests something different: focus on what’s actually unsolved.

  • Entity modeling? Adopt existing standards. Don’t spend months defining what an Account is. Microsoft already licensed that from WAND.
  • Temporal validity? This is where the work is. Facts that change over time. The ability to query historical state. The event clock.
  • Decision traces? This is where the opportunity is. Being in the execution path. Capturing reasoning as data.
  • Fact resolution? This is where the hard AI problems live. Using LLMs to determine what’s canonical, what’s superseded.

The Semantic Web Finally Won

Section titled “The Semantic Web Finally Won”

There’s a through-line in this conversation that deserves acknowledgment. The semantic web community—RDF, OWL, linked data—has been working on these problems for decades. Schema.org emerged from that tradition.

The vision of machines reasoning over structured, interoperable data wasn’t invented by the AI crowd in 2024.

What killed adoption wasn’t the ideas. It was the syntax. RDF, SPARQL, OWL—the learning curve was brutal, and most developers never climbed it.

LLMs changed that. They can read JSON-LD natively. They understand Schema.org types from training data. They reason over structured data without needing custom query languages or formal inference engines.

The semantic web’s concepts won. The syntax lost. And that’s fine—JSON-LD gives you the benefits without the tax.

Three Principles for Context Graph Builders

Section titled “Three Principles for Context Graph Builders”

  1. Adopt foundations, don’t reinvent them. The entity modeling problem is solved well enough. Don’t spend innovation budget rediscovering it.

  2. Build the temporal layer. Facts with validity periods. Queryable history. Time as a first-class dimension.

  3. Focus learning on what’s genuinely novel. Organizational patterns. Decision dynamics. The tacit knowledge specific to how this organization works. That’s where trajectories help—on top of a foundation, not instead of one.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

The context graph vision is achievable. The question is whether we build on what exists or waste years rediscovering it.

The prescribed vs learned dichotomy is too clean. The entities don’t need to be learned—they need to be adopted: Schema.org, CDM, WAND. The work has been done.

What does need to be built is the temporal and decision layer. Facts that change over time. Reasoning captured as data. Resolution that maintains coherence. That’s genuinely novel. That’s where the opportunity lives.

Issue: Content Loss Caused by GLM Quotas Limit

Jan 6, 2026
Vibe Sparking

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

In an effort to accelerate content creation, an experiment was conducted to write multiple blog posts simultaneously using Claude Code. However, this aggressive parallelization strategy hit a bottleneck: the GLM API quota limits. This unexpected constraint led to content loss, with several blog posts failing to complete.

The Experiment: Aggressive Parallelization

Section titled “The Experiment: Aggressive Parallelization”

To maximize efficiency, over 10 instances of Claude Code shells were launched in parallel. The goal was to generate a batch of blog posts concurrently.

The Issue: Hitting the Ceiling

Section titled “The Issue: Hitting the Ceiling”

As the multiple instances began generating content, they rapidly consumed the available API quota. The GLM API, having strict rate limits and quotas, blocked further requests once the threshold was reached.

The system immediately flagged the quota violation.

The Consequence: Content Loss

Section titled “The Consequence: Content Loss”

The direct result of hitting the API limit was that the generation process for several blog posts was abruptly terminated. This left the files in an incomplete state, effectively resulting in content loss.

Checking the WeChat Official Account Draft Box confirmed that the articles were not successfully fully generated or uploaded.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

While parallel processing is a powerful technique for efficiency, it must be balanced against external constraints like API quotas. Future batch processing will need to implement rate limiting or sequential queuing to ensure stability and data integrity.

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

If you observe carefully, you’ll notice a phenomenon:

People who genuinely “feel AI is useless” often can’t articulate what they want to accomplish.

This is not a coincidence.

AI Is Not a Tool Everyone Needs

Section titled “AI Is Not a Tool Everyone Needs”

Let’s start with a politically incorrect truth:

AI is not a “productivity equalizer.”

It’s not water, electricity, or air. Nor is it an emotional consumer good like short-form video.

AI is more like an amplifier—it only works on what already exists.

  • People with problems use AI to break them down more clearly
  • People with ideas use AI to push them further
  • People with direction use AI to compress paths and reduce trial and error

What about those without these things?

For them, AI truly is “useless.”

Why “People Without Ideas” Don’t Need AI

Section titled “Why “People Without Ideas” Don’t Need AI”

AI has a冷酷 prerequisite:

You must first know what you want to do.

AI won’t decide your life direction. It won’t generate desires for you.

It only responds to:

  • Problems
  • Hypotheses
  • Goals
  • Paths

Yet many people’s daily state is:

  • Passively binge-watching shows
  • Passively scrolling videos
  • Passively working
  • Passively waiting for opportunities

Ask them what they want, and after thinking they say:

“I want to get rich overnight.”

This statement itself declares AI’s uselessness.

Why “Get Rich Quick” Is AI’s Nemesis

Section titled “Why “Get Rich Quick” Is AI’s Nemesis”

Because it’s not a goal—it’s an escapist fantasy.

Its subtext is:

  • I don’t want to think about the process
  • I don’t want to own the journey
  • I only want the result

AI is precisely the opposite.

AI excels at exactly one thing:

Making “what you’re already thinking about” clearer.

If you think about nothing, hoping the world suddenly changes—what you need isn’t AI. It’s luck, lottery tickets, or miracles.

Binge-Watching vs AI: Two Opposite Systems

Section titled “Binge-Watching vs AI: Two Opposite Systems”

Many don’t realize:

AI is the first mass technology that doesn’t serve escapism.

Binge-watching shows and scrolling short videos share:

  • No intention needed
  • No choices needed
  • No responsibility needed

You just “continue to next.”

AI’s interface seems gentle, but its core is cold:

  • You don’t ask, it doesn’t move
  • You ask unclearly, it exposes your confusion
  • You don’t give direction, it spins in place

Some instinctively reject it. Not because it’s hard to use, but because it reflects their empty-spinning state.

Why Not Using AI Is Becoming a “Generation Gap”

Section titled “Why Not Using AI Is Becoming a “Generation Gap””

Over the past year, AI hasn’t been “improving efficiency”—it’s been restructuring cognitive division of labor:

HumanAI
Judgment, stance, trade-offsExpansion, verification, parallel execution, compression

Not using AI means:

  • All thinking runs only in your own brain
  • All trial and error costs your personal time
  • Once problem complexity rises, you immediately struggle

Short-term, they can still work. But long-term, their ceiling is locked.

A Less Polite but Real Conclusion

Section titled “A Less Polite but Real Conclusion”

It’s not that AI abandoned many people.

Rather, many people were never on the path that “needs AI” from the start.

They don’t need an amplifier because there’s nothing worth amplifying.

What they need is:

  • Distraction
  • Anesthesia
  • Miracles

Not:

  • Thinking
  • Reasoning
  • Action paths

The Final Line (The Boundary)

Section titled “The Final Line (The Boundary)”

AI belongs only to those who have started taking responsibility for their lives.

If you’re already thinking:

  • What do I want to change?
  • What do I want to advance?
  • Where am I stuck?

Then AI becomes your external brain.

If you’re still at:

  • “Whatever, doesn’t matter”
  • “Let’s see what luck brings”
  • “Wait for opportunity”

Then saying AI is useless is completely correct.
