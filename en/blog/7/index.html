Hedge AI Risk: Why Internet Workers Should Hold Nvidia Instead of Worrying About Pay Cuts and Layoffs

Jul 28, 2025
Vibe Sparking

The internet era had near-zero marginal costs for “information flow”; the AI era has persistently high marginal costs for “information generation” that scale linearly with usage. Resources shift from “people” to “computing power + electricity,” as companies accelerate from light-asset tertiary industries toward “secondary industrialization.” As internet workers, we should upgrade our knowledge and skills with one hand, while using asset allocation (like Nvidia) to hedge the risk of dilution in the value chain with the other.

TL;DR

Section titled “TL;DR”
  1. Fundamental Change: PC/Mobile Internet = low marginal cost “information distribution”; AI = high marginal cost “information generation.”
  2. Company Profile: OPEX→CAPEX, talent→GPU, electricity, data centers, internet companies becoming “industrialized.”
  3. Personal Strategy: Career shifts from “writing features” to “managing engineering and computing economics”; Assets use “AI upstream leaders + diversified ETFs” to hedge personal cash flow risk (not investment advice).

Recent Headlines

Section titled “Recent Headlines”

On July 2, 2025, according to WIRED magazine, Mark Zuckerberg offered top research talent compensation packages worth up to $300 million (approximately 21.8 billion RMB) over four years for his newly established Meta Super Intelligence Lab, with first-year total compensation exceeding $100 million (approximately 727 million RMB).

On July 11, 2025, Nvidia became the world’s first publicly traded company to exceed $4 trillion in market capitalization. Nvidia spent 30 years building a $1 trillion company, then took just two years to transform into a $4 trillion company.

On July 24, 2025, Google parent company Alphabet announced it would spend $85 billion in capital expenditures in 2025, up from the $75 billion indicated at the beginning of the year.

1. Three Fundamental Differences: PC/Mobile Internet vs. AI Era

Section titled “1. Three Fundamental Differences: PC/Mobile Internet vs. AI Era”

① Information Method: From “Information Flow” to “Information Generation”

Section titled “① Information Method: From “Information Flow” to “Information Generation””
  • Past: The internet delivered existing content faster, broader, cheaper to users (search, social, video).
  • Present: AI regenerates text, images, code, speech, etc. for each user request, consuming computing power and electricity with every generation.

② Marginal Cost: From Near Zero to Persistently High

Section titled “② Marginal Cost: From Near Zero to Persistently High”
  • Internet: Once network and CDN infrastructure is built, distribution marginal cost approaches zero.
  • AI: Training isn’t “one-time,” requiring annual iterations + continuously increasing investment; inference is pay-per-call, higher usage means higher costs. Even if hardware iterations bring “better performance at same price,” stronger models will consume the saved costs (trading experience for cost).

③ Usage Scale: From “Population/Device Ceiling” to “Unlimited Automation”

Section titled “③ Usage Scale: From “Population/Device Ceiling” to “Unlimited Automation””
  • Internet: Active users constrained by device and population ceilings.
  • AI: Agents/automation/autonomy remove “human usage” limits — machine-to-machine calls can amplify 7×24×N times, with demand side having exponential growth potential.

2. New Profile of Internet Companies in the AI Era

Section titled “2. New Profile of Internet Companies in the AI Era”

① Rising Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) with “Annual Renewals”

Section titled “① Rising Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) with “Annual Renewals””
  • Training: Large model infrastructure isn’t one-time, hardware depreciation cycles are short, replacing every 2-3 years.
  • Inference: Each call generates costs, revenue grows with usage, computing bills grow synchronously.
  • Financial Side: Shift from emphasizing OPEX (R&D salaries) to emphasizing CAPEX (GPUs, data centers, electricity).

② Resource Scarcity Reversal: From “Engineer Time” to “GPU + Electricity + Data Centers”

Section titled “② Resource Scarcity Reversal: From “Engineer Time” to “GPU + Electricity + Data Centers””
  • Internet era moats were network effects and near-zero distribution costs;
  • Current moats increasingly come from computing scale, power capacity, site selection and cooling, cluster scheduling and operational experience.

③ Industry Attribute Switch: Tertiary Industry → Secondary Industrialization

Section titled “③ Industry Attribute Switch: Tertiary Industry → Secondary Industrialization”
  • Internet companies increasingly resemble traditional industrial enterprises: heavy assets, long cycles, strong maintenance, engineering-focused.
  • Talent Structure evolution: From large-scale SDE software development engineers → Pre-training / Foundation Model and other “computing management” roles.

3. Crisis and Opportunity in the AI Era

Section titled “3. Crisis and Opportunity in the AI Era”

① Physical Constraints: GPUs and Electricity

Section titled “① Physical Constraints: GPUs and Electricity”
  • Power capacity and grid access are hard constraints; data center differences in location, electricity prices, cooling determine unit computing costs.
  • Result: Computing expansion isn’t purely “throwing money,” but a systematic capability competition of “engineering + supply chain + electricity.”

② Generation Excess: Strong Supply Side, Relatively Lagging Demand Side

Section titled “② Generation Excess: Strong Supply Side, Relatively Lagging Demand Side”
  • AI capacity increases exponentially, but human attention and purchasing power don’t match, easily creating periodic “bubbles.”
  • Solution: Let AI become “consumers” themselves — automated agents complete retrieval, ordering, coordination processes, connecting supply to real business loops.

③ Worker Reality: High-Benefit Era Contraction

Section titled “③ Worker Reality: High-Benefit Era Contraction”
  • Resources shift from “people” to “computing power,” SDEs’ marginal bargaining power gets diluted.
  • Philosophy: Collaborate with AI in a friendly, polite, efficient manner, upgrade to “engineering managers” (managing requirements, quality, data, costs, risks).

The AI wave won’t stop because of our fears.

Instead of worrying about salary cuts:

Improve your ability to “orchestrate AI” rather than “write business code”;

Include NVDA and other AI tech leaders in asset allocation, using stocks to hedge career risks;

Final note: How much of your salary would you convert to Nvidia? Why? Join the discussion in the comments.

Unlock Claude Code's Thinking Modes: From 'think' to 'ultrathink'

Vibe Sparking

When used well, think is your secret weapon for breaking through bottlenecks; when used poorly, Claude works like it “left its brain at home”.

Have You Ever Had This Experience?

Section titled “Have You Ever Had This Experience?”

You ask Claude to help analyze a problem, and it gives you an answer in three sentences.

It looks “decent enough”, but when you try to implement it, everything goes wrong, full of bugs.

Many people think Claude isn’t capable, but the truth is: you didn’t let it think properly!

Claude’s Hidden Skill: Thinking Gear Adjuster

Section titled “Claude’s Hidden Skill: Thinking Gear Adjuster”

Claude Code actually has a built-in “thinking accelerator” tool (called Think Tool), but you have to trigger it yourself.

🧩 It supports 4 thinking levels:

Section titled “🧩 It supports 4 thinking levels:”
GearKeywordsThinking Token LimitThinking Depth
NONENo keywords added0 TokensQuick execution, almost no thinking
BASICthink / 想4K TokensBasic thinking, quick rough planning
MIDDLEthink hard / 好好想10K TokensModerate thinking, suitable for complex tasks
HIGHESTultrathink / 仔细思考32K TokensDeepest thinking, suitable for tough challenges

This isn’t mysticism, but real settings written in Claude Code’s engineering code.

Add keywords, and it really will think for a while longer!

🎯Chinese Keywords Can Also Trigger

Section titled “🎯Chinese Keywords Can Also Trigger”

Don’t worry that Claude can’t understand Chinese, its trigger logic also supports our native language:

  • HIGHEST (32K): 仔细思考、深思、多想一会
  • MIDDLE (10K): 好好想、多想想
  • BASIC (4K): 思考、想

For example, if you say “Think carefully about this problem before giving me a plan” — Claude immediately switches to the highest gear, allocating the most thinking time!

Practical Advice: How to Use Most Effectively?

Section titled “Practical Advice: How to Use Most Effectively?”

Of course, here are typical usage scenario examples for each thinking gear, concise and clear, ready to use directly 👇

🧠 ultrathink Example: Breaking Down Complex Product Features

Section titled “🧠 ultrathink Example: Breaking Down Complex Product Features”

Prompt: I want to develop a “Smart Daily Report Assistant” that can automatically aggregate today’s key events from Slack, Notion, GitHub, and help me generate daily report drafts. Please think carefully (ultrathink) about this problem first, then output a module division, data flow, and API interface design.

📌 Use Cases: Multi-system integration, vague requirements, need deep architectural thinking

🧠 think hard Example: Evaluating Multiple Technical Solutions

Section titled “🧠 think hard Example: Evaluating Multiple Technical Solutions”

Prompt: I want to choose a database solution for a multi-user collaborative document application. Please think hard about whether to choose PostgreSQL + Redis or MongoDB + Kafka, output a comparative analysis and give recommendation reasons.

📌 Use Cases: Multiple routes available, need comparative analysis

🧠 think Example: Small Task Logical Planning

Section titled “🧠 think Example: Small Task Logical Planning”

Prompt: I’m planning to write a script that downloads all image links from a Markdown file and renames them. Please think about how to do this first, which Python libraries are most suitable, then write the code for me.

📌 Use Cases: Task isn’t complex, just need a simple run-through of the approach

🧠 No Keywords Example: Direct Action

Section titled “🧠 No Keywords Example: Direct Action”

Prompt: I’ve already written the usage instructions for this AI summary tool. Please optimize the following text format into a README.md file, output the result directly, no need to overthink.

📌 Use Cases: Clear requirements, just need execution, no planning needed

🚀Recommended Usage Pattern: “Explore, Plan, Code, Commit”

Section titled “🚀Recommended Usage Pattern: “Explore, Plan, Code, Commit””

Claude Code officially recommends a 4-step workflow:

  1. Explore: Let Claude read files first, but don’t write code
  2. Plan: Use ultrathink to make detailed plans (core step)
  3. Code: Write code based on the plan, self-check reasonableness
  4. Commit: Generate Pull Request and changelog

📌 Key Point: Step #2 Plan must add keywords to trigger “thinking gears”, otherwise it might skip deep thinking and start working directly!

🧠It’s Not That Claude Isn’t Smart, You Just Didn’t Let It Use Its Brain

Section titled “🧠It’s Not That Claude Isn’t Smart, You Just Didn’t Let It Use Its Brain”

Claude is very capable, but if you don’t give it “thinking space”, it can only respond hastily.

Use think, think hard, think harder, ultrathink well, and you can not only improve output quality but also solve complex problems in one go.

AI Vibe Coding: Speeding Up or Slowing Down? Insights from a 19% Slowdown

Jul 28, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

This year, AI Vibe Coding programming tools have created quite a buzz in developer circles. Many developers (myself included) thought that with AI intelligent assistants, coding efficiency would dramatically improve. However, recent research threw cold water on us: using AI tools actually slowed development by nearly 19%! This study by METR conducted controlled experiments with experienced open-source developers, yielding surprising results. Participants expected AI to reduce task completion time by 24%, but actually slowed down by nearly one-fifth. More interestingly, even after experiencing the slowdown firsthand, many programmers still believed AI “made me faster” — a kind of “psychological acceleration” illusion.

As an internet e-commerce backend developer, I actually resonate with these results. Reflecting on my own experience introducing AI assistants for coding, there were indeed many pitfalls: sometimes waiting for AI code output made me scratch my head anxiously, sometimes I had to interrupt my own thinking to accommodate AI. Below, I’ll combine actual development cases to discuss why AI programming might slow down the pace. I hope these experiences can serve as a warning to fellow developers.

The Long Wait for AI Execution

Section titled “The Long Wait for AI Execution”

Once upon a time, we coded with brain-to-hand fluidity - when inspiration struck, we’d tap it out immediately. But after introducing AI assistants, waiting for them to “think” and output became a new time sink. When implementing asynchronous message processing in my project, I thought I’d have AI help generate consumer logic. After asking a question, that little circle on screen just kept spinning, with AI “pondering” in the cloud while I could only stare and wait. Those few dozen seconds might seem insignificant, but they accumulate into substantial overhead. More troublesome is when AI occasionally outputs a huge chunk of code at once, forcing me to stop and wait for it to finish scrolling, completely disrupting my rhythm.

During these waiting periods, I’d unconsciously check emails or do other things, but my mind remained occupied with AI’s response. In this state, it’s hard to focus on other tasks, essentially putting me in “suspension”. Without AI, I might have already coded up an initial draft myself. Now, precious development time slips away waiting for the machine to “finish thinking.” Clearly, while AI can automatically produce code, its speed doesn’t necessarily match human intuitive response. The process of waiting for AI is like introducing additional latency into iterations, severely fragmenting what was once a smooth development rhythm.

Disruption from Multitask Switching

Section titled “Disruption from Multitask Switching”

With AI assistants, I once tried “parallel development”: having AI write one module while I worked on something else. But reality proved that the cost of context switching back and forth was far greater than imagined. For example: when developing idempotency handling for a payment callback interface, I had AI generate code for duplicate request validation while I debugged another piece of business logic. Minutes later, AI produced results, so I switched back to read and integrate. But with the other matter still occupying my mind, I had to shift my thinking to AI’s code. Going back and forth like this, both sides slowed down.

Humans aren’t truly adept at multitasking. In work requiring full concentration like coding, frequent context switching creates serious cognitive overhead. Each jump from my own thinking to AI’s thinking requires time to understand AI’s code intent and recall the previous problem context. It’s like listening to two people talk simultaneously - you need to constantly “fill in” context between them, and efficiency suffers accordingly. Especially when AI-generated content differs from my original code style or thinking, I need extra effort to adapt. Imagine developing a complex e-commerce backend feature during peak hours while handling AI “interruptions” like customer service - this parallelism not only didn’t save time, but slowed the entire process.

The Burden of Reviewing and Correcting AI Output

Section titled “The Burden of Reviewing and Correcting AI Output”

AI-generated code isn’t ready-to-use; careful review and debugging are often unavoidable. In actual projects, I’ve repeatedly encountered AI “hallucinations” - fabricating non-existent functions or misusing interfaces. For example, what made me both cry and laugh was when I asked AI to help implement idempotency handling for order payment callbacks. It enthusiastically wrote a bunch of code, but closer inspection revealed calls to module functions that didn’t exist in our system! AI’s overconfident wild guessing forced me to re-examine every line of code. Research found similar phenomena: developers accepted less than 44% of AI suggestions, with most code requiring modification or rewriting, and 75% of cases requiring programmers to review every line of AI output. This review and modification process essentially spent back the time supposedly saved.

Even when AI code runs, it likely doesn’t meet our business expectations. I once had AI optimize Kafka message processing logic. Its solution looked reasonable, but running it revealed improper handling of edge cases like out-of-order and duplicate messages, nearly introducing new bugs. I ended up spending time simplifying the fancy parts it “smartly” added. Another time, to solve a problem, AI provided a complex multi-threading solution that was overkill and introduced thread safety hazards. AI’s excessive enthusiasm sometimes backfires, making me spend more time on rework. Ultimately, current AI code reliability is poor, forcing me to stay vigilant and scrutinize with a critical eye, reviewing line by line like code review, fearing it might bury some trap.

Time Drain from Prompt Optimization

Section titled “Time Drain from Prompt Optimization”

Many think asking AI questions is effortless, but actually getting AI to understand you and provide useful answers requires repeatedly refining prompts. In my experience, this has almost become an art of “speaking human to AI.” When I had AI help generate product inventory synchronization logic in my project, my first problem description was met with an irrelevant answer; I had to rephrase and add business context, but the second attempt was still unsatisfactory. Going back and forth in deep dialogue with AI for several rounds felt like training a new employee: gradually correcting its misunderstandings and continuously refining requirements. Time spent crafting and optimizing prompts is invisible but very real.

Sometimes, to make AI understand complex scenarios, I’d even paste relevant code snippets and error messages, which is time-consuming itself. For example, implementing asynchronous refunds in order workflows, I needed it to focus on idempotency and transactionality, and just explaining this business context took a long message. By the time AI gradually “grasped” my intent, considerable time had passed. These back-and-forth communications essentially produced no new business value, only helped AI figure out the situation. We can say prompt optimization overhead partially offset AI coding’s acceleration advantages. We think “chatting” with AI is quick, but might actually fall into prolonged debugging conversations, dragging the pace even slower.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

The emergence of AI programming assistants undoubtedly provides developers with new productivity tools. But as revealed by the above personal experiences and research results, current-stage AI is not a silver bullet. It might improve efficiency and reduce grunt work in simple scenarios, but faces complex business logic and large codebases where AI is often willing but unable. Developers must not only spend time waiting and switching focus back and forth, but also cover for AI mistakes and painstakingly train it. These hidden costs accumulate into productivity regression. Of course, we’re not denying AI tools’ value, but reminding everyone to rationally view their applicability: when proficiency is insufficient or application scenarios don’t match, AI might actually slow you down.

From a frontline developer’s perspective, I feel “love-hate.” AI’s new approaches and paradigms have benefited me greatly, but when it gets in the way, it’s truly frustrating. Perhaps as models evolve and our usage skills improve, this 19% disadvantage will transform into real acceleration advantages.
