How to Effectively Tell AI What Not to Do: Positive Constraints and Structured Output for Prompts

Aug 14, 2025
Vibe Sparking

This article turns “don’t write ××” into “will reliably write √√.” I’ll first distill your six ideas into executable prompt patterns, then blend in frontline team best practices and research-backed evidence: why negative instructions (“don’t …”) often fail, and how to use hierarchical instructions and structured output to pull models back on track. Authoritative sources are cited conceptually so you can extend and reuse.

Introduction | Why does “the more you forbid, the more it appears” keep happening?

Section titled “Introduction | Why does “the more you forbid, the more it appears” keep happening?”

Many people begin with: “Don’t fabricate, don’t sound like AI, don’t write fluff.” And then what happens? The model proceeds to fabricate, gets long-winded, and gives you a “warm, upbeat” tone on top. It’s not being rebellious; rather, models are intrinsically less sensitive to negation and more likely to skip the “not” in complex language. Multiple studies and practical guidance point to the same conclusion: using positive phrasing instead of negative prohibitions yields more stable results.

Main Text

Section titled “Main Text”

1) Turn “don’t” into “do”: replace negative bans with positive constraints

Section titled “1) Turn “don’t” into “do”: replace negative bans with positive constraints”
  • Replace “don’t write ungrammatical sentences” with: “Self-check grammar and cohesion sentence by sentence and revise to fluency; when uncertain, write ‘I’m not sure’ and pause output.”
  • Replace “don’t sound like AI” with: “Use colloquial language and short sentences; keep paragraphs within four lines; provide two everyday examples before concluding.” Why it works: Research shows models have systematic weaknesses in handling negation; positive instructions reduce ambiguity and off-topic drift.

Bonus: Allowing the model to admit uncertainty significantly reduces hallucination. Hard-code this line into your prompt for a low-cost, high-return gain.

2) Set the rules before writing: put prohibitions up front and repeat them

Section titled “2) Set the rules before writing: put prohibitions up front and repeat them”
  • Structural order: [System/High-Level Instructions] → [Prohibitions/Positive Requirements] → [Task & Input] → [Output Format].
  • Repetition matters: Front-load once, remind again mid-task, and add a pre-output checklist. Enterprise-grade guides suggest you should “double down” on critical instructions to keep model attention steady in long contexts.

3) Use hierarchical instructions as a safety net: whose words are “harder” law?

Section titled “3) Use hierarchical instructions as a safety net: whose words are “harder” law?”

When instructions conflict, higher-level directives win. Place high-priority content such as style, prohibitions, compliance in the system/high-level slot, priority > task description > external materials. OpenAI’s instruction hierarchy research and practice reinforce this precedence rule. If you write the hierarchy clearly, the model is less likely to be “led astray.”

Practical crib sheet:

  • System layer: role, values, prohibitions (e.g., no privacy leaks / no fabrication)
  • User layer: this task’s goals, audience profile, scenario constraints
  • Third-party content: references, source links (readable, not to be copied) This way, precedence is clear when conflicts arise, keeping the model steady.

4) Lock down the form with structured output to reduce drift

Section titled “4) Lock down the form with structured output to reduce drift”

Instead of saying “don’t write fluff,” specify the output blueprint:

  • Ask the model to output via JSON Schema or a table, with bounded fields and lengths. Official docs from OpenAI and Google support structured output, converting “style slogans” into verifiable fields.

Example fields: tone (tone description), max_lines_per_paragraph, examples (must include concrete examples), etc. Upside: programmatic validation makes “out-of-bounds” easy to spot.

5) Provide high-quality examples so the model can “ape the cat”

Section titled “5) Provide high-quality examples so the model can “ape the cat””

Rather than “don’t be a laundry list,” give one or two positive/negative examples:

  • Positive example: short sentences, two brief stories, action-oriented takeaway.
  • Negative example: slogan-stacking, abstract nouns, no supporting data. Anthropic and OpenAI both stress that explicit exemplars materially improve hit rate.

6) Split prohibitions into two steps: produce first, then self-check

Section titled “6) Split prohibitions into two steps: produce first, then self-check”

Your fourth idea is spot on: split one-shot completion into two passes. Make it operational:

  1. Draft: generate according to the structured format.
  2. Self-check (performed by the model): compare against the prohibition checklist and style checklist; wherever unmet—fix only that part and leave the rest unchanged. This is a lightweight form of self-critique, aligned with Constitutional AI (CAI): use explicit principles and iterative self-audits to reduce boundary violations.

7) One more thing: can bans actually “ban”? Define a “style source” first

Section titled “7) One more thing: can bans actually “ban”? Define a “style source” first”

Many attempts to “ban AI-ness” fail because there’s no actionable style definition. Solution:

  • Specify audience, scenario, tone, sentence length, paragraph length;
  • Provide two sample passages you approve of as the “style source”;
  • Require citation-and-proofing (e.g., “one example every 300 words / data must have a source”). These practices are repeatedly validated in official best-practice guides.

8) The anti-hallucination trifecta (say goodbye to “don’t fabricate”)

Section titled “8) The anti-hallucination trifecta (say goodbye to “don’t fabricate”)”
  • Allow “I don’t know.”
  • Require citations and verification (extract source text first, then summarize; if nothing is found, withdraw).
  • Separate facts and opinions into columns in the output. Anthropic’s anti-hallucination guidance includes copy-ready phrasing—fold it into your long-term templates.

Copy-Ready Template | Operationalizing the six methods into an “engineering prompt”

Section titled “Copy-Ready Template | Operationalizing the six methods into an “engineering prompt””

Use cases: WeChat articles / project proposals / marketing copy Traits: high-priority bans, positive constraints, structured output, two-stage self-check

This template converts prohibitions into positive, testable, correctable constraints with a structured blueprint.

Mini case | Making “ban AI-ness” operational for content production

Section titled “Mini case | Making “ban AI-ness” operational for content production”

What not to do:

Don’t use an AI tone. Don’t write fluff. Don’t fabricate.

Engineering rewrite:

  • Style: colloquial + short sentences; ≤4 lines per paragraph; one concrete example per ~300 words.
  • Structured: { sections[].example, sections[].max_lines = 4 }
  • Anti-hallucination: allow “uncertain”; if no source found → write “none” in citations.
  • Two stages: draft → self-check (does each section include ≥1 example? are sources provided?). This aligns with official guidance on structured output and hallucination reduction, and works reliably in practice.

Conclusion | Turn “don’t” into habit

Section titled “Conclusion | Turn “don’t” into habit”

Rather than shouting “don’t,” turn “don’t” into clear, executable “do’s”: Pre-rules + positive phrasing + structured output + repeated reminders + two-stage self-check. With instruction hierarchy and permission to not know, most “the more you forbid, the more it appears” traps get paved over.

Action checklist:

  • Refine your “do-not”s into two positive rules;
  • Put them at the very top, and repeat once mid-way and once pre-output;
  • Close with JSON/table;
  • Produce first, then self-check, fixing only noncompliant parts. After that, hand the final draft to your human partner for a second pass.

One last key step: after completing the above, tell me your “do-not” checklist. I’ll run one self-check and localized revision per the template in this article, leaving all other content unchanged. This step is required.

As Claude Code Gets Stronger, Engineers Should Spend More Time on Architecture, Requirements, and Problem‑Solving

Aug 14, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Over the next 6–24 months, the direction of AI‑driven “Vibe Coding” will become ever clearer, and within 5 years the way we program will change dramatically; routine implementation and boilerplate coding will be highly automated, and engineers will spend more time on architecture, translating requirements, and creative problem‑solving. At the same time, don’t underestimate the speed and limits of this evolution. This captures the emerging consensus since the rise of AI coding assistants like Claude Code and Cursor.

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

Over the past year, you may have felt it: writing code is no longer “finishing a function,” but organizing intelligence and process. Cursor inside the IDE can “see the repo, run commands, and act as an executor.” Claude Code in the terminal brings Slash commands, Sub‑agents, and automated security reviews. This isn’t hype — it’s a new production line.

1) Where exactly are AI coding assistants strong?

Section titled “1) Where exactly are AI coding assistants strong?”
  • Code understanding and batch rewriting: Cursor’s Agent mode supports cross‑file exploration, edits, and multi‑turn fixes; Ask mode does read‑only retrieval to understand first, then modify.
  • Orchestratable workflows: Claude Code’s Slash Commands turn common actions into commands; Sub‑agents let different tasks use dedicated helpers with isolated context.
  • Built‑in security review: the official /security-review and GitHub Action can automatically scan PRs and suggest fixes.

Bottom line: the goal isn’t to “type a few extra lines for you,” but to turn requirements → changes → verification → merge into a semi‑automated assembly line.

2) What work will be “accelerated” or even “replaced”?

Section titled “2) What work will be “accelerated” or even “replaced”?”
  1. Boilerplate, CRUD, repetitive scaffolding — the most common AI use case. In Stack Overflow’s 2024 survey, among developers who use AI, 82% use it to “write code.”
  2. Small‑to‑medium feature work and debugging — GitHub and academic controlled studies show developers using Copilot complete tasks 55.8% faster.
  3. Tests, docs, migrations — trend reports and frontline practice alike reinforce that AI is taking over the grunt work, letting humans return to design and decision‑making.

3) Risks and pitfalls not to ignore

Section titled “3) Risks and pitfalls not to ignore”
  • Security regression risk: multiple studies indicate developers using code‑gen AI are more likely to produce insecure code; industry reports likewise note that more than half of developers frequently see security issues in AI suggestions.
  • Lack of understanding and over‑reliance: recent surveys show 59% of developers use AI‑generated code they don’t fully understand, creating quality and maintainability hazards.
  • Trust and quality gap: leaders and community voices stress that AI is powerful, but doesn’t automatically ship products faster — we need mature engineering methods and human gatekeeping.

4) How the engineer’s role is shifting

Section titled “4) How the engineer’s role is shifting”
  • From “implementer” to “designer and verifier”: leaders broadly agree developers will invest more in architecture, validation, and AI supervision.
  • From “writing functions” to “building factories”: connect Claude Code commands, Sub‑agents, CI security reviews, and Cursor Agent to build an end‑to‑end automated pipeline.

5) A 90‑day action plan (copy‑ready)

Section titled “5) A 90‑day action plan (copy‑ready)”

Days 0–30: Standardize the process

  • Provide a Spec template (user stories, boundaries, acceptance criteria).
  • In Claude Code, implement common Slash commands such as /create-mvp, /write-tests, /changelog.

Days 31–60: Shift security left

  • Add the Claude Code Security Review GitHub Action to the repo so every PR is scanned automatically with suggested fixes.
  • Set blocking rules and allowlists to reduce false positives and noise.

Days 61–90: Orchestrate the intelligence

  • Use Sub‑agents for a “test agent, docs agent, migration agent.”
  • In Cursor, combine Agent / Ask modes — Ask to read the codebase first, then modify.

6) Opportunity map for individuals and teams

Section titled “6) Opportunity map for individuals and teams”
  • New roles: Agent Ops (keep agents running reliably), Prompt QA (turn natural language into acceptance cases), security review collaboration (AI + SAST/DAST).
  • 10× individuals: one person can use Cursor/Claude Code to stand up the pipeline from scaffolding to deploy; small teams can punch above their weight.
  • Organizational compounding: codify useful Slash commands, Sub‑agents, and checklists in the repo — the more you use them, the smoother it gets.

7) Two suggestions for managers

Section titled “7) Two suggestions for managers”
  1. Bake “human‑in‑the‑loop” into the process: before merging code, require both AI security review + human review.
  2. Measure ROI with data: compare pre/post‑AI lead time, defect rate, rollback rate, refactor frequency — don’t just look at commit counts. The industry debate is a reminder to let metrics do the talking.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

AI isn’t here to steal keyboards; it’s here to reshape the production line. Lots of people can write code; far fewer can build an AI‑driven production line. State requirements clearly, contain risk, and choreograph intelligence — that’s the core competitive edge of the next‑gen engineer. Are you ready?

Food for thought: What are the first three things your team could “automate”?
How would you turn them into Slash commands or Sub‑agents?

Vibe Coding vs. Traditional: Stop Typing, Start Shipping

Aug 14, 2025
Vibe Sparking

Vibe Coding vs. Traditional Programming — key differences:

  • Vibe Coding: driven by natural language; you act as a director; focus on the end result; progress by questioning and dialogue. It’s fast and lowers the barrier, but can lead to a black‑box understanding.
  • Traditional programming: precise programming languages; you act more like an architect; focus on implementation details; emphasize algorithms and principles. It’s slower and higher‑bar, but yields deep code understanding.

Introduction

Section titled “Introduction”

Over the past two years, writing code has increasingly become an exercise in making intent explicit. Editing projects directly with natural language, refactoring across files, and auto‑generating PRs are now everyday workflows:

  • Tools like Cursor/VS Code support natural‑language edits to entire code blocks and cross‑file changes.
  • Replit Agent emphasizes “describe in everyday language and get a runnable app.”
  • Claude Code in the terminal turns “idea → code” into an end‑to‑end workflow. Adoption is surging: Stack Overflow’s 2024 survey shows 76% of developers use or plan to use AI helpers.

What Is Vibe Coding?

Section titled “What Is Vibe Coding?”

In one line: Define goals in human language → let AI draft first → humans calibrate scope and acceptance via dialogue. It’s not mystical “going by feel,” but rather turning intent, constraints, and acceptance into an executable collaboration language. Typical vehicles include natural‑language code edits in AI editors and dialog‑driven tasks/batch changes in the terminal/IDE.

Seven Differences from the Comparison Chart (Practical Read)

Section titled “Seven Differences from the Comparison Chart (Practical Read)”
  • Input: Vibe uses natural language + examples; traditional relies on design docs + handwritten implementation.
  • Role: Vibe is like a director, breaking down shots and pacing; traditional is like an architect, setting structure and detailing.
  • Focus: Vibe stresses runnable results; traditional stresses a traceable process.
  • Skills: Vibe emphasizes questioning and acceptance writing; traditional emphasizes algorithms and language details.
  • Cadence: Vibe iterates fast; traditional moves steadily.
  • Barrier: Vibe is lower, good for cross‑functional alignment; traditional is higher, good for high‑reliability domains.
  • Cognition: Vibe tends toward black‑box; traditional is auditable.
  • Key takeaway: Vibe is a communication‑paradigm upgrade, not a replacement.

Six Everyday Dev Scenarios: Vibe Flow vs Traditional Flow (Copy‑Ready)

Section titled “Six Everyday Dev Scenarios: Vibe Flow vs Traditional Flow (Copy‑Ready)”

1) Feature 0→1: Add “WeChat QR Login” to Next.js

Section titled “1) Feature 0→1: Add “WeChat QR Login” to Next.js”
  • Traditional: write requirements → choose library → draw sequence → branch → implement → PR.
  • Vibe (dialogue in Cursor/Claude Code):
    1. “Project is Next.js + Supabase, need a minimal WeChat QR login. Constraints: only touch the auth module, don’t change the existing user table.”
    2. Have AI generate routes, callbacks, and env var checklist, plus security and rollback notes.
    3. Ask for a PR description + E2E acceptance steps. Why it works: natural‑language cross‑file edits + auto PR/test checklists.

2) Fast Regression for a Production Bug

Section titled “2) Fast Regression for a Production Bug”

  • Traditional: dig logs → reproduce locally → patch → write regression tests.

  • Vibe:

    • Paste exception logs + relevant functions: “Provide the smallest diff fix, and generate the regression unit test and repro scenario.”
    • Humans focus on blast‑radius review and boundary checks. Essentials: small PR + must‑test + rollbackable.

3) Repo‑Wide Migration: axios → fetch

Section titled “3) Repo‑Wide Migration: axios → fetch”

  • Traditional: global search → manual replace → fix file by file.

  • Vibe:

    • “Scan axios usage, design a unified wrapper and a batched change plan (prioritize low‑risk directories), ship each batch with a rollback script.”
    • Let AI batch‑apply changes; humans ensure error‑handling strategy in the wrapper layer is solid. Tooling: natural‑language cross‑file rewrites.

4) Performance Tuning: Slow DB Queries

Section titled “4) Performance Tuning: Slow DB Queries”

  • Traditional: explain → index → adjust SQL/cache.

  • Vibe:

    • “Here are the slow‑query logs + schema. Propose a three‑step optimization order: validate index gains first, then consider caching; output target P95/QPS and a baseline script.”
    • AI produces SQL and metrics scripts; humans run benchmarks and do canary rollout.

5) Pre‑Review for Code Reviews

Section titled “5) Pre‑Review for Code Reviews”

  • Traditional: reviewer scans logic manually.

  • Vibe:

    • Have AI produce a pre‑review summary (high‑risk changes, uncovered paths, potential injection spots),
    • Humans gatekeep business semantics and compliance. Benefit: reviewers spend time on critical semantic judgment.

6) Personal Projects/Newcomer Onboarding

Section titled “6) Personal Projects/Newcomer Onboarding”

  • Traditional: read docs, poke around the repo.

  • Vibe:

    • Describe the tool/page you need; an agent scaffolds and runs it — great for spin‑up in an afternoon.

Risks and “Guardrails”

Section titled “Risks and “Guardrails””

  • Black‑box feel: many can use the tools yet don’t fully understand the generated code; industry surveys flag this as a risk.

  • Guardrail checklist (strongly bake into templates/commands):

    • PR template: background, acceptance criteria, impact surface, rollback.
    • CI: must‑run tests, coverage thresholds, dependency and license scanning, secrets scanning.
    • Conversation templates: goals/MVP/disallowed changes/log level/time‑budget and rate limits.
    • Slash Commands: codify common flows so the team can reuse with one command.

When to Use Vibe — and When Not Yet

Section titled “When to Use Vibe — and When Not Yet”
  • Great fit: prototyping, CRUD features, bulk renames/refactors, scaffolding, repetitive fixes.
  • Don’t fully “outsource” yet: high‑security/strict‑compliance systems, core algorithms, modules requiring strong performance explainability.
  • Team readiness: shared PR/test templates, clear rollback conventions, and at least one one‑command project bootstrap + acceptance script.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

The essence of Vibe Coding is a communication upgrade: turning human intent into machine‑executable steps. It doesn’t think for you — it makes the boundaries of your thinking clearer. As a next step, take your team’s three most common tasks and turn them into reusable conversation templates/Slash commands — you can speed up starting today.

Tuning Claude Code Output Style @agent-output-style-setup

Aug 14, 2025
