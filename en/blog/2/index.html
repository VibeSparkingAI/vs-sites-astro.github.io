Here are 7 proven methods to trick your brain into doing difficult things.

1. Shrink the Task to Just Two Minutes

Section titled “1. Shrink the Task to Just Two Minutes”

The Problem: Your brain resists big commitments. When you think “work out for an hour,” your amygdala sounds the alarm.

The Solution: Don’t make a big commitment. Make a tiny one.

  • “Work out” → Do one exercise
  • “Clean the house” → Pick up three items
  • “Write a report” → Open the document and write one sentence

The two-minute rule works because it bypasses your brain’s threat detection system. Once you start, momentum takes over.

2. Prepare Before You Start

Section titled “2. Prepare Before You Start”

The Insight: Don’t begin the task—just begin the setup.

Examples:

  • Put on gym clothes
  • Open your laptop
  • Clear your desk

Why It Works: Physical movement bypasses mental resistance. By the time you’ve completed the setup, momentum kicks in before you’ve even “started” the actual task.

This technique leverages what psychologists call “implementation intentions”—specific if-then plans that bridge the gap between intention and action.

3. Sandwich Rewards Around Hard Tasks

Section titled “3. Sandwich Rewards Around Hard Tasks”

The Pattern:

The Key: Make the reward conditional on completing the task. Your brain will chase it.

This technique is grounded in operant conditioning—your brain learns to associate the effort with the reward that follows. Over time, the effort itself becomes rewarding.

4. Make the Task Enjoyable While You Do It

Section titled “4. Make the Task Enjoyable While You Do It”

Examples:

  • Listen to your favorite podcast during cardio
  • Drink your best coffee during deep work
  • Light a candle while writing

The Result: Your brain learns: effort = enjoyment.

This is known as “temptation bundling”—pairing something you need to do with something you want to do. The neural pathways for pleasure and effort become intertwined, reducing resistance over time.

5. Frame It as an Experiment, Not a Performance

Section titled “5. Frame It as an Experiment, Not a Performance”

The Shift:

  • ❌ “I have to nail this presentation”
  • ✅ “I’m testing what gets more engagement”

Why It Matters: Experiments create curiosity. Performances create pressure.

When you frame tasks as experiments, failure becomes data, not defeat. This activates your brain’s curiosity-driven learning systems rather than its threat-avoidance circuits.

6. Act Like the Person You Want to Become

Section titled “6. Act Like the Person You Want to Become”

The Transformation:

  • “I need to get in shape” → Inertia
  • “I’m an active person” → Motion

The Science: Identity drives behavior more than willpower ever will.

James Clear, author of Atomic Habits, calls this “identity-based habits.” When you change who you believe you are, your behaviors naturally align with that identity.

7. Call Yourself a Beginner

Section titled “7. Call Yourself a Beginner”

The Reframe:

  • ❌ “I’m bad at languages”
  • ✅ “I’m learning Spanish”

The Power: Beginners can’t fail. They can only improve.

The beginner’s mindset activates growth-oriented neural pathways. When you’re “learning,” mistakes are expected. When you’re “bad,” mistakes confirm your negative self-view.

  2. Uncertainty about outcome
  3. Potential for failure

The seven techniques above work because they:
